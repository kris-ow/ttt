This is episode 134 of the Tesla Beat, a weekly live discussion hosted by the Futureaza channel covering the latest Tesla and related technology news. The panel — regular hosts Brian and Hans, along with guest Matthew DR and co-host Joe — covers Cybercab production milestones, a new budget Cybertruck variant, wireless charging regulatory approval, FSD performance data, and the Boring Company's Nashville tunnel approval. The overarching theme is that Tesla's autonomous vehicle ecosystem is converging rapidly, with multiple pieces — hardware, software, charging infrastructure, and regulatory approvals — falling into place simultaneously.

1. Cybercab production has officially begun at Giga Texas. The first production Cybercab rolled off the line, with footage circulating of it going around the facility. The panel views this as a significant signal: starting a production line is not something done two years before launch — it indicates Tesla has sufficient confidence in the underlying autonomy software for imminent deployment. Production is on track to begin in earnest in April 2026.

2. The launch implies a potential for the fastest vehicle scale-up in Tesla's history. Once production is optimized and the robotaxi platform can operate without remote monitors, the panel argues there is essentially unlimited demand for Tesla to deploy these vehicles itself rather than sell them to consumers. One panelist speculates Tesla may only sell a handful publicly, perhaps geofenced to Austin, since operating a single Cybercab in a fleet could recover its cost in roughly six months.

3. A new, more affordable all-wheel drive Cybertruck variant was announced, starting at $59,990. This is $20,000 less than the premium version. Key omissions include air suspension, the vault bed cover, cloth seats instead of vegan leather, a seven-speaker rather than nine-speaker audio system, and no premium center console. Everything else — four-wheel steering, steer-by-wire, power share, AWD, the large lockable frunk storage — is retained. Elon Musk subsequently tweeted that the pricing is only available for 10 days, which the panel interprets as either a demand-sensing test or a signal that the price will increase by $3,000–$5,000 afterward.

4. The value proposition of this Cybertruck was extensively benchmarked against competitors. A comparable XLT F-150 Super Crew at a local Ford dealership was quoted at $67,000 MSRP before dealer fees. The F-150 Raptor, which was $47,000 in 2019, now costs $79,000. The original 2019 Cybertruck AWD announcement price of $49,900 is equivalent to roughly $63,000 in 2026 dollars. The panel argues Cybertruck pricing has risen less than comparable ICE trucks and that ongoing fuel savings make the total cost of ownership significantly lower, especially for contractors who would benefit from power share.

5. The FCC granted Tesla a waiver to use ultra-wideband radio technology for its wireless EV charging system, specifically designed for the Cybercab. Normally this technology must be handheld or installed in fixed outdoor equipment under specific conditions; Tesla's charging pad required an exception. The panel notes this is critical infrastructure for running a fully autonomous fleet — eliminating the need for human intervention to plug in vehicles. Matthew DR added important technical context: Tesla has been pursuing a wireless charging frequency different from the SAE international standard, which he believes is the real reason wireless charging was quietly dropped from the Cybertruck (not vehicle height as Tesla stated). This FCC approval covers the UWB alignment component; the charging frequency standard issue remains unresolved.

6. Tesla announced 8 billion cumulative FSD miles driven. The panel notes that Elon had previously cited 10 billion as a milestone number, meaning Tesla is within months of hitting it given current daily mileage accumulation rates. Brian argued the significance is less about any specific threshold and more about the continuous improvement in training data output.

7. Tesla's FSD version 14.2.2.5 was highlighted for navigating a large highway flood puddle spanning nearly two lanes. The panel contrasted this with Waymo, which stops functioning in heavy rain or snow due to LiDAR scatter — a point Elon Musk himself made publicly. The panel noted Waymo's accident rate has remained flat quarter-over-quarter rather than improving, and criticized the broader legacy autonomous vehicle approach of retrofitting off-the-shelf vehicles with expensive sensor arrays rather than designing purpose-built hardware.

8. Tesla Giga New York (the original SolarCity Buffalo factory) was confirmed job-compliant with its $1/year lease agreement with New York State. The facility received $350 million in state funding and a nominal lease in exchange for job creation targets. After Tesla wound down its auto-labeling team there, compliance was in question. The factory now manufactures Superchargers (and potentially Megachargers) and is ramping solar panel production again, with Powerwalls manufactured in Nevada. A service center in Long Island was also counted toward the statewide job threshold.

9. The Boring Company's Nashville tunnel was approved unanimously by city council after a public hearing where officials asked basic questions about EV fire safety and wheel integrity. The panel noted that Boring's proof rock boring machine can drill through any terrain — in Las Vegas they simultaneously pumped groundwater while drilling. The tunnel lining system is a 12–14 inch six-piece overlapping ring designed for global terrain variability. The project is entirely privately funded at no cost to taxpayers. John noted that of the three tunneling companies operating in the US, one was acquired by a Chinese firm and another went bankrupt, leaving the Boring Company as the primary domestic option.
