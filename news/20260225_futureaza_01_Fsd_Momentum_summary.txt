This video covers the rapidly expanding global momentum of Full Self-Driving (FSD) technology across multiple automakers, with hosts Brian (Futurazza) and Mark (Tesla Life) discussing regulatory developments, safety data, and contrasting approaches from Tesla, Xiaopeng, Volkswagen, BMW, and Mercedes. The central thesis is that autonomous driving is accelerating worldwide, with vision-based systems proving superior to narrowly scoped alternatives, and that robotaxi deployment is increasingly imminent. The conversation blends news analysis with casual commentary on what these developments mean for the broader autonomous vehicle industry.

Key Points:

1. NHTSA public meeting on automated vehicle safety, March 10, 2026. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration held an all-day public meeting (9 a.m. to 4:30 p.m.) at US DOT headquarters to provide updates on ongoing and upcoming vehicle automation activities. This builds on an ADS workshop held November 20th, and the hosts view it as a potential regulatory turning point. There is a current 2,500-vehicle exemption cap for autonomous vehicles, though additional exemptions are possible — a process that was streamlined last June — and the meeting could yield clearer guidance on future rules.

2. Elon Musk confirms Cybercab will be sold to consumers in 2026. Musk confirmed in response to skepticism from tech reviewer MKBHD (Marques Brownlee) that the Cybercab will be delivered at under $30,000 before 2027. The hosts debate what "sold" means — Brian argues that even a single sale to a fleet manager would technically count — and separately, journalist Joe Tegmire reportedly observed hundreds of Cybercabs already parked inside the assembly facility, suggesting production is well underway.

3. Tesla's updated safety report shows a widening gap between AI and human drivers. Tesla's global FSD fleet has surpassed 8.2 billion miles driven. The crash data is segmented into four categories: FSD supervised (1 crash per 5 million miles), Tesla driven manually with active safety (1 crash per 2 million miles), Tesla driven manually without active safety (1 crash per 855,000 miles), and the US national average (1 crash per 600,000 miles). The definition of "crash" is strict — requiring airbag deployment, seatbelt pyrotechnic activation, or high-voltage battery pack fuse triggering — making the comparison meaningful and conservative.

4. Xiaopeng secures Volkswagen as first commercial customer for XIMO 2.0 smart driving. Xiaopeng's second-generation vision-language-action smart driving system (VA 2.0) has been licensed to Volkswagen, marking the first large-scale export of this core Chinese autonomous driving technology. VW had been working with Xiaopeng since 2023 and also has a separate software partnership with Rivian (for North America and parts of Europe). Mark had already seen VA 2.0 in beta and was scheduled to evaluate the consumer release version the following week. Importantly, like Tesla's FSD, Xiaopeng dropped LiDAR and runs on vision-only — a validation of that architectural direction.

5. BMW removing Level 3 autonomy from the refreshed 7 Series. BMW is eliminating its Level 3 autonomous driving feature from its flagship sedan, following a similar retreat by Mercedes, which temporarily scrapped its eyes-off Level 3 system because almost no one was buying it. The hosts explain that both automakers attempted to handcode narrow edge-case scenarios — such as Mercedes requiring a car ahead, speeds limited to 35-40 mph on highways posted at 60-80 mph, daytime only, and no sun glare — and while those cases may have worked reliably, the on-board hardware cannot scale to broader use cases like lane changes or urban driving. The fundamental issue is that off-the-shelf hardware plugged into vehicles cannot handle the "march of nines" (ever-increasing reliability thresholds) that true autonomy demands.

6. FSD subscription terms allow Tesla to change pricing and features at any time. A news item generated concern that Tesla's FSD subscription terms include standard language permitting price and feature changes. The hosts dismiss this as entirely normal for any subscription product, comparing it to Netflix's terms of service, and note it only becomes a concern if the agreement explicitly promises a fixed price for life.

Conclusions and Recommendations:
The hosts see vision-only FSD systems converging as the dominant approach globally, with Tesla leading on data volume and safety performance. BMW and Mercedes are cautionary tales about the dangers of narrow, handcoded autonomy solutions that don't scale. The NHTSA meeting and Tesla's safety report are framed as key inputs into an emerging regulatory environment that could soon formalize rules for broader autonomous deployment. Insurance companies like Lemonade are already beginning to price actuarial risk based on FSD safety data ahead of the broader industry.
