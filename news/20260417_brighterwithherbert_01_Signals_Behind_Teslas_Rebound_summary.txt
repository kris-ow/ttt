This panel discussion on "brighterwithherbert" brings together three Tesla bull investors — Alexandra Merz, Jeff Lutz, and Larry Goldberg — to analyze Tesla's recent stock rebound and the convergence of near-term catalysts including Cybercab production, FSD expansion into Europe, robotaxi hiring ramps, and the AI5 chip tape-out. The core thesis is that despite delays and a challenging macro environment, Tesla is approaching a genuine inflection point across its autonomous driving, robotaxi, and Optimus businesses. The hosts also engage in an extended debate about a potential Tesla-SpaceX merger of equals following a SpaceX IPO.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD Version 14.3.1 Rolling Out Broadly:** FSD version 14.3.1 was released over the weekend prior to the video and is already being received by users beyond early adopters, including users in Canada. Jeff Lutz noted personal experience with the update on his Cybertruck, describing it as "much improved" — more confident driving behavior, a less intrusive cabin camera nag, improved responsiveness when the driver looks at the camera, and better parking performance. The panel sees this version as potentially sufficient for robotaxi deployment, though they note it is not the "10x parameter" model some had anticipated.

2. **FSD Approved in the Netherlands:** Tesla received regulatory approval for Full Self-Driving in the Netherlands, marking what Alexandra Merz described as a significant European milestone. She believes this announcement — made late on a Friday — triggered a 3-day committee review cycle among institutional fund managers, contributing to the subsequent Tuesday/Wednesday stock movement. Several panelists had previously doubted FSD would ever receive European regulatory approval, making this a meaningful catalyst validation.

3. **NHTSA Safety Data Shows Zero Incidents:** The panel discussed the latest NHTSA incident reporting window from February 17th to March 15th, which showed zero incidents for Tesla FSD. The single incident referenced in a prior period (January 15th to February 15th) was described as a minor bumper contact. The panel views the clean safety record as supportive of broader deployment.

4. **AI5 Chip Taped Out — Production Timeline ~1 Year Out:** The AI5 chip has reached tape-out, which the panel estimates means first silicon parts could arrive in roughly three to four months, followed by testing cycles. Jeff Lutz estimated volume production would realistically begin in the second half of 2026 at the earliest. Elon Musk was quoted as saying AI4 is sufficient to achieve better-than-human safety for FSD, while AI5 is being prioritized for Optimus and supercomputer clusters — though the panel agreed AI5 will eventually make it into vehicles as well, adding significant capability beyond FSD to areas like real-time mapping updates and in-car intelligence. Lutz highlighted Tesla's silicon vertical integration — designing its own ASICs alongside layer-1 software — as a deeply underappreciated competitive moat that no other automaker or robotics company replicates at scale.

## Robotaxi

1. **Unsupervised Robotaxi Count Reaches 13:** As of the video, Tesla has 13 unsupervised robotaxis operating, up from 12 the prior day. The panel noted the count is growing incrementally and expects continued expansion as FSD 14.3.1 rolls out more broadly. Jeff Lutz expressed frustration that near-term guidance from the January earnings call — implying approximately 2,000 robotaxis on the road by this point — has not been met, and emphasized that short-term predictions need to be held to a higher standard of accuracy than long-term ones.

2. **Robotaxi Hiring Spans 34 Cities:** Tesla is actively hiring for robotaxi-related roles across 34 U.S. cities, covering an estimated 46% of the U.S. population. Tessyota (referenced as an X/Twitter analyst) organized the open roles into three tiers: pre-deployment cities (self-driving data collection supervisors, test specialists, test engineers), deployment cities (AI safety operators, robotaxi field response specialists, fleet support specialists, fleet support supervisors, operations managers), and core engineering/business functions (RL engineers, AI engineers, data engineers, robotaxi business analysts, business managers, technical program managers). Dallas and Houston specifically are hiring for 24/7 field response specialist shifts. Twenty-eight of the 34 cities align with previously identified future deployment markets.

3. **Cybercab Production Likely Underway:** Tesla's official account posted a teaser video for the Cybercab with the phrase "golden era," which the panel interprets as a strong signal that Cybercab production has either already started or is imminent. Alexandra Merz confirmed that Cybercab production did begin around April 1st. Jeff Lutz noted Tesla is not obligated to formally announce a production start but that a factory line photo would be the typical signal. The panel emphasized the Cybercab's expected sub-$30,000 price point as a historically unprecedented value proposition in the autonomous vehicle space. Larry Goldberg expressed disappointment that inductive charging does not appear to be available at launch, with cable charging expected initially.

4. **Peak Demand Fleet Economics — Retail Cars as Critical Input:** Larry Goldberg argued at length that Tesla's robotaxi business case fundamentally depends on the ability to draw retail-owned vehicles into the fleet during peak demand hours, which he estimates could require 2.5 to 3 million vehicles when the core fleet reaches 1 to 1.5 million. He contrasted this with Waymo, which is constrained to its owned fleet and cannot flex capacity to meet peak demand — the period when fare revenue per mile is two to three times higher than off-peak. Goldberg stated this analysis has been documented in detail by his research group (AR) and represents the key competitive differentiator for Tesla's economics.

5. **Earnings Call Context — Robotaxi Guidance a Key Topic:** The upcoming Wednesday earnings call (Q1 2026) is expected to focus heavily on robotaxi expansion tone and forward guidance. Jeff Lutz submitted a question to Say Technologies asking what Tesla is seeing in internal data that caused them to modify their robotaxi rollout plans from the January guidance, and what the gating factors are for transitioning from manufacturing revenue to autonomy revenue. The panel broadly expects the free cash flow number to be tight — possibly slightly negative or near zero — and does not anticipate a major positive stock reaction to the earnings report itself.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus V3 Reveal and Production Timeline Unconfirmed:** The top question submitted to Say Technologies for the upcoming earnings call asks when the Optimus Gen 3 reveal will occur and when Optimus production will start, given that Model X production ended mid-year to free up factory capacity. No specific dates were provided by the panel, though Jeff Lutz noted AI5 is being prioritized for Optimus before vehicles, suggesting the chip's production ramp in the second half of 2026 could be tied to Optimus scaling. The panel acknowledged that Terafab (Tesla's manufacturing innovation initiative) will carry the engineering vertical integration forward into humanoid robot manufacturing.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Q1 Deliveries Near Consensus, Energy Light:** The panel noted Q1 2026 delivery numbers came in roughly in line with consensus for automotive, with some positive regional bright spots. Energy deliveries were significantly below expectations — down approximately 6 gigawatt-hours versus consensus — though Alexandra Merz relayed a theory from a community member that Megapacks may have been redirected to build out Cortex (Tesla's AI training cluster) rather than sold externally. The panel views energy as a "lumpy" business where one weak quarter is not necessarily alarming.

2. **Consumer Price Perception Gap:** Larry Goldberg raised a recurring concern that potential Tesla buyers continue to believe the cars cost $100,000, illustrating a persistent advertising and awareness gap. Alexandra Merz disagreed that advertising is necessary in the near term, arguing Tesla should maximize Model Y and Cybercab inventory for when unsupervised FSD is ready to deploy at scale rather than selling cars to retail customers who may not know the price. The debate was left unresolved, though all panelists acknowledged Elon Musk's longstanding philosophical opposition to paid advertising.

## Financials

1. **Free Cash Flow Expected to Be Tight or Negative in Q1:** Alexandra Merz identified free cash flow as the single most important number to watch in the upcoming earnings call, warning investors not to panic if the number is slightly negative or near zero. She cited higher Megapack inventory (potentially redirected to Cortex) and general automotive inventory build as contributing factors. Jeff Lutz noted automotive inventory doubled quarter-over-quarter, which will weigh on free cash flow. The panel characterized this as a temporary and explainable dynamic rather than a structural concern.

2. **Outstanding Share Count Update Pending:** Alexandra Merz highlighted uncertainty around Tesla's current outstanding share count. Following the shareholder vote approving Elon Musk's 2025 compensation package, approximately 400 million restricted stock units were issued, bringing the total from approximately 3.3 billion to approximately 3.75 billion shares outstanding. She noted this dilutes major index fund holders (e.g., Vanguard's stake moved from roughly 10% to roughly 8%), and said the upcoming 10-Q filing should provide an updated figure.

## Market & Competition

1. **SpaceX IPO and Potential Tesla-SpaceX Merger:** A significant portion of the discussion focused on a widely anticipated SpaceX IPO, expected around June 2026, with a target valuation the panel debated between $1.5 trillion and $2 trillion. Larry Goldberg indicated Elon Musk has publicly rejected the $2 trillion figure, with $1.5–$1.75 trillion seen as more realistic at IPO pricing, potentially trading up to $1.8 trillion on the first day. Both Goldberg and Merz expect a Tesla-SpaceX merger of equals to eventually follow the IPO, though they disagree on timing — Goldberg suggested an announcement no earlier than August/September 2026, with a shareholder vote potentially in November 2026. Merz argued the merger would bring Tesla and SpaceX share prices into lock-step immediately upon announcement, benefiting from index inclusion dynamics across NASDAQ 100, MSCI, and ultimately S&P 500. The panel noted that SpaceX's team has been systematically working with multiple indices to coordinate inclusion timing, and that a 15-trading-day NASDAQ 100 inclusion window was negotiated — an unusually fast timeline they viewed as deliberate financial engineering.

2. **Waymo Compared Unfavorably on Fleet Economics:** The panel cited Waymo as a cautionary example of a constrained robotaxi model — one where the fleet is entirely company-owned and cannot flex to meet peak demand. Larry Goldberg argued this structural limitation prevents Waymo from capturing the two-to-three-times revenue-per-mile premium available during peak hours, and that Tesla's hybrid owned-plus-retail model is fundamentally superior for unit economics.

3. **Macro Environment — Cautious Optimism:** The panel discussed the broader macroeconomic backdrop, including tariff uncertainty and Middle East geopolitical risk (specifically Iran negotiations). Larry Goldberg predicted a brief, mild economic slowdown with a temporary uptick in inflation, followed by a return to strong growth by year-end 2026. He expressed cautious optimism that Iran nuclear negotiations could resolve relatively quickly, though flagged the risk of a brief resumption of conflict before a settlement. Jeff Lutz noted that markets appear more resilient than the macro headlines suggest, and that Tesla's stock felt technically oversold heading into the current rebound.
