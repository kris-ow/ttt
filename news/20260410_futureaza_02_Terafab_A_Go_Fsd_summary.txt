This episode of the Tesla Beat Podcast (episode 141) covers a wide range of Tesla-related topics including the latest FSD v14.3 update, Cybercab production observations, the Tesla/Intel chip foundry partnership, Tesla Semi competitive positioning, and global EV market dynamics. The hosts — joined by Brian, Matthew, and Joe — frame Tesla's near-term moves around autonomous driving and robotaxi deployment as the central thesis, while providing color on competitive pressures facing legacy automakers and the broader EV trucking market.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD v14.3 rollout impressions:** The hosts report that FSD v14.3 has rolled out and features a completely rebuilt AI compiler, resulting in approximately 20% faster reaction times. The host notes personal testing in San Jose, including narrow roads and school drop-offs, and observes that the system feels more assertive and confident compared to prior v14 releases, particularly in parking decisions — the system now targets the first available spot rather than circling repeatedly.

2. **Remaining v14.3 issues — navigation regressions:** Despite improvements, navigation remains a known regression across v14 sub-releases. Hosts report the system routing to incorrect destinations (e.g., loading docks, wrong addresses) and not consistently showing parking pin indicators across all location types. One host notes workarounds like entering a neighbor's address to prevent the system from targeting an incorrect driveway approach.

3. **FSD architecture context — small vs. large model:** Elon Musk is quoted as saying the small model has advanced so fast that the large model has not yet caught up. V15 is identified as the large model update, suggesting the current v14.x line represents small model iterations and that more substantial capability jumps are expected with V15.

4. **Training data milestone approaching 10 billion miles:** Tesla is reportedly close to 9.2 billion miles of FSD training data, with Sawyer Merritt cited as the source. Elon Musk has said roughly 10 billion miles is needed to achieve unsupervised self-driving. The host notes the accumulation rate is approximately 1,000 miles every 4 seconds. The hosts caution that Elon used the word "roughly," meaning hitting 10 billion may not be a hard trigger for unsupervised deployment.

5. **Netherlands FSD regulatory approval imminent:** FSD regulatory approval in the Netherlands is described as potentially imminent, with posts suggesting it could happen very soon. The hosts note that once approved in the Netherlands, other EU countries could accept that approval without requiring individual country-level testing and approval processes, potentially unlocking FSD across Europe quickly.

6. **Unsupervised robotaxi operation in Austin already functionally ready:** The hosts assert that Tesla already has the software capable of unsupervised robotaxi operation running in Austin, noting that supervisors in Austin are no longer actively at the controls. The remaining blockers are described as regulatory and internal readiness thresholds, not technical capability.

## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab production ramp visible at Fremont:** The host reports personally observing approximately 60 Cybercabs in two groups in the Fremont outbound lot. Crash testing is underway and vehicles are being shipped across the United States for environmental testing in different conditions. Trailers at the Fremont factory are being used to bring Cybercabs in and out after testing runs on the factory track.

2. **Cybercab seen as imminent, not vaporware:** Brian pushes back on skeptics, arguing that manufacturers do not produce hundreds of units for something speculative. The pattern of nationwide shipping for testing, combined with Austin supervisors no longer actively controlling vehicles, signals that commercial deployment is on the near-term roadmap, likely starting in Austin.

3. **S and X factory winding down for robotaxi/Optimus transition:** The hosts note that custom Model S and X orders have been closed, prices on remaining inventory have been raised approximately $15,000, and the Fremont line is winding down SNX production. This is framed as Tesla clearing the runway for the Cybercab and Optimus production ramp using the unboxed manufacturing method.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **SNX factory conversion underway:** The hosts connect the end of Model S/X production to Tesla's stated plans to repurpose that factory space for Optimus production. The unboxed manufacturing method and the conversion of the SNX line are described as clear signals that Tesla is serious about Optimus at scale. No specific Optimus production numbers or timeline dates are stated.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Model Y dominates China March sales:** Joe presents March China sales data showing the Model Y at approximately 39,000 units, with the next closest competitor at roughly 31,000 units — a gap of approximately 25%. Brian contextualizes this as remarkable given Tesla's limited model lineup versus Chinese competitors offering 10–30 vehicles.

2. **Tesla outsold BYD in Q1 for total EV volume:** The hosts note that Tesla outsold BYD across all models in Q1 2026, which is described as a significant feat given BYD's broader market presence, more factory capacity, and lower price points. Brian cautions this lead is unlikely to hold long-term.

3. **New Model Y (YL) expanding globally:** The refreshed long-wheelbase Model Y is expanding to multiple new markets. Israel is cited specifically, with first deliveries starting in June. The model has also expanded to nine or ten additional markets across Asia. Brian notes that Tesla marketing the YL under the Model Y name mirrors Toyota's Corolla strategy — using a single popular nameplate to aggregate sales across distinct variants.

4. **Giga Texas Model Y line reportedly down for two weeks:** Matthew mentions reports that the Model Y line at Giga Texas may be down for approximately two weeks, speculating this could be related to retooling for the long-wheelbase Model Y for the North American market.

5. **Model S and X price increases on remaining inventory:** Tesla raised prices on remaining Model S and X inventory by approximately $15,000, attributed to addition of a "Lux package." The hosts frame this as rational end-of-life inventory pricing, not a demand signal, comparing it to dealership markups on rare models.

## Market & Competition

1. **Tesla Semi competitively positioned as the only sub-$300K Class 8 electric truck:** Brian reports that Tesla Semi is priced between approximately $240,000–$260,000 based on California rebate application public records — roughly half the price of competing electric Class 8 trucks priced at $400,000–$500,000. He identifies $300,000 as the economic break-even threshold for fleet operators. With California rebates of $120,000–$150,000, the Semi becomes cheaper than diesel from day one.

2. **Electric trucking industry broadly struggling outside Tesla:** Brian describes the Daimler Freightliner eCascadia going from roughly 2% of line production to a "rounding error," while Matthew's contact at a competing truck manufacturer reports BEV order share among competitors dropping from ~10% to 0.5–1%, with his own company holding at 5–10%. The rollback of U.S. ZEV credits and emissions mandates is cited as the primary driver of the collapse in demand.

3. **Tesla Semi efficiency advantage:** Brian cites real-world trucker data showing Tesla Semi achieving approximately 2.1 kWh per mile, versus the industry standard of 1.7–1.8 kWh per mile for typical Class 8 electric trucks. A Canadian study comparing eCascadia to Cascadia found $167,000 CAD (~$140,000 USD) in savings over six years, but at $400,000–$500,000 purchase price, the payback period is too long for most operators.

4. **Tesla/Intel chip foundry partnership:** The hosts discuss a reported partnership between Tesla, SpaceX, and Intel to build a chip foundry in the U.S. Brian frames it as Tesla and SpaceX providing funding while Intel provides manufacturing expertise, potentially reducing what would have been a $100 billion, 10-year effort to approximately a $30 billion, five-year timeline. Intel is described as one of only three companies globally capable of producing chips to Tesla's required precision, alongside TSMC and Samsung.

5. **Toyota CEO warns of existential threat from Chinese EV competition:** The Toyota CEO is quoted as saying "Unless things change, we will not survive. I want everyone to acknowledge this sense of crisis." The hosts frame this as a recognition Japanese OEMs are arriving at far too late.

6. **BMW acknowledges software-defined vehicles:** BMW publicly stated that "cars are now defined by software, not engines." The hosts react skeptically, noting that acknowledging this reality means little without restructuring the fragmented multi-supplier software architecture that legacy OEMs rely on.

7. **Japan incentivizing locally sourced EV components:** Japan has introduced incentives favoring domestically produced and locally sourced vehicles, effectively disadvantaging BYD imports. Notably, Teslas (made in China) were still reportedly qualifying for top-tier incentives at the time of discussion.
