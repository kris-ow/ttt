This video from "brighterwithherbert" features host Herbert and guest Jeff Lutz, a multi-decade supply chain executive and manufacturing consultant, discussing two major stories: a mystery vehicle spotted at Giga Texas that may be a Model Y L or an entirely new vehicle, and what they argue is an underappreciated signal of imminent mass-scale robotaxi deployment across multiple U.S. cities simultaneously. Their core thesis is that Tesla is not doing a slow, incremental robotaxi rollout but is instead staging a broad, near-simultaneous launch across at least four cities, likely timed to coincide with a major FSD software update (referred to as 14.3).

## Robotaxi

1. **Multi-city staging signals mass deployment**: Fleets of Model Y vehicles equipped with rear camera washers and California manufacturing license plates have now been spotted in four cities — Orlando (Florida), Las Vegas (Nevada), Dallas (Texas), and Phoenix (Arizona) — all of which are among the seven planned metro areas for robotaxi launch before the end of June. Jeff Lutz argues this simultaneous staging is a deliberate strategy: rather than a slow city-by-city drip, Tesla appears to be preparing all cities at once and waiting for a triggering milestone (likely FSD 14.3 software readiness) before launching broadly.

2. **Austin and Bay Area as the foundational "nest"**: Lutz explains the logic of the rollout structure: Austin serves as the hardware home for the Cybercab and robotaxi Model Y fleet, while the Bay Area is the software home for FSD and autonomous driving development. These two areas were always meant to be the initial proving grounds. Once edge cases are sufficiently resolved and safety metrics reach an internal threshold, the program is ready to "leave the nest" — and the four dry-weather, warm-climate cities (Phoenix, Dallas, Orlando, Vegas) are the first destinations for that expansion.

3. **Robotaxi fleet now at ~519–522 vehicles, largest weekly growth recorded**: According to the Robotaxi Tracker app, the active Tesla robotaxi fleet has grown to approximately 519–522 vehicles, representing roughly an 8–9% increase in a single week — described as the largest fleet growth in a week for the entire program. Jeff Lutz notes the app likely undercounts due to its manual reporting nature, particularly at inflection points, and that Waymo's fleet is also undercounted by the same tracker. Only 8 of those vehicles are listed as unsupervised, located in Austin.

4. **Supervised-to-unsupervised gradient, not a binary switch**: Lutz emphasizes that the removal of safety monitors will happen on a gradient, not all at once. He references Tesla VP Ashok (likely Ashok Elluswamy) stating that a certain percentage of the fleet will be unsupervised and that percentage will grow over time. New cities will launch with supervisors in the vehicles, with unsupervised operation expanding as the software matures. Herbert and Lutz argue critics demanding full driverless operation immediately misunderstand how such a rollout works in practice.

5. **FSD 14.3 as the likely gating factor**: Both hosts argue the most probable reason for the delay in broader expansion is Tesla waiting to validate a newer FSD software version (referred to as "14.3") before deploying at scale. The theory is that Tesla has internal performance milestones at set intervals before a release (e.g., t-minus 3 weeks), and can compare safety metrics across software versions. Once 14.3 hits those internal thresholds, that is expected to trigger the operational green light to send fleets to all staged cities simultaneously.

6. **Tesla's Cybercab monthly production volume projected to exceed Waymo's entire fleet**: Lutz makes the projection that at some point in 2026, Tesla's monthly production volume of Cybercabs will equal Waymo's entire fleet. He also states that Tesla's cost per mile will eventually be a fraction — not 80% or 90%, but a true fraction — of Waymo's cost per mile. He argues the Tesla-vs-Waymo comparison is the "most disjointed comparison in the history of anything Tesla has made" because the two companies are at completely different points on the axes of time, volume, and cost structure.

7. **Tesla chief designer spotted in a Cybercab in Los Angeles**: Franz von Holzhausen, Tesla's chief designer, was spotted being driven (or driving) a Cybercab in Los Angeles. Lutz interprets executive use of vehicles near launch as a signal of production-grade readiness — executives typically do not use early prototypes and only adopt vehicles once they reach near-production quality. He suggests this kind of executive "dogfooding" is a standard signal of readiness before capital commitment to full production.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD supervised approval in the Netherlands now expected April 10th**: Tesla announced it has completed the final vehicle testing phase for FSD Supervised with the Dutch vehicle authority (RDW) under UN R171 approval plus Article 39 exemptions, and has submitted all required documentation. The new expected approval date is April 10th, shifted from the previously communicated date of March 20th. Tesla stated that following Netherlands approval, other European countries will be able to recognize the approval nationally, and Tesla is anticipating a possible EU-wide approval during the summer of 2026.

2. **Extensive EU testing data submitted**: Over the past 18 months, Tesla's EU FSD approval process has involved 1.66 billion kilometers of FSD Supervised testing on EU roads, 13,000+ customer sales ride-alongs, 4,500 track test area executions, thousands of pages of written documentation covering 400+ compliance requirements, and dozens of research studies. Lutz interprets the submission as a strong signal Tesla expects to pass, arguing companies do not submit complete packages unless they are confident of approval.

3. **Elon Musk jokes about April 20th as a possible approval date**: Elon Musk responded to the April 10th announcement with a joke that "inevitably" the date will become April 20th (a humorous number), suggesting some awareness that further delays are possible but treating it lightly.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Mystery vehicle spotted at Giga Texas — possibly Model Y L or new SUV**: Spotted by Joe Tegtmeyer, a crated and wrapped vehicle was delivered to Giga Texas. The vehicle's rear hatch appears open in photos, and the rear profile is described as too tall to be the standard Model Y L. Grok (when queried) suggested it is likely a Model Y body shell shipped from Giga Shanghai for production reference or line setup at Giga Texas — consistent with Tesla's known practice of shipping reference samples from the lead factory to the next production site. Lutz speculates it could be a US-market Model Y L with a slightly extended chassis relative to the Chinese version, or possibly a more traditional SUV-type vehicle. He notes that with Model S and Model X production ending at Fremont to enable Optimus robot manufacturing, a larger Model Y variant filling the premium family vehicle segment would make strategic sense with extensive parts and platform reuse.

## Market & Competition

1. **Waymo comparison criticized as fundamentally misleading**: Lutz argues that comparing Tesla's robotaxi program to Waymo's is deeply flawed across three dimensions: time scale (Waymo has had 12 years of rollout vs. Tesla's much shorter timeline), volume scale (Tesla's projected Cybercab monthly output will eventually exceed Waymo's total fleet), and cost structure (Tesla's cost per mile projected to be a fraction of Waymo's). He argues that at the current early stage, both companies' revenues are "relatively meaningless," but the divergence in these three dimensions will become dramatically apparent within a year. He draws a parallel to critics comparing Cybertruck sales to the F-150 and Rivian despite those competitors having 2–3 years of production head start.
