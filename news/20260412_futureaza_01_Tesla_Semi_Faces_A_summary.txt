This video is a recorded presentation by Brian, host of the YouTube channel Futurazza, delivered at the Oregon Electric Vehicle Association (OEVA) 2026 event, hosted at a Daimler facility. The talk focuses on the Freightliner eCascadia — a Class 8 electric semi-truck — covering its specs, history, economics, and competitive positioning, with a direct comparison to the Tesla Semi. The core argument is that while Tesla's Semi holds long-term promise, the eCascadia is the practical, available option today, and the Tesla Semi's limited availability and production delays make it largely a theoretical competitor for most fleet operators right now.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **eCascadia Overview and History:** The Freightliner eCascadia is an electrified version of the Cascadia platform introduced in 2007, a Class 8 truck rated for 80,000 lbs gross vehicle weight (with a regulatory allowance up to 82,000 lbs for electric vehicles). Freightliner began pilot production in 2019 with initial deployments at the Port of Long Beach and Penske Trucking in California. By 2020 they had dozens of units with various customers, by 2021 over 100 units, by 2022 they were in serial (though low-volume) production with 100 different customers, and by 2025, Schneider alone had 100 trucks logging 10 million miles — averaging over 100,000 miles per truck.

2. **Performance Specs — Torque and Power:** The eCascadia is available in single-axle and tandem-axle configurations. Horsepower ranges from approximately 320 to 470 hp depending on configuration, but the vehicle's primary performance advantage lies in torque: 11,500 lb-ft for the single-axle and 23,000 lb-ft for the tandem axle. Attendees noted that comparable diesel engines produce roughly 3,000 lb-ft of torque. The electric drivetrain enables the truck to climb grades without gearing down, and regenerative braking recovers energy on descents — reducing brake dust, eliminating engine braking noise (Jake brakes), and improving net energy efficiency.

3. **Battery and Range:** Battery capacity ranges from 291 kWh to 438 kWh depending on configuration, yielding an estimated range of approximately 150 to 230 miles. The battery chemistry is NMC (nickel manganese cobalt), supplied by CATL (referred to in the transcript as "CL") from China. The presenter noted the battery is subject to a 25% tariff, and while a tariff drawback might exist given sales volumes, he could not confirm it. He speculated battery life could reach approximately 500,000 miles, though range degradation — particularly from frequent 100% charging — remains a concern. The eCascadia only comes in a day cab configuration (no sleeper option in the electric version, unlike the diesel Cascadia).

4. **Charging Infrastructure and Speed:** Because the eCascadia entered pilot production in 2019, it was built around the CCS1 charging standard — the only standard available for trucks at the time. A single CCS1 handle delivers approximately 180 kW; using two handles simultaneously can reach 270–280 kW, achieving a 10–80% charge in approximately 90 minutes. A joint public charging venture was established with NextEra and BlackRock under the brand "Green Lane," including a major site in Colton, California featuring over 40 chargers, solar canopy, and pull-through lanes. Green Lane has plans to add MCS (Megawatt Charging System) connectors to that site and is scouting additional MCS-capable locations. Faster charging is planned for the next vehicle program, timeline unspecified. A representative noted transformer supply constraints in the U.S. remain a limiting factor for faster charging infrastructure deployment, though transformer production is ramping in other markets.

5. **Weight Advantage Over Tesla Semi:** The eCascadia weighs between 16,500 and 21,800 lbs (curb weight depending on configuration), which is approximately 3,000 lbs lighter than the Tesla Semi in each comparable configuration. Combined with the 82,000 lb GVWR allowance for electric trucks, this means fleet operators can carry 2,000–3,000 lbs more payload with the eCascadia than with either a Tesla Semi or a diesel truck.

6. **Pricing and Total Cost of Ownership:** The eCascadia does not have a publicly listed price. Industry-wide, the average Class 8 electric truck cost approximately $411,000 in 2025. Estimates for the eCascadia range from $400,000 to $500,000, compared to a diesel Cascadia priced at roughly $120,000 (day cab) to $180,000 (sleeper). A Canadian study found operators can save approximately $167,000–$170,000 in fuel and maintenance over six years compared to a diesel Cascadia. Available incentives include up to $185,000 in New York and $120,000–$150,000 in California (with higher amounts for drayage trucks). With incentives, the presenter suggested total cost of ownership approaches parity after roughly seven to eight years. He estimated that a price point around $300,000 would be needed for the eCascadia to compete effectively without heavy reliance on incentives.

7. **Domestic Battery Production Plans:** Freightliner is working to domesticate battery production through a joint venture in Mississippi with Cummins, PACCAR, and Freightliner. This would eliminate the 25% tariff on imported CATL cells, reduce shipping costs and lead times, and qualify for battery manufacturing incentives at multiple levels. Domestic batteries from this venture are expected around 2027.

## Market & Competition

1. **Tesla Semi's Real-World Availability:** Brian directly challenged the assumption that the Tesla Semi is a competitive alternative today, noting that very few customers have actually received trucks. He referenced early California grant applications suggesting Tesla Semi pricing in the $240,000–$260,000 range, but cautioned these could be loss leaders and that Tesla has significant capex to depreciate. He acknowledged Tesla's strategy of high volume and lower pricing but questioned whether that scale would materialize. The eCascadia's key advantage today is simply that it exists, can be purchased, and fits into existing Cascadia-based fleet operations.

2. **Fleet Integration Advantage:** For fleets already running diesel Cascadias, the eCascadia offers a parts and service compatibility advantage — a high percentage of frame, cab, and structural components are shared, meaning fleet operators maintain one parts inventory and can leverage Freightliner's well-established nationwide service center network. An attendee from a school district illustrated the challenge of maintaining two separate parts streams for mixed fleets, underscoring the practical value of platform continuity.

3. **Competitive Landscape and China:** The presenter acknowledged that ground-up, purpose-built electric trucks — as Chinese manufacturers and Tesla are producing — will ultimately be cheaper than platform-adapted designs. However, he argued that the eCascadia's earlier market entry built important manufacturing and operational competencies, and that Freightliner likely has a next-generation purpose-built electric truck in development. The broader industry inflection point, he argued, will come when electric truck pricing drops to the point where the payback period is short enough — ideally well under 10 years — to make diesel obsolete without relying on government incentives.
