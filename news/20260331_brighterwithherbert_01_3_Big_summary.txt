## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus Gen 3 Unveil Delayed Past Q1 2026:** Elon Musk had promised a Gen 3 Optimus reveal in Q1 2026, reiterating the timeline at the January earnings call. On the final day of the quarter (March 31, 2026), Elon responded to social media discussion by confirming "Optimus 3 is walking around, but it needs some finishing touches before it's ready to be shown." The host noted a tell that something had shifted — the team had been posting photos of themselves rather than building toward a big reveal. An April unveil remains possible but is not expected with confidence.

2. **Gen 3 as a Fundamental Commercial Redesign:** Jo Bhakdi emphasized that Optimus Gen 3 is not an incremental upgrade but a full redesign aimed at mass commercial deployment, describing it as a "fundamental revamp" across the entire system — not just the hands. The host noted that Tesla engineers on the Optimus team had described the Gen 3 as something that will "look like a human in a superhuman suit." Bhakdi added that Elon was reportedly disappointed with Optimus 2 and 2.5 and effectively restructured the program from scratch, which Bhakdi views as a bullish signal rather than a failure indicator.

3. **Mass Scale Ambitions for Optimus:** The host stressed that the purpose of Gen 3 is not simply to demonstrate capability but to serve as the foundation for production at 1 million units per year, eventually scaling to 10 million units per year. Bhakdi argued that 2026 is the critical year where these technologies — Optimus, FSD, Cybercab, and Starship — must prove themselves commercially, as competition in humanoid robotics is intensifying. He characterized a Gen 3 reveal in July or September as still acceptable, provided it feels genuinely commercial rather than another prototype.

## Robotaxi

He noted that a significant robotaxi rollout by June 2026 would be a key positive catalyst for Tesla's stock, and that failure to do so before the SpaceX IPO could trigger retail investor capital rotation away from Tesla toward SpaceX shares. The discussion framed the robotaxi launch as a pivotal moment for validating Tesla's revenue narrative in 2026.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **Elon's Vision-First Philosophy for AI and FSD:** Elon Musk publicly stated that "the future of AI is primarily video understanding and generation because photons are by far the highest bandwidth form of communication" and called these "essential tools for AGI." The host contextualized this as consistent with Elon's seven-year-old bet on vision-only autonomy — cameras plus neural nets rather than LiDAR — which has been applied to FSD and is now being extended to Optimus and to what the host described as "Macrohard," a system for controlling computers via vision rather than APIs. Grok Imagine was cited as contributing positively to XAI's gross margin, not a money-losing product.

2. **Vision AI as a Training Data Pipeline for Optimus:** Jo Bhakdi connected Grok Imagine's video generation push to Optimus training, explaining that if Tesla can translate any YouTube or third-party video into a first-person instruction video, it could generate virtually unlimited training data for humanoid robots. He argued that video understanding and video generation are "the flip side" of each other, and that Elon's aggressive push on Grok Imagine is strategically tied to building the world models needed for both FSD and Optimus, not just consumer entertainment.

## Market & Competition

1. **SpaceX IPO: Retail Allocation and Strategic Intent:** Reuters reported that SpaceX is planning to allocate up to 30% of its IPO to retail investors — three times the typical 5–10% norm — with Bank of America, Morgan Stanley, and UBS expected to lead US retail distribution. Elon denied reports that Robinhood and SoFi would be excluded. Bhakdi interpreted the retail-heavy allocation as a strategic move by Elon to build a loyal retail shareholder base from day one, analogous to how retail investors at Tesla have been critical in shareholder votes on compensation and corporate governance matters.

2. **NASDAQ Rule Change Accelerates SpaceX Index Entry:** NASDAQ announced a new rule allowing newly listed large-cap companies already qualifying for the NASDAQ 100 to be added to the index in just 15 trading days — down from the previous minimum of three months. The 10% minimum float requirement has also been removed, which is significant given SpaceX's reportedly low float (estimated at 3–5%). The host and Bhakdi framed this rule change as an example of large corporations exercising leverage over institutions, noting that S&P, Dow Jones, FTSE, and Russell are all reportedly considering similar accelerated-entry reforms.

3. **SpaceX Valuation vs. Tesla: Bhakdi's Caution:** Jo Bhakdi stated he is skeptical of buying SpaceX at IPO, arguing that at a rumored valuation exceeding $1.75 trillion, it is likely overvalued relative to near-term cash flows — describing its cash flow base as "much lower than Tesla's" at IPO. He said he expects SpaceX shares could be purchased cheaper after the IPO and that he is "inclined to stay out." He also dismissed concerns that retail selling Tesla to buy SpaceX would significantly depress Tesla's price, noting that institutional investors with DCF models would fill any tactical dip. A potential Tesla–SpaceX merger was discussed as unlikely before 2027 given the complexity of preparation required.
