This episode of "brighterwithherbert" features host Herbert alongside guests Jeff Lutz and Cern Basher discussing a mixed week of Tesla news, including Q1 delivery results, FSD version 14.3's imminent wide release, the delayed Optimus Gen 3 reveal, Cybercab production beginning, and the SpaceX IPO filing. The panel's core thesis is that while near-term delivery numbers and milestone delays are disappointing on the surface, the structural buildout for robotaxi deployment and humanoid robot scaling is accelerating in ways the market is underestimating — and that FSD 14.3 may be the critical unlock that triggers rapid expansion of unsupervised robotaxi operations.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Q1 Deliveries Miss Consensus but Context Matters:** Tesla reported Q1 2026 deliveries of 358,000 units, missing Wall Street consensus estimates that ranged from 365,000 to 375,000 units, with Tesla's own internal consensus from approximately 25 analysts sitting at 365,000 — a miss of roughly 7,000 units. The panel argued the miss was not alarming given the tailwinds and headwinds at play: the $7,500 U.S. federal EV tax credit that existed in Q1 2025 is now gone, and Chinese government EV subsidies also expired as of January 1, 2026. Despite those headwinds, the Model 3/Y number was approximately 7% higher year-over-year, and most other automakers were down year-on-year.

2. **Inventory Levels Are Healthy, Not Alarming:** Tesla produced roughly 50,000 more vehicles than it delivered in Q1, leaving inventory at approximately 28 days. Jeff Lutz argued this is actually appropriate and even slightly lean when accounting for transit time — Cern Basher estimated global average shipping time at roughly 3 weeks, or 18 days in inventory terms on a six-day work week. That implies a "true" on-hand inventory of closer to 10 days, which the panel described as lean by any industry standard. By comparison, total new car inventory across all U.S. OEMs is around 90 days, and EV-specific inventory in the U.S. is approximately 130 days. Tesla is running at 28 days. The panel noted that leaving Q4 2025 with only 15 days of inventory likely suppressed January sales, and that the current inventory build sets Tesla up for a stronger Q2 2026.

3. **Energy Segment Missed Significantly:** Tesla deployed 8.8 gigawatt-hours of energy storage in Q1, well below the Wall Street consensus of approximately 14.4 gigawatt-hours. The panel acknowledged there is no sugarcoating the miss but emphasized that energy is a "lumpy" business that does not conform neatly to 90-day reporting windows, and recommended evaluating it on at least a six-month or trailing annualized basis.

## Market & Competition

1. **BYD Cuts 100,000 Jobs Amid Profit Pressure:** BYD reduced its workforce by approximately 10%, cutting roughly 100,000 employees to bring its headcount from approximately 1 million to 870,000. This followed four consecutive quarters of declining profitability and a reported 19% reduction in net income in the most recent quarter. Cern Basher noted BYD's EV unit volumes were down approximately 30% versus their Q1 comparison from the prior year, allowing Tesla to reclaim the title of most EV shipments in the quarter. The panel interpreted BYD's situation as a sign of a cash crunch driven by the combination of rising unit costs and falling margins.

2. **BYD's "God's Eye" FSD Competitor Drawing Poor Reviews:** The panel noted that BYD has announced an autonomous driving feature called "God's Eye," but early customer reviews have been negative, citing poor maps and poor overall experience. Jeff Lutz made the point that Tesla remains the only OEM in North America and Europe where a customer can walk into a showroom, schedule a test drive, type in an address, put their hands in their lap, and have the car drive them to that destination — underscoring that the competitive gap in Full Self-Driving capability remains very wide.

3. **Macro Pressures on Auto Demand, but EV Tailwinds Emerging:** The panel discussed how falling oil prices could theoretically drive EV demand but noted the relationship is complicated by rising interest rates, tariff uncertainty, and the removal of government subsidies in multiple markets. Jeff Lutz argued that lower oil prices benefit consumers who buy vehicles in the $18,000–$30,000 range more than Tesla's typical buyer in the $40,000–$50,000 range, and that a ramped robotaxi fleet could be a more powerful demand lever than oil price movements for Tesla specifically.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD Version 14.3 in Employee Beta, Wide Release Imminent:** Elon Musk announced that FSD version 14.3 is now in the hands of Tesla employees for testing, and indicated it could go to wide public release by the end of the week of the video's recording (around April 3–6, 2026), though the panel acknowledged delays of a week or more are possible. The panel described 14.3 as a major architectural step, featuring a 10x parameter increase compared to the prior version, and said it had originally been planned for a January 2026 release before being pushed back approximately three months. Jeff Lutz described the parameter increase as enabling a new level of reasoning capability.

2. **Expected Capabilities of FSD 14.3:** The panel speculated on what 14.3 would deliver without having seen the release notes. Jeff Lutz listed expected improvements including: resolution of repeatable edge cases (driveways, curbside behavior, emergency vehicle handling), improved parking lot navigation, "Banish" functionality (the car parks itself autonomously after the driver exits), faster and more accurate route planning, improved map-following precision, and potentially enhanced Grok AI integration. Updated, more visually compelling real-time visualizations were also mentioned as a desired feature. Grok voice control — allowing verbal commands to direct the vehicle — was discussed as a desired capability but may require the AI5 chip and may not be present in 14.3.

3. **FSD Safety Performance Framed Against AI Benchmarks:** Jeff Lutz argued that FSD's real-world decision-making accuracy is extraordinary — making millions of decisions per mile from camera photons to vehicle controls, with correctness rates he described as a "99.9999x phenomenon." He contrasted this with leading large language models like Claude and ChatGPT, which he estimated have error rates of approximately 5–10% on complex interdisciplinary tasks, arguing that physical-world AI systems operate at a fundamentally different and higher standard than conversational AI.

4. **FSD Adoption Metrics Referenced:** Cern Basher cited a recent statistic — not sourced to a specific report — that over 50% of people who pay for FSD continue using it after trying it, and that those users engage it for 90–100% of their drive time. This was offered as evidence of FSD's growing real-world value as a driver retention and differentiation tool.

## Robotaxi

1. **Robotaxi Infrastructure Buildout Points to Imminent Launch:** The panel cited multiple signals suggesting Tesla is preparing for a significant robotaxi expansion in Q2 2026. These include: hiring of Autopilot support staff in approximately 18 cities, including a newly posted nighttime cleaning and support shift for Cybercabs; fleets of Model Y vehicles equipped with rear camera washers spotted parked in lots in at least four cities; Cybercab validation vehicles spotted across many additional cities; and a new iOS engineer job posting describing a robotaxi app that allows customers to "summon, track, and interact with a driverless vehicle." The panel argued Tesla's capital discipline means these expenditures would not be happening unless a launch was imminent.

2. **Unsupervised vs. Supervised Rollout Debate:** The panel discussed whether Tesla's near-term expansion would be supervised (with safety operators in vehicles) or unsupervised. Cern Basher noted that in the San Francisco Bay Area there are approximately 450 supervised Model Y vehicles already operating. Elon Musk stated as recently as January 22, 2026 that each new city launch would begin with a three-month supervised period. However, the panel argued this timeline should compress as Tesla builds experience across similar road networks, and that the operators currently in vehicles would transition to service-center roles (cleaning, charging) once unsupervised operation is approved — making the staffing investments scalable regardless.

3. **Cybercab Production Starting in April 2026:** The panel confirmed that Cybercab production is beginning in April 2026, and that these vehicles are expected to go directly into robotaxi service upon production. Cern Basher argued this is the key inflection point: at some point in 2026, Tesla will produce as many Cybercabs in a single month as Waymo's entire current fleet, and then within six months of that milestone, will produce as many in a single week as Waymo's total fleet — creating an exponential ramp that dwarfs any competitor's pace.

4. **Waymo Comparison and Competitive Context:** The panel compared Tesla's robotaxi timeline to Waymo's, noting that Waymo began supervised rides in Phoenix in December 2018 and only removed safety drivers in October 2020 — nearly two years later. Approximately nine months after going driverless, Waymo had roughly 500–600 robotaxis across two cities. The panel argued Tesla has only been running its service for approximately nine months and is already at a comparable or faster pace, but that the slope of the production ramp — driven by Cybercab manufacturing scale — will be the decisive differentiator.

5. **Regulatory Framework Needed:** Jeff Lutz called for federal executive action on autonomous vehicle regulation, specifically: a consistent federal standard across states to prevent a patchwork of conflicting laws, and federal protections making it a serious offense to damage or attack autonomous vehicles (referencing incidents involving Waymo vehicles). He framed federal AV policy as a national competitiveness issue, arguing that reducing transportation costs would lower CPI and free up economic productivity.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus Gen 3 Reveal Delayed Past Q1 2026:** Elon Musk confirmed the day before Q1 ended that Optimus Gen 3 "is walking around but needs finishing touches before it's ready to be shown," pushing the reveal past the previously stated Q1 2026 target. The panel discussed a photo of the Optimus AI team with what appeared to be a new Optimus unit in the center, which they noted looked more human-like than Gen 2 but could not confirm was Gen 3. Jeff Lutz was mildly critical of the pattern of announcing aggressive public dates for unprecedented engineering challenges, while also acknowledging that the reveal delay matters far less than whether production timelines are affected.

2. **Fremont Factory Conversion to Bot Production Begins Now:** Elon Musk announced that Model S and Model X production at Fremont is complete — existing SNX inventory is all that remains, with Model X inventory reportedly already sold out — and that Fremont is now beginning conversion to a humanoid robot production facility. The panel described this as a 1 million units per year production line. A separate, much larger facility in Giga Texas is described as being designed for a 10 million units per year bot production line, with groundwork reportedly underway. The panel emphasized this physical factory conversion is the most important leading indicator — not the reveal date — and said it does not appear delayed based on available information.

3. **Optimus Production Start Targeted for Summer 2026:** Elon Musk stated in an interview within approximately two weeks of the video's recording that Optimus production would begin in summer 2026, ahead of a prior expectation of by end of 2026. The panel treated this as an encouraging acceleration, while Jeff Lutz cautioned he would have preferred Tesla not pre-announce the summer target until production was actually underway, given the pattern of timeline revisions. The panel also noted that Gen 4 Optimus is expected to follow Gen 3, with Gen 3 being the version intended for the initial mass-scale 1 million unit ramp.

4. **Optimus Reveal Event Speculation:** The panel speculated about whether Tesla might combine the Optimus Gen 3 reveal with a Model S farewell event and/or a Roadster reveal, given the convergence of timing. Jeff Lutz expressed a preference for Optimus having its own dedicated event given the significance of the announcement.

## Financials

1. **SpaceX IPO Filed Confidentially, June 2026 Listing Target:** SpaceX filed confidentially for an IPO with a June 2026 listing target. Bloomberg reported a valuation target of over $2 trillion, up from a prior reported figure of $1.75 trillion. Starlink revenue was cited at approximately $20 billion, while xAI revenue was described as less than $1 billion. Jeff Lutz urged caution for Tesla investors considering selling Tesla shares to buy into the SpaceX IPO, noting that post-IPO the company will face the same short-term quarterly earnings scrutiny that Tesla does, and that index fund buying rules (15-day post-IPO eligibility) combined with retail demand could create volatile price action with limited share availability.
