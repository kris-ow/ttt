This video features host Herbert interviewing Jo Bhakdi — economist, investor, and tech entrepreneur — to discuss Jensen Huang's (Nvidia CEO) recent interview with Lex Friedman, in which Huang claimed we may have already achieved AGI. The core thesis is that this AGI inflection point dramatically strengthens Tesla's competitive position, particularly in robotaxi deployment, and that Tesla and SpaceX together represent the central vehicles for capitalizing on the coming era of infinite AI scaling. The conversation also explores the evolving relationship between Nvidia and Tesla/SpaceX, and whether Nvidia needs to urgently pivot toward space-based AI infrastructure to remain relevant.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **Tesla must scale unsupervised robotaxi in 2026 or risk losing ground:** Jo Bhakdi argues that Tesla needs to begin scaling unsupervised robotaxi operations in 2026. If they do, he believes no competitor can realistically catch up within 12 to 24 months, because even a fully solved autonomous driving system would still require at minimum 24 months of implementation, hardware calibration, and real-world testing before it could safely operate at scale. This gives Tesla a two-to-three-year head start in the best-case scenario for competitors.

2. **No AGI-level self-driving solution exists yet without real-world data:** The hosts note that as of now, there is no AGI-level capability that can solve self-driving purely by observing a scene without relying on trained data. Additionally, Nvidia's 19 automaker partners have not yet produced vehicles capable of full autonomous driving at scale — meaning Nvidia's partnership announcements do not translate directly into deployed autonomous vehicles.

3. **Nvidia's 19 automaker partnerships are tools, not full AV solutions:** The hosts clarify that Nvidia's claim of 19 automaker partners does not mean those automakers are deploying Nvidia's full autonomous driving technology stack. The partnerships may only involve chip purchases or the use of Nvidia's simulation engine, and actual implementation remains entirely dependent on the automakers themselves.

## Robotaxi
1. **Fleet scale is Tesla's decisive competitive moat:** Jo Bhakdi emphasizes that even if a competitor perfectly solved the autonomous driving software problem today, building a robotaxi fleet to meaningful scale would require two to three additional years. Tesla's existing fleet of FSD-capable vehicles already on the road gives it a structural head start that cannot be quickly replicated, making early 2026 deployment critical to locking in that advantage.

## Market & Competition
1. **Jensen Huang identifies three reasons Elon Musk wins at the industry level:** In his Lex Friedman interview, Jensen Huang cited Elon Musk's ability to (1) rethink systems from first principles, (2) rebuild entire supply chains, and (3) execute with a speed and urgency that forces suppliers and teams to prioritize his projects above all others. Huang described Musk as being "present at the point of action" and relentlessly minimalist — stripping everything down to only what is necessary while preserving full capability.

2. **Jo Bhakdi's AGI assessment: already past AGI from an economic and utility standpoint:** Jo Bhakdi argues that from an economics and utility perspective, current agentic AI systems (he references "Open Claude," a persistent agentic AI he uses via Telegram) are already performing in the top 1% of humans at minimum. He dismisses critiques that move the AGI goalposts toward superhuman or Einstein-level capabilities, arguing that current AI already outperforms the median human on economically relevant tasks, and that AGI has likely been achieved for approximately five months.

3. **Terafab $25 billion investment cited as proof of serious execution:** Jo Bhakdi highlights the Terafab announcement — described as a $25 billion real-money investment involving a combination of SpaceX, xAI, and Tesla — as the most important signal that space-based AI infrastructure is not just talk. He describes Tesla and SpaceX as deeply partnered "bed partners," with Tesla effectively having locked up rocket access that no other company possesses.

4. **Nvidia faces an innovator's dilemma around space AI infrastructure:** Jo Bhakdi predicts that Nvidia will need to urgently pivot toward space-based AI factory architecture within the next 24 months or risk being outcompeted by Tesla/SpaceX in that domain. He argues Nvidia currently does not understand satellite architecture, orbital economics, or the physics of space-based compute — and will likely need to acquire satellite companies (e.g., StarCloud) and space-capable engineering talent. He predicts Nvidia will recognize this urgency within nine months of the video's publication. AMD, Broadcom, and Samsung are mentioned as companies that could potentially leapfrog Nvidia if they move on space AI faster.

5. **Tesla and Nvidia are currently partners, not true competitors — but a collision course is forming:** The hosts note that Elon Musk has publicly stated Tesla is not competing with Nvidia and intends to buy as many Nvidia chips as possible. However, Jo Bhakdi argues that on a longer strategic horizon, the two are on a collision course — particularly if Nvidia fails to pivot to space AI, at which point Tesla's vertically integrated space-based compute stack would make Nvidia chips irrelevant for Tesla's core use cases. The current relationship is described as "frenemies."

6. **Hyperscalers (Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta) expected to be buyers of Tesla/SpaceX space compute:** Jo Bhakdi theorizes that as Tesla and SpaceX build out space-based AI infrastructure, hyperscalers will be the primary buyers of that compute capacity. Tesla itself would retain a portion for its own AI and manufacturing workloads, but would sell the majority to hyperscalers who cannot afford to build it themselves. He frames this as a future business model question — how much capacity Tesla keeps versus sells — with Tesla and SpaceX acting as the builders of the infrastructure.
