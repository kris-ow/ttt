This video features host Herbert (brighterwithherbert) in conversation with economist and tech entrepreneur Jo Bhakdi, discussing two major developments: Intel's announcement joining the Terafab semiconductor project alongside Tesla, SpaceX, and XAI, and a Wall Street Journal opinion piece arguing that SpaceX's Starship Heavy could revolutionize military warfare. The broader thesis is that Tesla and SpaceX are no longer merely investment opportunities but are evolving into strategic national assets spanning AI, chips, defense, energy, and space — and that most investors still dramatically underestimate this transformation.

## Market & Competition

1. **Intel Joins Terafab as a Major Strategic Partner:** Intel announced it is joining Terafab, the ambitious semiconductor manufacturing project being built by Tesla, SpaceX, and XAI. Intel's CEO Lip-Bu Tan stated the company will contribute capabilities in "designing, fabricating, and packaging ultra high performance chips at scale," and described the project as "a step change in how silicon logic, memory, and packaging will get built in the future." Terafab's stated goal is to produce one terawatt per year of compute to power advances in AI and robotics. Tesla itself announced that Terafab combines logic, memory, and advanced packaging under one roof — something described as never having been done before. A new Terafab website was also launched alongside the announcement.

2. **Why Intel Is the Right Partner — and Why It Needs Tesla:** Jo Bhakdi argues that Intel is the ideal partner precisely because it is in a weakened competitive position — having been beaten badly by Nvidia, AMD, and Broadcom — and desperately needs a growth story. He suggests Tesla could potentially acquire Intel at some point given Intel's depressed valuation and engineering talent, though he notes Tesla has historically preferred targeted technology acquisitions (pointing to the approximately $1 billion acquisition of a German robotics factory) over large corporate buyouts. He previously noted Tesla had a chip supply relationship with Samsung, including a commitment to receive all output from Samsung's factory in Taylor, Texas and a planned second factory in Taylor, Texas — but frames the Intel partnership as superior from a national security standpoint, since relying on Taiwanese or Korean technology represents a strategic vulnerability.

3. **Terafab's Chip Design Paradigm Is Fundamentally Different from Nvidia's:** Jo Bhakdi argues that Terafab is not trying to compete with Nvidia on general-purpose GPU design, which he says would be a losing battle. Instead, the design target is radically different: producing chips optimized for inference at unlimited scale, eventually for deployment in space-based AI satellites where energy constraints on Earth no longer apply. He frames this as Elon Musk's core economic insight — identifying the one lever that changes everything — and argues that manufacturing unlimited chips at reasonable cost is actually a simpler engineering challenge than FSD, Optimus, or Starship, even if it is still enormously complex in absolute terms.

4. **Space AI Data Centers Projected to Undercut Terrestrial Costs by 2027:** Jo Bhakdi argues that once Starship's cost per kilogram to orbit falls below $200 — which he projects will happen in 2027 — space-based AI data centers will become cheaper than terrestrial ones. He describes this as a sudden inflection point where demand immediately shifts to space infrastructure. He frames this as an inevitable economic outcome: if AI demand is unlimited and space data centers are cheaper, all future data center investment flows to space. This is offered as a key justification for SpaceX needing an IPO now, with a cited figure of $75 billion in capital being raised — a portion of which would go directly into capex to manufacture thousands of Starships at scale.

5. **Tesla and SpaceX Merger Seen as Near-Certain:** Both Jo Bhakdi and Chamath Palihapitiya (played in a clip from the All-In Podcast) independently assign near-100% probability to Tesla and SpaceX eventually merging. Chamath put the odds at "99.999%," arguing that a SpaceX IPO would create an externally validated mark-to-market valuation that makes combining the companies financially and legally tractable, while eliminating shareholder complaints about Elon's time allocation across entities. Jo Bhakdi agrees, noting the two companies already exchange too many critical dependencies — SpaceX needing things from Tesla, Tesla needing things from SpaceX, and both needing XAI — to remain efficiently separate. He suggests the merged entity could be called "X Corp" or similar, referencing Elon Musk's longstanding X brand.

6. **SpaceX and StarShip Heavy as a Military Asset:** A Wall Street Journal opinion piece arguing that Starship Heavy could revolutionize warfare was discussed at length. The article describes a scenario where the US pre-positions conventional munitions — including kinetic weapons, bunker busters, cluster munitions, and fuel-air explosives with terminal guidance systems — in low Earth orbit for rapid deployment anywhere on Earth within minutes, without risk to American troops. The piece cites an estimated cost of approximately $100 per kilogram to orbit as the threshold that makes this cost-effective. It also argues that key Starship technologies should be treated as national secrets, and that leaking them to China should be treated as treason. Jo Bhakdi adds the strategic "higher ground doctrine" framing — historically, controlling the high ground (arrows, artillery, planes, rockets) has determined military outcomes, and SpaceX's lift advantage over any competitor is described as a factor of 100x to 1,000x for at least five to ten years.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus Manufacturing Scale Is the Core Challenge, Not Capability:** Jo Bhakdi draws a direct parallel between the Terafab chip manufacturing philosophy and Optimus: the real challenge is not building a robot that can do somersaults, but manufacturing 100 million and eventually one billion units. He argues that competitors showing off impressive individual robot movements are missing the point entirely — the only question that matters is whether you can manufacture at civilizational scale, and Elon Musk's companies are uniquely oriented around this framing.

## Market & Competition

1. **AGI as the Master Unlock Underlying All of This:** Jo Bhakdi argues that AGI — not Elon Musk, not Tesla, not SpaceX individually — is the real driver of the investment thesis. He states that the world crossed an AGI inflection point approximately three weeks prior to the recording (around mid-to-late March 2026), and that from this point forward, being even one month ahead of a competitor in AGI development could be decisive. He cites Nvidia's announcement of a humanoid robotics model (referred to as "Neo Claw" or "Nemo Claw" in the transcript) as evidence that major players are moving fast. He also notes that major institutional investors including Fidelity have publicly stated they understand the SpaceX space-AI thesis and are valuing SpaceX at approximately $2 trillion despite roughly $15 billion in revenue, suggesting the institutional money class has broadly accepted the strategic framing.
