This video from "brighterwithherbert" features host Herbert and guest Jo Bhakdi, an economist, investor, and tech entrepreneur, discussing the potential impact of Tesla's FSD technology on its stock price and on society more broadly. The conversation covers the macro and technical setup for Tesla's stock, the global rollout of FSD supervised driving, the Cybercab production debate, and the societal implications of autonomous transportation — including the jobs-versus-lives argument around robotaxi deployment.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD Version 14.3 and European Rollout:** The host highlights FSD version 14.3 as a significant recent release, alongside the supervised FSD rollout in the Netherlands following regulatory approval from the RDW (Dutch road authority). The general manager of the RDW went further, actively recommending that all EU member states adopt similar approval. Ashok Elluswamy, VP of Tesla AI, noted that EU regulatory standards are followed by many countries beyond the 27 EU member states, potentially expanding FSD's reach significantly. FSD supervised is now officially operating in the US, Canada, Mexico, Puerto Rico, the Netherlands, Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, and China.

2. **FSD as a Stock Catalyst — When Will the Market React?:** Jo Bhakdi argues that FSD milestones are accumulating as incremental positive signals that collectively move market sentiment. He cites the Netherlands approval as an example — the stock moved up 0.5% after-hours and then 4–5% a couple of days later, illustrating how the market digests good news slowly before repricing. He compares it to a sales process: each argument builds until the buyer finally commits. He expects FSD version 15 may arrive within roughly two months of the recording.

3. **Waymo's Safety Data as a Benchmark:** The video references Waymo's autonomous driving safety record, citing a 92% reduction in serious injury and fatal crashes versus human drivers. A widely shared argument is made that if every car were driven like a Waymo, approximately 9% of America's life expectancy gap would disappear. A doctor's op-ed in the New York Times is referenced arguing that Waymo's safety data is so compelling that, were it a drug in clinical trials, it would be unethical not to immediately make it available to the control group.

## Robotaxi

1. **Tesla Robotaxi Expansion — Groundwork Being Laid:** The host notes that Tesla is hiring in 34 cities and over a dozen states for data operator roles, which they interpret as Tesla laying the groundwork for an unsupervised robotaxi rollout. Jo Bhakdi acknowledges the rollout is happening "drip by drip" and slower than some expected, but views it as an inevitable juggernaut. He previously stated that seeing 1,800 unsupervised robotaxis on the road would be a "forcing function" for the stock, but also said that visible progress alone is meaningful.

2. **Unsupervised Robotaxi as the Central Repricing Event:** Jo Bhakdi identifies the moment when unsupervised robotaxi numbers begin visibly scaling — moving from 10 to 20 to 40 vehicles — as the key stock repricing catalyst. He speculates this could possibly begin as early as June, July, or August 2026, though he emphasizes uncertainty. He describes this as the event that would push Tesla's stock "way beyond all-time highs."

3. **New York Robotaxi Ban — Union Pushback:** The video discusses how the New York taxi union successfully lobbied the New York governor to ban robotaxi deployment, effectively excluding Waymo from the city. This is framed as a concrete example of organized labor blocking autonomous transportation despite compelling safety data, and is used to illustrate the broader societal tension between job preservation and life-saving technology.

4. **Cybercab Production Debate — Steering Wheels or Not?:** The host references a post by Joe Tegtmeyer, who was given a private tour of the Cybercab production line, arguing against the theory that Cybercabs with steering wheels are destined for consumer sale rather than robotaxi use. Tegtmeyer's evidence shows that Cybercabs are built without steering wheels from the factory floor, and that adding a steering wheel requires retrofitting — it is not the default configuration. Jo Bhakdi reinforces this, arguing that a vehicle optimized for robotaxi use (suspension, speed, acceleration, handling) is fundamentally different from a consumer car and that simply adding a steering wheel to make it a consumer vehicle would be "completely amateurish." He also notes the Cybercab has no rear window, making it impractical as a standard consumer vehicle.

He describes Tesla as "lagging behind the NASDAQ recovery" and characterizes the current setup as a "coiled spring."

2. **Macro Backdrop and Q2 2026 Bullishness:** Jo Bhakdi expresses a strongly bullish view on Q2 2026, citing easing Iran-related geopolitical concerns and a broader AI-driven market rally (pointing to Nvidia moving from $175 to $200 as an indicator). He had previously reduced his Tesla position due to macro concerns but re-entered after the Iran situation appeared to stabilize. He views Tesla as benefiting from positive AI category sentiment while trading at a discount relative to recent highs.

3. **Societal and Economic Impact of Autonomous Transportation:** Jo Bhakdi and the host discuss the broad ripple effects of robotaxi deployment on the wider economy. Key points include: the potential to reduce the cost of car ownership (cited as the third-highest household expense after housing); the unlocking of real estate markets in areas far from train lines (using Greenwich, CT as an example of train-driven real estate pricing); and the productivity gains from converting commute time into working or leisure time. The host also argues that over a third of the cost of consumer goods is attributable to transportation, and that autonomous delivery could reduce those costs broadly. Jo Bhakdi estimates a hypothetical unlimited monthly robotaxi subscription could cost around $199, freeing up the $300–$500/month many households currently spend on car ownership.
