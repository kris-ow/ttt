This video from "brighterwithherbert" features host Herbert and guest Brian White (of the "Future Aza" YouTube channel) analyzing the competitive landscape in the autonomous driving and robotaxi space. The core thesis is that Tesla's competitors — Waymo, BYD, and Nvidia's ecosystem of 19 automotive partners — are each facing significant structural, technical, or operational challenges, while Tesla's approach is being systematically underestimated. The discussion draws heavily from an interview of Waymo co-CEO Dimitri Dolgov conducted by John Collison on his podcast "The Cheeky Pint."

## Autonomous Driving

1. **BYD's "God's Eye" Falls Short of Its Promises:** BYD's autonomous driving platform, called God's Eye, has drawn user complaints about steering flaws, navigation screen malfunctions, and delays, according to a new report discussed in the video. Brian White characterizes BYD's current capability as roughly equivalent to Tesla FSD version 8 or 9 — a "very good ADAS" driver assist system, but nowhere near true self-driving. He notes that BYD's code is described as a "hodgepodge of neural net and hand-coded" logic, a heuristic approach he argues is fundamentally limited. Only BYD's top-tier premium vehicles receive anything approaching a full self-driving feature, not the broad fleet as initially implied by BYD's announcement.

2. **BYD Partnerships with Nvidia Signal Technical Gap:** Brian White flags that BYD's newly announced partnership with Nvidia is a sign that the company is farther behind in autonomous driving than previously thought. His argument is that if BYD were a true world leader in self-driving, it would not need to seek out Nvidia as a dance partner at this stage. He acknowledges that BYD's strategy of including God's Eye for free across its fleet is smart for accelerating data collection, but emphasizes that training data alone is insufficient — the right engineers and the right architectural approach are also required.

3. **Waymo Co-CEO Acknowledges Core Technology Is Ready but Deployment Is Not:** In the Cheeky Pint podcast clip, Waymo co-CEO Dimitri Dolgov states that Waymo has moved past "scientific research and deep core technology development" into a phase of "accelerated global scaling and deployment." He says he sees no limitations or gaps in the core technology today, but stresses that significant specialization and validation work is still required before deploying responsibly in new cities. He confirms Waymo is planning to start operating in London and Tokyo in 2026, but explicitly states you cannot simply take the San Francisco driver and deploy it in London without additional work.

4. **Waymo's Lidar Legacy Rooted in Early Technological Constraints:** The hosts explain that Waymo's reliance on lidar was not a deliberate technical superiority claim but a legacy of when the company started — when vision cameras were not good enough and neural networks did not yet exist. They note that Dolgov himself signals Waymo will change its sensor approach in the future. Brian White argues this legacy commitment to lidar adds cost, complexity, and points of failure, and that Waymo's Gen 6 sensor hardware, while no longer rotating, is still physically large and visible on top of the vehicle.

5. **ADAS-First Approaches Are a Dead End for True Self-Driving:** Both hosts agree that companies trying to incrementally improve ADAS into full self-driving are pursuing a fundamentally flawed path. Brian White uses the analogy of trying to miniaturize a clock using gears — at some point, the approach itself must be abandoned in favor of a completely different architecture. Dolgov himself is quoted as acknowledging this distinction, stating that the transition from ADAS to self-driving requires a different technological foundation entirely.

## Robotaxi

1. **Waymo's Fleet Operations Remain Heavily Manual:** In the podcast clip, Dolgov describes Waymo's depot operations, where cars automatically navigate to charging stalls or cleaning bays, but the actual charging (plugging in a cable) and cleaning are still entirely manual processes. Brian White and Herbert emphasize this point — at roughly 3,000 vehicles across its operating cities, Waymo has not yet automated the fundamental physical maintenance tasks. Brian White questions whether Waymo's internal cameras are sufficient to even detect the full range of cleaning needs (e.g., smell, debris behind seats).

2. **Waymo's Scaling to Four Cities Compared Unfavorably to Tesla's Potential:** The hosts note that Waymo, after 12-plus years of operation, is now able to launch in four cities simultaneously, which it presents as a major scaling milestone. They counter that Tesla, which only launched its unsupervised FSD robotaxi service in June 2024 (less than a year prior to the video), can already deploy across a comparable number of cities — and with a vision-only, non-geofenced system that generalizes globally rather than requiring city-specific preparation.

3. **Waymo Doing Half a Million Rides Per Week:** Brian White acknowledges Waymo is conducting approximately half a million rides per week, calling this "commendable" and "not nothing." However, he questions whether Waymo is making any money, noting the company has not disclosed profitability data and currently represents the most expensive robotaxi option on the market. He does not see a path where Waymo's unit economics become substantially cheaper than Uber, given its ongoing manual operational overhead and hardware complexity.

4. **Waymo's Gen 6 Custom Vehicle Faces Tariff and Scale Barriers in the US:** The hosts discuss Waymo's sixth-generation custom robotaxi platform, which Dolgov says he rode in fully autonomously and which is coming in 2026. Brian White identifies it as likely based on the Zeekr minivan — a Chinese-manufactured vehicle. He argues that 100% US import tariffs apply at the point of import (not the point of sale), making the economics of importing and upfitting the vehicle prohibitive. He also raises the regulatory and security concern of a Chinese-manufactured vehicle with active sensors operating on US streets, regardless of Google owning the data.

5. **Cybercab's Design Addresses Waymo's Known Operational Weaknesses:** The hosts argue that the Cybercab was purpose-built to solve the kinds of operational problems Waymo is now encountering after years in the field. Specifically, they note the Cybercab's interior design leaves little space for debris to accumulate (making cleaning easier), its color reportedly masks exterior dirt well, and its single interior camera has a field of view covering the full passenger area from the knees up. Brian White contrasts this with standard cars retrofitted for robotaxi use, which have under-seat crevices and other hard-to-clean areas.

## Market & Competition

1. **Nvidia's 19 Automotive Partners Do Not Represent 19 Self-Driving Solutions:** The hosts address what they frame as a widespread misunderstanding following Nvidia's announcement of partnerships with 19 automakers. They clarify that these companies are simply customers who purchase one or more Nvidia products — not companies with complete, turnkey self-driving systems. Nvidia's VP of Automotive is quoted directly stating that Tesla is Nvidia's largest automotive customer, but that Tesla does not use Nvidia's in-car computer — it uses Nvidia's data center infrastructure for training only. The hosts use this to argue that Nvidia's ecosystem is a collection of tools (picks and shovels), not finished autonomous driving solutions.

2. **BYD's Financial and Competitive Struggles Are Real but Overstated:** The hosts acknowledge that BYD has faced declining sales volumes and shrinking profitability and margins, and that there was a period when BYD outsold Tesla in EVs — which at the time prompted widespread claims that Tesla was finished. Brian White does not believe BYD is on the verge of failure and notes the company is gaining momentum in European markets. However, on the autonomous driving front specifically, he is unambiguous that BYD is years behind and currently rethinking its technical architecture from the ground up.
