PART ONE: MARKET AND INVESTMENT QUESTIONS

Institutional investors and serious retail investors bucket Tesla and SpaceX separately, pointing to Cathie Wood's ARK as an example where space and robotics/autonomy funds have separate budgets. A survey by "Tesla Economics on X" apparently surprised some observers but confirmed retail holders largely plan to keep Tesla shares.

2. SpaceX IPO timing — likely mid-2026, within three to four months of the stream date.
SpaceX is preparing Starship version 3 for an April launch, which Joe calls potentially the commercial version. Version 3 could push cost-to-orbit below $200 per kilogram, unlocking a new AI-driven space race. Joe says SpaceX needs capital now and believes the IPO is likely to happen within three to four months.

3. The real, overlooked risk for Tesla options holders — merger/acquisition scenario.
Joe identifies a serious but underappreciated risk: if SpaceX acquires Tesla (which he assigns a "double-digit percentage probability"), all outstanding options, including long-dated LEAPS, would likely be accelerated and executed at the acquisition price. If Tesla is acquired at $600 and a holder has a $700 or $800 strike price LEAP, those options expire worthless. Even a rumor of acquisition post-IPO could trigger panic selling of LEAPS. He flags this as requiring much deeper research given his emphasis on LEAP strategies for Tesla.

4. Tesla FSD approval in the EU — country-by-country rollout more likely than bloc-wide approval.
The Netherlands is described as essentially a "safe bet" to be first, with Czech Republic or Slovakia likely following. Once one EU country approves, there is a regulatory shortcut for others to follow. Joe does not expect the full EU to approve simultaneously, but believes the majority of EU countries will approve individually before the EU as a whole acts.

Joe expects unsupervised commercial operations to begin in April, May, or June 2026. He has a video forthcoming on speculation that Cyber Cab could be sold as a buffer product if Tesla is not ready to scale fully by April. He notes that hundreds of Cyber Caps have reportedly been spotted in Texas.

This is the session's major reveal. The Pioneer Reserve is a regulated Luxembourg securitization company that Joe founded. It holds his "Pioneer Alpha" portfolio (primarily Tesla, Nvidia, Robinhood) and issues bonds to external qualified investors. The portfolio is up 113% since September 1, 2025 (the tracking start date), though Joe acknowledges this includes favorable conditions.

The flagship bond type mentioned is the "G45" — targeting 30% annual return, capped at 45% per year, with a three-year maturity (potential to triple capital). Excess portfolio returns above the cap flow into a reserve buffer; when the portfolio underperforms, the reserve subsidizes the bond to maintain the yield corridor. The goal is to translate AGI-era upside into a stable, high-yield instrument, described as the opposite of ARK Invest's volatile fund structure.

Excess capital beyond the reserve buffer threshold is distributed into: future bond issuances (pre-funding new investor buffers), distribution partners, research, and operations — with the research budget funding Pioneer Lands content and analysis.

Joe explicitly states this is not a public solicitation and is restricted to qualified/accredited investors. He notes the U.S. has the most accessible regulatory pathway. MicroStrategy's financial model was a partial inspiration for the structure.
