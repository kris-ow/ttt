The hosts interpret this as equipment for the assembly line being staged for installation. 3. The stamping section is visible and shows signs of expansion capacity. The hosts note a large "stamping cathedral" door and observe that there are empty pits on one side of the stamping area — interpreted as pre-built capacity to install additional stamping machines in the future.

4. The factory's footprint appears capable of exceeding 50,000 units per year, though production will need to ramp gradually. The hosts note that 50,000 units per year would represent approximately 25% of the entire US Class 8 truck market, meaning a slow ramp is both expected and appropriate, particularly given battery supply constraints.

5. The Tesla Semi was completely absent from Tesla's most recent earnings call, which both hosts found surprising. Mark notes it is a new product line, a new market segment, and the factory is close to opening — all of which would normally warrant mention. Brian speculates it may have been overshadowed by higher-profile announcements like Robotaxi, unsupervised FSD, and Optimus robots, and also attributes the omission partly to the broken "Say Technologies" investor Q&A platform, which prioritizes earliest questions over most-upvoted ones.

6. Megacharger infrastructure for the Tesla Semi varies by location. In response to a viewer question, Brian notes that Pepsi's Bakersfield/Fresno depot has a 700 kilowatt charger (from a 2023 article), while Pepsi received 18 new 1-megawatt Megachargers in North Carolina last summer. For depot operations where trucks return nightly, slower chargers are sufficient — full overnight charging is adequate for daily routes without needing fast charging stops.

7. The economic case for the Tesla Semi is strong over its lifetime despite a high purchase price. Brian references a trucking brokerage article noting electric semis cost 10–80% more upfront than diesel before rebates (ranging from $150,000 to over $275,000 depending on spec, vs. diesel sleepers exceeding $500,000 for long-range models). However, diesel trucks get roughly 7 miles per gallon, costing approximately $0.81 per mile in fuel alone. Tesla Semi uses 1.7 kWh per mile vs. 2–2.5 kWh for most competitors, and at $0.12–0.20 per kWh electricity, the fuel cost savings are approximately $0.50 per mile. Over one million miles, that represents $500,000 in savings — effectively making the truck free relative to its premium.
