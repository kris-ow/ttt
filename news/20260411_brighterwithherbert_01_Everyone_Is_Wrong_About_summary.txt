This video from the "brighterwithherbert" channel discusses the release of FSD v14.3, arguing that the broader online reaction dismissing it as another overpromise is wrong. Host Herbert is joined by Phil (a founding engineer at Rivian with Apple engineering management experience) and Cern Basher (a chartered financial analyst running Brilliant Advice wealth management) to break down what the update actually delivers and why it matters for unsupervised robotaxi deployment. The core thesis is that v14.3's two most significant changes — a 20% faster reaction time and a complete rewrite of the AI compiler and runtime from the ground up — together form the foundation needed for unsupervised robotaxi at scale, without needing to wait for version 15.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD v14.3 reaction time is now approximately 0.2 seconds:** Video analysis by "AI Driver" (a prominent FSD tester) found that v14.3 reacted to a changing traffic light in just 2/10 of a second from the moment the light turned yellow to when the car began controlling for it. For context, the industry standard reaction time used in accident reconstructions is 1.5 seconds for the full window from detecting a threat to physically applying brakes. Even an above-average human driver reacting in one second is still 5 to 7 times slower than v14.3. Elite sprinters, by comparison, are disqualified for false starts if they react in under one-tenth of a second, meaning FSD is now approaching the absolute physiological limit of human reaction capability.

2. **The AI compiler and runtime were rewritten from the ground up using MLIR:** Tesla's release notes confirm the rewrite was accomplished using MLIR (Multi-Level Intermediate Representation), which Phil explains allows Tesla engineers to take an abstract, system-wide view of where compute latency actually occurs — primarily in memory loads rather than in raw computation itself. By optimizing how data flows through the neural network stack (targeting approximately 36 inference cycles per second), the team eliminated bottlenecks that previously constrained both reaction time and model iteration speed. Phil speculates this groundwork was likely added to the roadmap in early fall and was a key reason the release took longer than expected.

3. **The runtime rewrite enables faster future model iteration:** Both the release notes and AI Driver's analysis highlight that a critical but underreported line item is the improvement to model iteration speed. Phil explains that with the previous custom-built runtime that had been incrementally patched over years, there was no good system-wide optimizer. The new architecture now allows Tesla to rapidly iterate on the underlying AI model — adding reinforcement learning cases, increasing vision resolution, and eventually scaling parameter count — without the bottlenecks of the old system constraining each update cycle.

4. **v14.3 introduces some regressions alongside its improvements:** AI Driver noted that some of the indecisive, jerky steering behaviors from early v14 builds have returned in v14.3, particularly at straightforward unprotected left turns and flashing crosswalks without pedestrians. He attributes this directly to the speed of v14.3's decision-to-control-output pipeline: the system is now so fast it appears to be simultaneously weighing many more options before committing, which creates perceptible hesitation. The panelists agree this is expected from a ground-up rewrite and will be addressed in point releases (e.g., 14.3.1, 14.3.2) before a full wide release.

5. **Reinforcement learning drove a large portion of the v14.3 improvements:** Cern Basher and Phil both emphasize that many of the specific scenario improvements listed in the release notes — improved handling of small animals, school buses, emergency vehicles, rare objects, complex intersections, yellow light stopping, and pothole avoidance (upcoming) — are all products of reinforcement learning (RL) cases built from incidents observed by Tesla's safety drivers in the robotaxi program. Phil describes a "human pipeline" that has matured significantly since the robotaxi program began, where safety observers identify edge cases, engineers construct RL scenarios, run them through the simulator, and integrate the results into the model.

6. **Version 15 will be the large-model, 10x parameter release — but is not required for unsupervised robotaxi:** Elon Musk confirmed in a post that v15 will be the "large model" with the 10x parameter increase, and that the small model has been iterating so fast the large model has not yet caught up. However, all three panelists agree that Elon did not say v15 is a prerequisite for unsupervised robotaxi deployment. Phil notes that increasing parameters 10x would be meaningless without first having an optimized runtime to support it on Hardware 4, which is exactly what v14.3 delivers. Herbert states he has predicted that fully unsupervised operation in Austin could happen as early as April 2026.

7. **Hardware 3 vehicles may receive a "v14 light" but are unlikely to run unsupervised:** Cern raised the possibility that the more efficient new runtime could enable a lighter version of upgraded FSD to run on Hardware 3 vehicles, citing Tesla's prior earnings call comments about seeing "a potential pathway" for Hardware 3. Phil acknowledges this speculation is reasonable given that engineering under hardware constraints often produces optimizations that benefit all platforms. However, Phil is firm in his prediction that Hardware 3 vehicles will never run unsupervised — neither in the robotaxi network nor in a customer scenario — because the compute environment is simply too limited for that level of operation.

8. **The improved driver monitoring system upgrade is targeted at supervised Hardware 3 use:** The upcoming feature to improve driver monitoring system sensitivity — with better eye gaze tracking, eyewear handling, and variable lighting accuracy — is interpreted by Phil as primarily a Hardware 3 feature. Since unsupervised FSD is not expected to come to Hardware 3, Tesla wants the supervised experience on those vehicles to be as accurate and non-intrusive as possible, only intervening when genuinely necessary. Cern also notes practical annoyances like sunglasses or looking at the in-car screen triggering false attention warnings, which this update aims to address.

## Robotaxi

1. **v14.3's faster reaction time directly addresses robotaxi safety incidents:** Cern specifically calls out that the one incident Tesla's robotaxi had to report between January 15 and February 15, 2026 — a front vehicle stopping suddenly, resulting in a bumper touch — is exactly the type of scenario that v14.3's 20% faster reaction time would prevent. He also notes that Waymo's well-known highway collision issues stem not from lidar failing to detect cutting-in vehicles, but from slow reaction time after detection. The 0.2-second reaction window would effectively eliminate that class of accident.

2. **Destination handling with reasoning was added first because robotaxi requires it:** Herbert highlights that v14.3 added reasoning specifically to destination handling (pickup and drop-off), with broader reasoning expansion to other driving behaviors listed as an upcoming improvement. Phil explains that this feature was essentially absent from consumer FSD data because regular FSD users never needed the car to autonomously pick them up or drop them off — they simply took control at the end of a trip. The robotaxi operation mode requires precise, safe, and efficient pickup and drop-off logic, and this is one of the areas most specific to the robotaxi use case that had to be built from near-scratch using robotaxi operational data.

3. **Scaling robotaxi requires minimizing remote operations calls:** Phil makes the point that whatever edge cases remain in FSD — trees blocking roads, unusual drop-off situations, system degradation — must be handled autonomously by the vehicle rather than escalating to a remote operations center. He argues Tesla is actively thinking about what their operations center would look like at 1,000x, 2,000x, or 5,000x fleet scale, and that the goal is to reduce calls to only truly critical situations. The v14.3 feature improving handling of temporary system degradations and automatic recovery without driver intervention is directly relevant to this requirement.

4. **Tesla is prioritizing robotaxi unsupervised deployment ahead of consumer unsupervised FSD:** The panel agrees that Tesla's internal priority order is robotaxi-first, consumer unsupervised second. Phil argues there is a distinct and complex set of consumer-side items required for customer-unsupervised FSD that are simply lower priority right now. Cern and Phil also note a business logic rationale: scaling robotaxi first generates revenue and cash flow needed to absorb the inevitable litigation from edge-case accidents — a point Elon himself made publicly, noting that Tesla will likely avoid 90% of accidents but will still get sued for the unavoidable 10%.

5. **Safety observer pipeline has matured significantly since summer 2025 robotaxi launch:** Phil describes the early days of the robotaxi program as "a bit of chaos" in terms of how incidents were reported, categorized, and fed back into the engineering process. He believes the human pipeline — from safety driver observation through to RL case construction and model integration — has now matured to a level of efficiency that is enabling the quality and breadth of scenario coverage seen in v14.3. This maturation is described as one of the less-discussed but important reasons why the robotaxi program is now positioned to scale.
