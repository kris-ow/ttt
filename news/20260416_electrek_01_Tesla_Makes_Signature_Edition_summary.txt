Tesla is requiring buyers of its limited Signature Edition Model S and Model X to sign a "No Resale Agreement" carrying a $50,000 liquidated damages penalty — or the full resale value, whichever is greater — if they sell within the first year of ownership.

## Electric Vehicles
1. **Signature Edition terms and restrictions:** The agreement prohibits buyers from selling or attempting to sell the vehicle within the first year. If a sale becomes necessary, Tesla must be notified in writing and given reasonable time to repurchase the vehicle at the original price minus $0.25 per mile driven, wear and tear, and costs to meet Tesla's Used Vehicle Standards. Third-party sales require Tesla's written consent. Breach — or Tesla's "reasonable belief" a breach is imminent — triggers the $50,000 penalty or full resale proceeds, whichever is greater, plus potential injunctive relief and a ban on future Tesla purchases.

2. **Vehicle details and scarcity:** The Signature Edition was announced as a final farewell for the Model S and Model X, which are being discontinued after more than a decade. Only 250 Model S and 100 Model X units will be produced (350 total), finished in Garnet Red with gold accents, white Alcantara interior, and numbered dash plaques. Both are priced at $159,420 and include FSD (Supervised), lifetime Supercharging, four years of Premium Service, and lifetime Premium Connectivity. Access is invite-only via email to select existing owners.

3. **Cybertruck precedent:** Tesla deployed a nearly identical anti-flipping clause at the Cybertruck launch in late 2023, with the same $50,000 penalty and right of first refusal. Early Cybertrucks were selling for $200,000–$350,000 on the secondary market. Tesla added the clause, removed it after backlash, reinstated it, then quietly dropped it by mid-2024 as supply caught up and resale premiums evaporated. Tesla never publicly enforced the penalty against any Cybertruck flipper, though some owners reported being blacklisted.

## Editorial Stance
The author finds the no-resale agreement understandable given the genuine scarcity (350 units, no additional supply), but questions whether Tesla will enforce it any more successfully than the Cybertruck clause, noting that courts in many states are skeptical of liquidated damages clauses functioning as penalties. The author argues the blacklist threat is likely the more credible enforcement mechanism, as Tesla can act unilaterally, whereas the $50,000 damages clause would face legal uncertainty and PR risk if pursued in court.
