This video is an in-car review of the Rivian R1S tri-motor with max pack battery, conducted by the futureaza channel host along with family members. The host offers a candid assessment of the vehicle's features, software, charging experience, and price, drawing occasional comparisons to Tesla. The core argument is that the R1S is an impressively built, capable adventure vehicle but comes at a steep price with significant charging limitations.

## Electric Vehicles
1. **Rivian R1S Tri-Motor Specs and Price:** The R1S tri-motor starts at $107,000, and the version reviewed — equipped with the max pack battery — comes in at $119,500. The max pack brings a 141 kWh battery pack, delivering a claimed 0–60 mph time of around 3 seconds and over 300 miles of city range. Highway range is notably lower depending on speed, as the host reported chewing through the battery quickly at higher speeds.

2. **Interior Quality and Third-Row Experience:** The host praised the R1S's interior fit and finish as "gorgeous" — though noted it was "too fancy" for their taste — and highlighted the third row as genuinely comfortable and well-equipped. Compared to the third row in a Model Y, the Rivian offers more space, dedicated vents, speakers, cup holders in better positions, and USB-C ports on the sides rather than in an awkward low position. The host said it cannot be compared to the Model Y third row and that the discontinued Model X would be the only real Tesla comparison point.

3. **Exterior Build Quality:** The host found the exterior fit and finish to be "absolutely flawless," with no gaps, misalignments, or color differences. They attributed this to the vehicle being built in Illinois ("the Midwest"), noting that the rust belt has deep expertise in steel manufacturing.

4. **Charging Experience and Limitations:** Charging was described as "painfully slow" due to the massive battery size. The R1S has access to the Tesla Supercharger network but only via a non-native adapter port, meaning compatibility is limited to certain Supercharger stations. During the trip, the host encountered a full Supercharger, then an incompatible one, before finding a third that worked — arriving at around 13–16% charge. In 38 minutes at 180 kW, the battery went from 16% to 83%, which the host characterized as a poor experience for a $119,500 vehicle. The home fast-charging adapter also did not work during the review period, requiring reliance on public DC fast charging. The host suggested this car requires home charging access.

5. **Software and Features:** The host praised Rivian's software team, describing the overall software experience as "quite good" — not as "crisp and snappy" as Tesla's but well ahead of most other EVs tested. Notable features include adjustable ride height, adjustable regenerative braking, automatic bird's-eye and front camera views when pulling into parking spots, dog mode, camp mode, sentry mode (with a Sasquatch animation), a Wi-Fi hotspot, 110V outlets in the rear, and Alexa integration (though the host found Alexa limited and unhelpful). The app-based unlock occasionally failed to wake the car.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **Rivian's Highway Driver Assist vs. Tesla FSD:** The R1S includes a highway driver assist system described as similar to Autopilot but with lane changes included at no extra cost. Tesla, by contrast, has removed lane changes from its base driver assistance tier, which the host speculated is a strategy to push FSD adoption — a strategy the host criticized. Over approximately 500 miles of use, the host experienced only one hard disengagement, triggered by intense rain, and otherwise found the system to work well. The host was clear this system is not comparable to FSD and has no city autonomy.

2. **Skepticism About Rivian's Autonomous Driving Roadmap:** The host expressed skepticism about Rivian's autonomous driving ambitions, noting that Rivian held an "autonomy day" event but did not invite independent analysts, EV journalists, or prominent reviewers. The host interpreted this lack of transparency as a red flag — "smoke and mirrors rather than tech" — and said they would not accept Rivian's autonomy claims as credible until demonstrated openly. They characterized Rivian's autonomous future as "hopes and dreams rather than facts and reason."

## Market & Competition
1. **Rivian R1S Target Audience and Competitive Positioning:** The host positioned the R1S as a vehicle for adventurers and enthusiasts who need serious off-road capability and large cargo/passenger space. They noted it is not a first-generation product — Rivian's Gen 1 was already good, and this is meaningfully better — but it remains very heavy and slow to charge. Direct Tesla comparison was described as difficult given how different the vehicles are. The host mentioned anticipation for the upcoming Rivian R2 as a potentially more broadly accessible product.
