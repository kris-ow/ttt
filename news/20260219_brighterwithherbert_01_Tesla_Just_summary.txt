Tesla's robotaxi fleet is experiencing rapid growth and measurable safety improvements, according to the hosts of this discussion between the channel's presenter and guest commentator Brian White (of the YouTube channel Futurza). The video uses newly released NHTSA data to argue that mainstream media coverage of Tesla robotaxi "crashes" is deeply misleading, and that the underlying safety trajectory tells a far more optimistic story. The central thesis is that Tesla's autonomous vehicle program has crossed an inflection point where safety is improving faster than any competitor, and regulatory barriers are far less significant than critics claim.

Key Points:

1. Fleet size is growing rapidly. As of the filming date, Tesla's robotaxi fleet stands at 372 vehicles — 89 in Austin and 283 in the Bay Area — after 42 vehicles were added in just two days. Analyst Joe Bacti projects the fleet could hit 450 vehicles by the following week and reach 1,800 vehicles by April 2026. The robottaxitracker.com, maintained by Ethan McKenna, is cited as the tracking source, and the hosts note the number was still sitting around 303–305 just days before filming.

2. NHTSA data shows Tesla's Austin fleet doubled its safety in one month. Between December 2025 and January 2026, Tesla's miles-per-incident figure in Austin went from approximately 200,000 to 417,000. An X user named "Reigns" (identified as holding a PhD in computer science) analyzed the NHTSA data and projects that Tesla's robotaxi will surpass human driver safety levels — estimated at 700,000 miles per at-fault incident — by approximately March 15, 2026, which was roughly one month from the recording date.

3. The "crash" reporting standard is heavily distorted compared to human driver reporting. NHTSA requires autonomous vehicle operators to report all incidents, including parking lot bumper taps and being struck while stationary. Human drivers routinely do not report minor low-speed contacts, fender benders under 10 mph, or parking lot dings. This creates a systematic bias that makes AV incident counts look worse in headlines than they actually are. The hosts emphasize that when you remove not-at-fault incidents and stationary vehicle contacts, Tesla's true at-fault safety record improves significantly.

4. The January 2026 incidents were almost entirely minor. Of the five incidents reported in January, one involved an Austin city bus striking a stationary Tesla robotaxi, and two involved the vehicle backing into objects in a parking lot. All occurred with a safety monitor present. The majority of incidents were under 8 mph, with only one or two reaching 17–27 mph. The hosts express skepticism about one hospitalization report linked to a 2 mph incident, suggesting possible legal opportunism.

5. Waymo's safety trajectory is flat while Tesla's is rising sharply. Waymo, operating approximately 3,000 vehicles across six U.S. cities, is logging 4 million autonomous miles and 400,000 paid rides per week. However, Waymo's miles-per-at-fault-crash metric sits at roughly 46,000 and has shown no improvement over time. Brian White attributes this plateau to Waymo's handcoded software architecture, arguing it has reached a "local maximum" and will not improve until the company transitions to neural net-based systems. Tesla's metric, by contrast, is on a steep upward curve.

6. Waymo uses 70 remote assistance operators globally, raising scalability questions. Waymo confirmed to Congress that it employs approximately 35 operators in the Philippines and 35 in the U.S. to provide "remote assistance" — not remote driving — when the vehicle's system requests help. The hosts note this is functionally similar to having a supervisor ready to intervene and question whether this model can scale cost-effectively as fleet size grows into the tens of thousands.

7. Regulatory barriers to Tesla's rollout are widely misunderstood. Brian White clarifies that in the U.S., vehicle certification is self-certification by automakers — Tesla does not need government approval to declare a vehicle road-ready. Explicit regulatory approval exists in approximately five states (including Texas, California, and Florida, which represent a large share of U.S. population), and most other states offer tacit approval with no explicit prohibition. NHTSA does cap fully autonomous vehicles (no steering wheel or pedals) at approximately 2,500–2,900 units, but this cap can be waived and does not apply to standard vehicles like the Model Y used as robotaxis. The SAFE Drive Act, potentially passing by June, could raise the cap to 95,000 units.

8. The Cybercab's lack of steering wheel is not a hard barrier to scale. The hosts argue Tesla could equip Cybercabs with non-functional steering wheels and pedals to bypass the NHTSA cap while still operating them as fully autonomous robotaxis — similar to Maggie Simpson's toy steering wheel. Alternatively, Tesla can continue scaling with Model Y vehicles, which face no unit cap.

Conclusions and Predictions:
The hosts expect Tesla's robotaxi to reach human-level safety benchmarks by mid-March 2026. They anticipate incident rates will fall further in Q2 as winter weather — historically Tesla's most dangerous FSD season — gives way to summer. Tesla's fleet will not likely exceed 90,000 vehicles in 2026, making the SAFE Drive Act cap a non-issue for the near term. The broader recommendation is that investors and observers should focus on the safety improvement trajectory rather than raw incident counts reported in headlines.
