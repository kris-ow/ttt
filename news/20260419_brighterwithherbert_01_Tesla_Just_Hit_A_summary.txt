This video from "brighterwithherbert" covers four interconnected developments in the robotaxi space, framed around Tesla's growing safety record, the unresolved Hardware 3 FSD question, Uber's dramatic strategic shift toward owning robotaxi fleets, and ARK Invest's projection of a multi-trillion-dollar autonomous transport market. Host Herbert is joined by Larry Goldberg, a serial entrepreneur and active venture capital investor, to analyze what these developments mean for Tesla's autonomous driving thesis. The core argument is that Tesla's safety trajectory is improving rapidly, the broader robotaxi market is far larger than commonly understood, and competitors like Uber are making expensive strategic miscalculations.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **NHTSA Safety Data Shows Tesla at Zero Incidents:** The most recent NHTSA data covering February 17 to March 15 shows Tesla recorded zero autonomous driving incidents — down from one incident in the prior period (January 15 to February 17) and five incidents the period before that (December 15 to January 15). The discussion notes that the five-incident period generated negative headlines claiming Tesla was multiple times worse than human drivers. Both host and guest interpret the trend — five, one, zero — as a strong positive signal, emphasizing that this represents the worst FSD will ever be, and that performance will only improve from here.

2. **FSD Version 14.3.1 Going Wide:** The latest FSD version (14.3.1) is described as beginning to roll out broadly at the time of the video. Larry Goldberg characterizes version 14.3 as delivering noticeably smoother driving and significantly improved responsiveness to conditions the prior version missed. The only remaining weakness cited is parking, which he says still needs work even at version 14.3. The broader point is that each release has produced meaningful safety improvements, and this trend is expected to continue.

3. **Comparing Tesla vs. Waymo vs. Zoox — Apples-to-Oranges Problem:** The hosts walk through a comparison of the three companies' safety records using NHTSA data but caution heavily against direct comparisons. A key distinction is raised between ADS (fully automated driving systems, like Waymo) and ADAS (advanced driver assistance systems, like Tesla's current Bay Area operations where a safety monitor is present). Using Grok estimates and a breakdown credited to a community analyst named Tobias, Waymo is estimated to have approximately 3,000 vehicles driving roughly 4 million miles per week, while Tesla is estimated to have 300–400 vehicles in Austin and the Bay Area combined with roughly 1 million miles driven over the same comparison period — approximately 15 times fewer miles than Waymo. Despite this disparity, Tesla's incident count was zero versus Waymo's 25 in the most recent partial month (March 1–16). Larry notes this is still the worst autonomous driving will ever be, and that no company has yet demonstrated significant freeway mileage, which is where the most serious accidents occur.

4. **FSD Safety Holds Across All Players — With a Freeway Caveat:** Both host and guest agree that across Tesla, Waymo, and Zoox, all reported incidents have been minor fender benders — no hospitalizations, no airbag deployments, no serious injuries to humans. Larry frames this as proof that automated driving is already safer than human driving. However, he adds an important caveat: none of the current robotaxi operators are doing significant freeway mileage yet, and freeway accidents are unlikely to be fender benders. He describes all vendors as appropriately cautious about freeway deployment for this reason.

## Robotaxi

1. **Hardware 3 and Unsupervised FSD — Elon's Q3 2024 Commitment Revisited:** The video replays a clip from Tesla's Q3 2024 earnings call in which Elon Musk acknowledged there is "some chance" Hardware 3 cannot reach the safety level required for unsupervised FSD, and committed to free hardware upgrades for those customers who purchased Full Self-Driving. He also stated Tesla's priority is to achieve unsupervised FSD on Hardware 4 first, then address Hardware 3. Critically, on that same call, a Tesla representative stated that once the V14 release series is complete, Tesla plans to release a "V14 light version for Hardware 3," with an expected timeline of Q2 of the following year — which, given the Q3 2024 context, would place that target around Q2 2026.

2. **Larry's Theory — AI5 Chip as the Universal Upgrade Path:** Larry Goldberg raises a speculative but detailed hardware thesis: rather than engineering a Hardware 3 to AI4 conversion (which he notes is complicated by completely different connector sets), Tesla may find it more logical to design the AI5 computer with backward-compatible adapters for older vehicles in mind, since the AI5 hardware design has not yet been finalized. He suggests this could be a more elegant long-term solution, though it would add six to twelve months of wait time for Hardware 3 owners. He also notes that vehicles may eventually require AI5 for reasons beyond FSD — including what he calls "sentience," meaning in-vehicle intelligence, passenger recognition, and communication capabilities that go far beyond navigation.

3. **Timeline Expectation for Hardware 3 Light Version:** Despite the Q2 2026 target stated on the earnings call, Larry estimates the V14 light version for Hardware 3 is more realistically three to five months away from the time of the video (April 2026), potentially arriving toward the end of 2026. He expresses increased confidence in this timeline due to the architectural rewrite in version 14.3. A hardware upgrade kit, if pursued, might not arrive until the following year (2027) at the earliest.

4. **Tesla Has 11 Unsupervised Robotaxis in Operation:** The video mentions that Tesla currently has 11 robotaxis operating without a safety monitor, with the remainder of its fleet in Austin and the Bay Area requiring a safety monitor in the driver's seat. This limited unsupervised fleet is flagged as a key reason why direct safety comparisons to Waymo remain difficult.

## Market & Competition

1. **Uber's $10 Billion Strategic Pivot to Owning Robotaxi Fleets:** According to a Financial Times report cited in the video, Uber is making a decisive $10 billion commitment to robotaxis, representing a fundamental shift away from its asset-light gig economy model. The breakdown is $7.5 billion for direct fleet purchases and $2.5 billion in equity stakes in vehicle partners. Named partners include Rivian (a reported deal of up to $1.25 billion for up to 50,000 vehicles), Lucid, and BYD. The fleet is planned across 28 cities. Larry Goldberg is sharply critical of this strategy, describing Rivian and Lucid as "functionally dead" companies that Uber must invest in simply to keep alive, and arguing that adding lidar hardware to already expensive third-party vehicles makes the unit economics unworkable versus a purpose-built, lidar-free vehicle like the Cybercab.

2. **The Uber-Tesla Partnership That Hasn't Happened:** The hosts note a recurring dynamic in which Uber's CEO consistently says in interviews that Tesla won't engage with them on a partnership, while Tesla has publicly stated it is open to selling Cybercabs to fleet operators. Larry and Herbert agree the logical move for Uber would be to simply purchase Cybercabs once they are available for sale — which Elon has suggested could happen by end of 2026. Larry frames the absence of this conversation as a strategic failure on Uber's part, questioning why Uber hasn't taken this route instead of committing billions to partnerships with struggling manufacturers.

3. **ARK Invest's $3.9 Trillion US Robotaxi Market Projection:** ARK Invest analyst Brett Winton is cited as projecting that the US robotaxi market could reach $3.9 trillion annually at equilibrium, based on users simply trading the cost of vehicle ownership for robotaxi fares — even without paying more than their current marginal cost of transport. A second, lower estimate of $2.8 trillion is given for the scenario where users only substitute trips they are currently taking. Larry elaborates that there are two compounding growth drivers: people choosing to forgo car ownership entirely, and entirely new trips being taken because robotaxi transport is cheaper and more productive. He specifically cites short-haul flights being replaced by robotaxi rides as one example of new demand creation.
