## Autonomous Driving

1. **Elon Musk's Safety Statements and FSD Statistics:** Elon Musk posted publicly that "the statistics are unequivocal" on Tesla self-driving saving lives, citing both Waymo and Tesla as examples. According to Grok's response to a query Elon shared, Tesla's latest vehicle safety report (April 2026) shows FSD Supervised is seven times safer than the average U.S. human driver — one crash every 5.5 to 7 million miles versus one crash every 660,000 miles for human drivers — based on over 9 billion miles of data. Grok rated FSD Supervised at 8.5 out of 10 on an autonomy safety scale.

2. **FSD v14.3 Rollout as the Key Catalyst:** The hosts identify FSD v14.3 as the critical near-term catalyst for the robotaxi thesis. Elon had suggested the release would come before a prior Sunday, but it did not arrive on that timeline. Some Tesla employees reportedly received the update based on screenshots shared publicly. Joe states high confidence that the wide rollout will occur within April 2026, and argues that Tesla has been deliberately holding back wide robotaxi deployment until v14.3 is ready — reasoning that it would make no sense to expand the fleet to thousands of cars just before a major software version jump.

3. **FSD Supervised vs. Unsupervised — Operational Differences:** The hosts discuss how Waymo's remote monitoring is not a person watching live feeds at all times, but rather humans who are called in when a vehicle encounters a stuck or ambiguous situation. They agree that truly dangerous situations — requiring microsecond reaction times — cannot practically involve human remote intervention, meaning the vehicle must handle them autonomously. Joe also notes that Tesla's robotaxi system likely includes additional software components beyond standard FSD for tasks like pickup/dropoff sequencing and risk triage.

4. **Liability and Legal Risk Acknowledged by Elon:** Elon agreed with a post stating Tesla will eventually reach a point of saving 99% of lives that could have been lost in accidents, while acknowledging Tesla will still face lawsuits from the remaining fatalities. He framed this as an accepted cost of deploying the technology at scale, given the broader societal benefit. Insurance premiums are expected to fall significantly as a downstream effect.

## Robotaxi

1. **Fleet Staging in Phoenix and West Los Angeles:** The host reports that 60 Model Y vehicles with rear camera washers were found staged in a lot in Phoenix, and another 30 were found in a lot in West Los Angeles. The rear camera washer is identified as a distinguishing hardware feature exclusive to robotaxi-configured vehicles — not available on consumer Model Y purchases. The Phoenix vehicles also carry Texas license plates, indicating they were manufactured at Giga Texas and shipped to Phoenix. The host's interpretation is that these staged vehicles are being prepared for robotaxi launches.

2. **Robotaxi Groundwork in at Least 25 Cities:** Citing a tracking table built by Jonathan Stokes (updated as of April 6, 2026, using AI), the hosts note that Tesla's robotaxi program appears to be laying groundwork in at least 25 cities across the U.S. Cities specifically mentioned include Phoenix, Mesa (Arizona), Los Angeles, San Diego, Denver, Miami, Chicago, Las Vegas, New York, Dallas, San Antonio, Houston, Washington D.C., and Seattle. The table tracks metrics including permit status, vehicle operator job ads, fleet support ads, regulatory approvals, Cybercab sightings with lidar setups, robotaxi app access, public test programs, and geofenced areas.

3. **Current Fleet Count and Deployment Status:** The most recent community-tracked fleet count stands at 555 total vehicles, including inactive units, with only 9 running unsupervised. The Bay Area fleet is roughly seven times larger than Austin's in absolute terms, mirroring the population ratio of approximately 7 million to 1 million. The Austin fleet appears to be flatlining at around 100 vehicles, leading Joe to speculate that this may represent the natural saturation level for a supervised commercial operation at the current pricing and staffing model, and that the real scaling signal will come from unsupervised deployments ticking upward.

4. **Cybercab Deployment Status in Austin:** The host reports that a fleet of roughly 30 Cybercabs has been observed driving in Austin, with a human in the vehicle. The Cybercabs spotted have no side mirrors. There has been no confirmed video of a Cybercab carrying a paying passenger as of the video date. Joe emphasizes that Cybercab unsupervised deployment — not supervised Model Y service expansion — is the critical metric he is watching, and that Cybercab being on the road is his most reliable indicator that a meaningful scale-up is imminent.

5. **Milestone Timeline — April/May 2026 as Make-or-Break:** Joe maintains his prior forecast that unsupervised robotaxi scaling begins in April or May 2026, and that Cybercab unsupervised deployment is the key signal. He states that if no Cybercabs are deployed by end of May, the timing thesis will need to be reassessed. He also reaffirms confidence that Tesla can hit its earlier-stated goal of seven cities with supervised service by the first half of 2026, given the visible fleet staging already underway.
