This video covers Tesla's accelerating progress on the Cyber Cab robotaxi, competitive responses from Waymo and Uber, and other Tesla developments. Host Herbert and guest Joe Bacti, an economist and tech entrepreneur, discuss what these developments mean for Tesla investors.

Key Points:

1. Cyber Cab spotted on public roads in Palo Alto, California for the first time, with no steering wheel or side mirrors — previously sightings had been limited to Giga Texas.

2. Influencer Joe Techmire received a private tour of Giga Texas and confirmed hundreds of Cyber Cabs inside the factory, that production is already underway, and that the "unboxed" manufacturing method (five main structural components assembled separately) is being used.

3. The robotaxi tracker shows approximately 31 Cyber Cabs in deployment and 8 unsupervised units, with the count on a steep upward trajectory. Volume production is targeted for April 2026.

4. Joe Bacti argues that Cyber Cab production itself is not the bottleneck — the real question is whether Tesla's FSD software is ready for unsupervised deployment at scale. He outlines three investor scenarios: smooth April launch, dramatic delay followed by eventual deployment with a stock dip to around $200 before a major recovery, or a deeply depressing long-term delay.

5. Waymo submitted a proposal to California's DMV to ban Tesla from using the words "driverless," "self-driving," or "robo taxi" in marketing. This follows a December ruling that previously forced Tesla to drop "autopilot" and "full self-driving" terminology. Tesla simply changed its marketing language to comply and has now filed a lawsuit against the California DMV to reverse the original ruling.

6. A narrative claiming Tesla has four times more crashes than human drivers circulated widely, based on five January incidents. The hosts dispute this characterization, noting all five were minor fender-benders at under 4 mph, one involving a stationary Tesla struck by a bus.

7. Uber announced "Uber Autonomous Solutions," a comprehensive service suite for AV partners including training data collection via a Lucid vehicle fleet, data-enriched mapping, regulatory support, fleet financing, and remote operations. The hosts view this as a desperate strategy entirely dependent on Tesla failing, as Tesla robotaxi scaling would likely devastate Uber's business model.

8. Tesla is hiring Optimus motor and actuator design engineers in Athens, Greece, signaling continued Optimus robot development expansion.

9. The Cybertruck at a new $59,000 price point sold out through September within roughly one day of announcement, pushing new order delivery estimates out three to four months. This is interpreted as a demand elasticity test, with the possibility of future cost reductions using 4680 batteries enabling further price cuts.

Key Takeaway: Both Waymo and Uber's moves are characterized as defensive reactions to Tesla's robotaxi threat. The hosts believe production is clearly on track, but unsupervised FSD deployment readiness remains the critical and uncertain variable for 2026.
