A Tesla Model X owner driving the length of the Americas ran out of battery in Chile's Atacama Desert, deploying emergency solar panels to trickle-charge the vehicle while stranded on the Pan-American Highway. The incident illustrates both the creative resilience possible with EVs and the persistent infrastructure gaps in South America's charging network.

## Electric Vehicles
1. **Overlanding Tesla Model X runs dry in the Atacama:** Sandro van Kuijck (YouTube channel EverydaySandro) has spent three years driving his modified Model X, "Beluga," from Tuktoyuktuk, Canada toward Ushuaia, Argentina. The vehicle is equipped with a custom slide-out kitchen, refrigerator, sleeping setup, and a 287-watt solar array on the hood feeding a 2 kWh EcoFlow Delta 2 house battery. South of Calama, Chile, after charging to 95% at a Copec fast charger, the climb to 3,000 meters and desert headwinds left him with 37 km of range and 42 km to the nearest charger.

2. **Solar trickle and improvised rescue:** Parked on the highway shoulder, the solar array delivered 180–200 watts, adding roughly 1–2 km of range per hour — enough to prevent the high-voltage battery from fully shutting down but not enough to reach the charger. Five tow companies declined the 30 km call-out. A nearby road construction crew allowed him to plug into their industrial generator at 6 amps. Friends eventually arranged a tow to Calama for $135, where a Copec charger delivered 36–40 kW and had him back on the road within two hours.

## Market & Competition
1. **Chile's charging infrastructure remains sparse outside Santiago:** Tesla launched its Supercharger network in Chile in late 2024 — its first South American entry — with coverage concentrated around Santiago and major corridors. In early 2026, Tesla and Copec announced a partnership to deploy additional Supercharger stations at Copec service stations, each featuring four charging points at up to 250 kW. Copec itself operates over 90 fast-charging stations nationwide, but long stretches of the Atacama highway remain effectively charging deserts. A compounding issue: van Kuijck's Tesla navigation still registered him as being in Mexico and could not locate Chilean chargers. Chile has set a target of electric-only new vehicle sales by 2035, but long-distance infrastructure lags well behind that goal.

## Editorial Stance
Author Fred Lambert argues the story is uniquely compelling because solar self-rescue is only possible with an EV, framing the panel as a legitimate emergency backup — not a practical charging solution — while noting that additional deployable panels could have allowed van Kuijck to escape the situation on solar power alone.
