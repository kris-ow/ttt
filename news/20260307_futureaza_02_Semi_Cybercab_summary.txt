This is episode 136 of the Tesla Beat podcast, hosted by the futureaza channel, featuring co-hosts Joe, Hans, and Brian (joining from Guangzhou, China). The episode covers Tesla Semi and Cybercab production developments, FSD global expansion, the competitive EV landscape, and a farewell to regular co-host Hans. The panel's overarching theme is Tesla's widening engineering and strategic lead over legacy automakers and newer EV entrants.

1. Tesla Semi factory tour by Ashley Vance. Journalist and Elon Musk biographer Ashley Vance received an exclusive, fully filmed tour of the Tesla Semi production line, led by what the hosts believe is Dan Priestley, senior director or VP of the Semi program. The new Semi has a redesigned exterior that aligns with the Model Y, Cybertruck, and Cybercab aesthetic — hosts note that the old design looked great until they saw the new one. Tesla is building a dedicated factory for the Semi, and existing first-generation Semis are actively being used for logistics at and around the Fremont factory, with units also spotted in the wild with Height Logistics, a California-based port drayage and trucking company operating in CA, AZ, NV, and UT with 25 e-trucks.

2. Cybercab production ramp is underway. Reporter Joe Tagmire documented 25 Cybercabs on site at Giga Texas, which have since been dispersed: 15 to crash testing, 4 at a factory exit point, and the rest loaded onto covered transports, likely heading to California for further testing. The hosts emphasize this is not yet about maximizing output volume — it's about getting units to testing locations and building confidence before scaling. Austin is expected to be the first meaningful deployment city.

3. Cybercab fleet composition and modular rumors. A report from MDR floated the idea of a 5-seater, 7-seater, and 6-seater Cybercab variant for the robo taxi network. Brian, speaking from China, pushed back firmly, noting the Cybercab platform is too narrow for a large-scale bus configuration and that Tesla historically focuses on perfecting one product before expanding. The more credible interpretation offered by Hans is that "5-seater" variants are likely stripped Model Y or Model Y L units with steering wheels and pedals removed, purpose-built for the robo taxi fleet, probably in rear-wheel-drive trim to reduce cost.

4. Ford CEO Jim Farley admits EV platform mistakes. The panel discussed Farley's public admission that Ford should have built a dedicated EV platform from the ground up rather than adapting existing architecture. The bombshell comparison cited: the Mustang Mach-E's wiring harness was 70 lbs heavier and 1.6 km longer than Tesla's. Brian's pointed observation: Ford is currently trying to copy where Tesla is today, meaning in three years Ford might catch up to where Tesla was six months ago. Hans argued Tesla's real moat is the hardware deployed at scale in the real world, and that the Cybertruck is already positioned to undercut Ford and GM's F-150 and Silverado EV cost of goods sold to consumers, eventually threatening their ICE trucks as well.

5. Rivian faces a structurally difficult future. Hans argued Rivian is making similar mistakes to Ford, just with less legacy overhead. Key concern: Rivian has never been profitable, is planning to sell its software suite for $2,500 (which costs more to develop), has a take rate well below 100%, and is adding a lidar sensor to the R2 that Brian estimates will cost hundreds of millions across a million-unit production run — hardware he views as wasted spend. The panel agreed Rivian's most realistic exit is acquisition by a company like Google or Apple that can absorb losses and build on the platform.

6. FSD performing well in the Netherlands, European expansion building. A user reported a smooth 30-minute FSD-supervised ride through chaotic city center traffic in the Netherlands — bikes, intersections, and all. Tesla is also posting job listings at Giga Berlin for electrical design engineers for robo taxi and autonomous vehicles, and a March 26–28 date was mentioned as a potential window for initial FSD regulatory approvals in the Netherlands. The EU approval process was noted as extremely burdensome, with Tesla having to produce massive documentation that, in the hosts' view, nobody reads.

7. Vision-only autonomy vindicated globally. The hosts noted that a major Chinese EV manufacturer switched to vision-only autonomy approximately three years ago after reaching the same conclusions as Tesla — that lidar and radar introduce noise rather than improve performance. The panel sees this as historical validation that Tesla's controversial bet on cameras-only was correct, and that competitors are still spending hardware costs on sensors that will likely be deprecated.

8. Tesla leads 2026 EV supply chain sustainability scorecard. Tesla topped the "Lead the Charge" auto EV supply chain scorecard for the second consecutive year, increasing its overall score by 6 percentage points versus Ford's 2-point gain, now nearly 5 percentage points ahead. The hosts expressed mild surprise the gap wasn't larger given Tesla's streamlined product lineup compared to Ford's dozens of F-150 variants, and Hans noted the methodology may be measuring ESG sustainability rather than supply chain resilience per se.

9. Norway ICE vehicle sales: 12 units in February. Norway recorded only 12 petrol car purchases in February — not 12,000, twelve — which the panel celebrated as a signal of where EV adoption can lead when a market reaches maturity.

10. Farewell to co-host Hans. The episode marked Hans's departure as a regular co-host. He plans to shift to a building/development phase, likely involving AI and compute infrastructure. He can be found on X at @HansCNelson. His parting advice: "Go build stuff."
