This video from the "brighterwithherbert" channel features host Herbert and guest Jo Bhakdi — an economist, investor, and tech entrepreneur — discussing Tesla's robotaxi rollout trajectory, the significance of Tesla's hiring activity across 34 U.S. cities, and what FSD software progress means for the timeline to unsupervised scaling. The core thesis is that while the rollout is proceeding more slowly than originally hoped, the aggregate signals — Cybercab production ramp, data collection supervisor hiring across 34 cities, FSD version progress, and shifting institutional sentiment — point to an accelerating, nationwide infrastructure buildout that could surprise to the upside.

## Robotaxi

1. **Tesla hiring self-driving supervisors in 34 cities:** Tesla is actively hiring self-driving data collection supervisors across 34 U.S. cities, with day, night, and graveyard shift positions. The job posting describes "high-intensity, action-oriented leaders" to accelerate vehicle-level testing, coordinate local operations, and assist with troubleshooting and incident reporting. According to the hosts, 46% of the U.S. population would be covered by these markets, and 28 out of 34 of the targeted cities overlap with Waymo's future deployment plans. The states mentioned include Louisiana, Texas, Illinois, Kansas, New York, New Jersey, Nevada, and Washington, among others.

2. **Cybercab production ramp as a key indicator:** Jo Bhakdi points to drone footage published by Joe Tegtmeyer showing dozens of Cybercabs emerging from the factory every single day as a key secondary indicator of Tesla's momentum and confidence. Combined with Cybercab sightings across the nation and the aggressive hiring activity, he interprets this as Tesla committing serious capital and management resources to laying infrastructure across what he calls "a giant board" representing the U.S. and eventually Europe.

3. **Current fleet status and growth pace:** As of the video, there are 573 vehicles available in Tesla's supervised robotaxi tracker and 11 unsupervised robotaxis operating. The hosts acknowledge growth is occurring "one by one, blip by blip" and that Tesla's earlier guidance — announced at the January earnings call — of supervised robotaxi doubling every month across seven cities has not materialized on schedule. However, they frame the current slow pace as a precursor to a faster scaling phase, not a sign of failure.

4. **Jo Bhakdi revises his forcing-function thesis:** Jo Bhakdi had previously argued that 1,800–2,000 unsupervised robotaxis was the critical "forcing function" because, based on his model, that number represents approximately 5% of Tesla's annualized free cash flow — a level significant enough to compel even skeptical analysts to acknowledge the business. He now believes that given the improved market sentiment, even scaling from 11 to 22 to 50 to 100 unsupervised vehicles with consistent month-over-month growth could be sufficient to trigger a major repricing of the stock, without needing to wait for 1,800.

5. **Rollout expected to be a grind, not a switch:** Both hosts align on the view that the rollout will resemble Waymo's — a systematic, gradual expansion — but at a faster pace. Jo Bhakdi explicitly walks back any expectation of a binary "flip the switch" moment, instead predicting an "accelerated grind" where supervised and unsupervised robotaxis scale together across cities in H2 2026, with many cities potentially coming online even in H1 2026. He suggests unsupervised scaling could go from 11 to 20, then to 40, then 80, with version 15 potentially enabling a larger step-change.

6. **Deutsche Bank analyst tests robotaxi in Austin:** Edison U of Deutsche Bank tested Tesla's Model Y robotaxi in Austin, Texas. His experience included no disengagements and no issues; the vehicle handled dense traffic merges, lane change signals, and a truck cutting into the lane. A safety monitor was present in the front passenger seat but did not intervene once. His sole complaint was routing logic — the vehicle opted for a significant detour to avoid a route it was not yet authorized to use, turning a 20-minute trip into nearly 40 minutes. Deutsche Bank noted this issue is expected to resolve as operating parameters expand.

7. **Cantor Fitzgerald turns more bullish:** Cantor Fitzgerald is cited as one of the institutional analysts shifting sentiment positively, specifically citing the EU FSD approval milestone in the Netherlands and Tesla's expansion plans as supporting their more bullish outlook.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD approved in the Netherlands — EU-wide expansion likely:** FSD has received regulatory approval in the Netherlands, marking the first European regulatory approval. The general manager of the RDW (Netherlands' vehicle authority) has already submitted to EU member states recommending they adopt the approval as well. Ashok and others have confirmed this decision is expected to influence not just the 27 EU member states but many other countries globally. The hosts expect this could go wider by the end of summer 2026.

2. **FSD version 14.3 — 20% reaction time improvement:** FSD version 14.3 is described as a major overhaul, with the headline capability being a 20% improvement in reaction time. The hosts reference a detailed video by a creator called "AI Drivers" demonstrating this improvement: the car detected a parked car beginning to move into the lane and braked preemptively, then detected a yellow traffic light and began slowing within 0.2 seconds — before the in-car display even registered the yellow light. The average human reaction time to a yellow light is cited as 1.5 seconds. Version 14.3 has not yet gone to wide release as of the video, though the hosts say community response is universally positive and it is expected to roll out broadly soon.

3. **FSD version in Europe equivalent to 14.3:** Elon Musk confirmed that the version deployed in Europe, labeled version 14.2.2.5 due to different version numbering conventions, is functionally equivalent to version 14.3. A European user named Keith Roland Chop, based in Amsterdam, had achieved a 672 km FSD streak over 2 days. The hosts suggest European users may actually be receiving the latest version ahead of some U.S. users.

4. **FSD version 15 expected within approximately two months:** Jo Bhakdi believes FSD version 15 is the minimum threshold needed for real-scale unsupervised deployment, characterizing it as a "10x version." Based on the historical cadence of major version number releases, he and others have estimated version 15 could arrive in approximately two months from the video's date. He does not view this as a hard blocker for incremental scaling in the interim — the current version could still support modest expansion to 20–30 unsupervised vehicles — but sees version 15 as the catalyst for a more aggressive ramp.

5. **Safety incident tracking — five incidents then plateau:** The hosts note that between December 15 and January 15, there were five safety incidents in the robotaxi program. Between January 15 and February 15, there was one incident, described as just a fender bender. As of the video date, no additional incidents had been publicly reported, though the hosts note the next monthly data point would be available within a few days.

6. **Regulatory landscape favors Tesla in most U.S. states:** Jo Bhakdi argues the regulatory environment for robotaxis is widely misunderstood. Most U.S. states outside of New York and California have no specific autonomous vehicle regulations — he describes states like Kansas, Georgia, and Illinois as effectively saying "put it on the road, just don't kill anyone." He estimates Tesla could reach approximately 160 million Americans without navigating significant regulatory hurdles, framing regulation as a secondary concern relative to technology maturity.

## Market & Competition

1. **Institutional sentiment shifting in Tesla's favor:** The hosts observe a broader shift in institutional analyst sentiment toward Tesla, driven by three concurrent signals: the EU FSD approval, FSD version 14.3's performance capabilities, and the visible Cybercab production ramp. They argue this sentiment shift means the stock does not need to wait for the full 1,800-vehicle forcing function to see meaningful repricing — any sustained month-over-month growth in unsupervised vehicles could be enough to trigger a 10–20% or larger rally.

2. **Waymo as the comparison benchmark — Tesla expected to be faster:** Waymo is repeatedly used as the benchmark comparison. The hosts note Waymo has had cars with lidar testing in Seattle for approximately 10 years, cautioning that data collection hiring activity alone does not guarantee near-term rollout. However, they expect Tesla's rollout — once it begins scaling — to move significantly faster than Waymo's due to Tesla's camera-based, software-driven approach and the much larger fleet baseline.
