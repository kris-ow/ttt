This video features host Herbert in conversation with Cern Basher, a Chartered Financial Analyst and founder of the investment advisory firm Brilliant Advice, who walks through a century of U.S. stock market data to argue that wealth creation is radically concentrated among a tiny number of companies — and that AI is accelerating that concentration. The core thesis is that investors must identify the "operating systems of civilization," and that Tesla, alongside a handful of other mega-platforms, is positioned to be one of the defining wealth creators of the next era. The second half of the conversation broadens to SpaceX, the potential Tesla-SpaceX merger, and why both companies could dominate Earth and space respectively.

## Market & Competition

1. **Century of stock market data reveals extreme wealth concentration:** Drawing on academic research by Hendrik Bessembinder of Arizona State University covering 29,754 listed stocks from 1926 through December 2025, Cern Basher highlights that the median stock has a lifetime return of -6.9%. Only 48% of stocks delivered positive lifetime returns, only 41% outperformed Treasury bills, and only 27.6% outperformed the market itself. The data shows that 100% of all U.S. stock market wealth was created by just 1,082 companies (3.6% of the total), while 50% of all wealth was created by just 46 companies (0.15% of the total), and roughly 29% by just 10 companies (0.03% of the total).

2. **Concentration is accelerating, driven by AI and platform dynamics:** Over the first 90 years of the study (1926–2016), it took 89 companies to account for 50% of net wealth creation. Over the last 10 years alone, it took only 13 companies to account for 50%. Similarly, in the first 90 years, 30 stocks accounted for 31% of wealth created, but in the last 10 years, 30 stocks accounted for 61% of wealth created. Cern Basher attributes this narrowing to scale advantages, network effects, technology platform dynamics, proprietary data moats, and increasingly to artificial intelligence — which favors companies that can deploy massive capital early to build dominant infrastructure.

3. **Tesla ranked 9th in total lifetime wealth creation among all U.S. stocks:** According to Bessembinder's data, Tesla accounts for approximately 1.5% of all shareholder wealth created in U.S. stock market history since its IPO in June 2010 — making it the 9th largest wealth creator of all time despite being one of the youngest companies on the list. Apple leads at approximately 5.5%, followed by Nvidia at approximately 5%, and Microsoft at approximately 4.5%. On a wealth-created-per-year basis (adjusting for listing age), Tesla has generated approximately $84 billion per year in shareholder wealth since its IPO, placing it 7th on that adjusted ranking.

4. **Tesla is the 5th best compounding stock over the last decade:** Filtering for companies with at least 10 years of public market history, Tesla ranks 5th on compound annual return since IPO, compounding at nearly 44% per year through December 2025. Nvidia, which has been public longer, compounded at approximately 37% per year over the same study period. Cern Basher notes that Broadcom also appears on this list, three spots below Tesla.

5. **"Operating system of civilization" framework for identifying future winners:** Cern Basher argues that the companies most likely to generate outsized future returns share specific characteristics: they control critical infrastructure, they are capital-efficient at scale with near-zero marginal costs once built, they possess proprietary and self-compounding data moats, they continuously expand their total addressable market (TAM), and they function as "optionality machines" that can layer new businesses on top of existing platforms. He applies this lens to Apple (mobile computing), Alphabet (search, advertising, AI), Amazon (cloud, logistics, commerce), Nvidia (AI compute), Visa/Mastercard (payments), and explicitly includes Tesla and SpaceX.

6. **S&P 500 index inclusion risk highlighted via Tesla's example:** Cern Basher warns that passive index investing carries concentration risk because index inclusion decisions lag behind actual wealth creation. Tesla is cited as a prime example where the S&P 500 added the stock well after a substantial portion of its wealth creation had already occurred, meaning passive S&P investors missed much of those gains. He notes that index providers are now proactively preparing for SpaceX's IPO to avoid repeating that mistake.

7. **AI expected to further concentrate wealth among a shrinking number of winners:** Both Cern Basher and Herbert argue that AI favors companies with the most compute, data, and capital — creating self-reinforcing advantages that are nearly impossible for smaller companies to overcome. They suggest that sector distinctions will blur as AI-native companies (Alphabet, Tesla, xAI) potentially enter industries like pharmaceuticals, law, and logistics, displacing incumbents who cannot compete on AI capability.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **Tesla's real-world driving data cited as a structural moat:** Cern Basher identifies Tesla's proprietary real-world driving data as one of the company's most defensible competitive advantages, analogous to Google's search data or Meta's social data. He argues that this data compounds in the same self-reinforcing way that capital does — more miles driven leads to better FSD, which attracts more users, which generates more data. He suggests that Tesla may be approaching a tipping point where FSD adoption becomes self-reinforcing, with consumers increasingly feeling they "have to have" the technology.

## Robotaxi

1. **Robotaxi described as Tesla's key near-zero marginal cost opportunity:** Cern Basher specifically names the robotaxi business as Tesla's clearest example of a platform that requires large upfront capital deployment but enjoys near-zero marginal costs at scale — the same structural dynamic that made Amazon Web Services and Google Search so profitable. Herbert notes that 2026 is the year Tesla is beginning to actually introduce the robotaxi business model, which he frames as the transition from a car company to a true AI-driven platform company.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus positioned as Tesla's entry into the global labor TAM:** Herbert frames humanoid labor as one of the largest total addressable markets in existence — comparable historically to energy — and identifies Optimus as Tesla's vehicle for capturing that market. Cern Basher adds that Tesla could even become a healthcare company through Optimus applications, illustrating how sector definitions will blur. Both host and guest reference "Digital Optimus" (software/AI labor) alongside physical Optimus as components of Tesla's expanding platform.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Tesla described as having outgrown its car company identity:** Both Cern Basher and Herbert emphasize that Tesla's appearance in the top 10 wealth creators of all time was achieved primarily as a car company, and that the market has only partially priced in its AI, robotaxi, and robotics potential. Cern Basher states that if Tesla were valued as a true tech platform company, it would rank alongside Nvidia and Alphabet in valuation — a threshold he believes has not yet been reached.

## Financials

1. **SpaceX valuation trajectory discussed in merger context:** Herbert and Cern Basher discuss a potential Tesla-SpaceX merger, noting that SpaceX has grown from a $400 billion valuation "last summer" to potentially $2 trillion or more. Herbert speculates that a SpaceX IPO could occur in June 2026, followed by a merger or acquisition announcement with Tesla later in 2026 or 2027. Cern Basher notes that any merger structure would require Tesla shareholder approval and would need to be financially attractive — either a merger of equals or a buyout at a premium — to secure sufficient votes. He references a separate conversation with Larry Goldberg, Jeff Lutz, and Alexandra Merz on this topic.
