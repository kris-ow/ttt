This is a live-streamed Monday news roundup from the Futurazza channel, hosted by Brian, covering over 20 headlines related to electric vehicles, Tesla, autonomous driving, and the broader EV industry. The tone is opinionated and conversational, with Brian frequently fact-checking media coverage he considers misleading or sensationalized. His overarching thesis is that EV adoption is accelerating globally despite negative press narratives in the US, and that American hesitancy represents a strategic mistake.

1. Tesla odometer class action lawsuit dismissed. A lawsuit alleging Tesla manipulated odometers to prematurely void warranties was thrown out. Brian explains the actual allegation was absurd: a driver expected 300 miles of range at highway speeds and received 200, then claimed the odometer must have overcounted — when in fact it was simply a range estimate discrepancy. Tesla was already offering longer warranties than legally required, undermining any motive for the alleged fraud.

2. Lucid Motors planning a robotaxi. Lucid is developing a midsize platform called Cosmos/Earth and wants to include a dedicated two-seater driverless robotaxi variant. Brian is supportive but skeptical, noting Lucid has not solved the autonomy software side, which he considers the hardest and most critical component. He sees value in competitors copying Tesla's model-based robotaxi approach.

3. Mercedes-Benz slashes $25,000 off Maybach EV. The Maybach EQS, starting around $90,000, is seeing massive discounts due to weak demand. Brian explains this reflects a structural problem for luxury EV makers: adding premium features adds weight and power draw, which reduces range, often to around 200 miles. The formula that works for luxury ICE vehicles doesn't translate cleanly to EVs without significantly larger batteries.

4. US drivers reconsidering EVs as gas prices spike. An Iran-related conflict is pushing gas prices up, with Brian observing over $5 per gallon in Portland. He argues this illustrates the core instability of petroleum as a commodity and notes that electricity prices, while not immune to increases, do not spike with the same speed or magnitude. He personally didn't notice the gas price increase because he no longer buys fuel.

5. EV slowdown narrative called "unAmerican" and factually wrong. Citing IEA data, Brian pushes back on the idea that EVs are stalling globally. EV sales grew 29% in 2024, reaching 14.2 million vehicles, representing 18% of all new cars sold worldwide. The IEA projects 17 million EV sales in 2025. China is approaching 50% EV market share, and Europe sold 3.2 million EVs last year, up 14%. Brian frames US hesitancy as a strategic error that cedes global automotive leadership for short-term profitability.

6. GM and the Chevy Equinox EV's uncertain future. An article highlighted that a GM product engineer gave a vague answer when asked whether the Equinox EV — described as the cheapest EV in America — would remain on sale longer than 18 months. Brian connects this to GM's history of introducing and then canceling EV products, calling it a pattern of "rug pulls."

7. Tesla's Terafab chip project announced for March 21st launch. Elon Musk stated that Tesla's in-house AI chip fabrication project, Terafab, would begin on March 21st. Brian is curious but cautious, noting chip manufacturing is dominated by TSMC, Samsung, and a handful of others, and that chip experts he has spoken to say there are missing pieces that would make Tesla's entry into fab extremely difficult. Land near the SpaceX facility in Bastrop, Texas is reportedly being cleared, possibly for this or a solar fab.

8. Tesla stock snapshot. At the time of recording, Tesla was trading above $400, up about 2% on the day. Year-to-date it was down 9%, one-month down 3%, six-month down 5.5%, but up 82% over five years and up approximately 31,000% from its all-time low.

9. CyberCab "no steering wheel is illegal" article debunked. Brian strongly criticizes a piece by David Lazarus claiming the CyberCab is illegal because federal safety rules require steering wheels, pedals, and mirrors. Brian calls this factually wrong, noting the federal regulatory framework has already been adapted to accommodate autonomous vehicles, and that NHTSA has issued exemptions for exactly these configurations. He compares the CyberCab to a regular taxi — a car a passenger cannot drive anyway.

10. "Former Tesla executive shocked by Chinese EV cost savings" — a nothing story. Brian examined this headline and found the article contained no actual Tesla executive, no specific quote, and no real finding. The entire substance amounted to: cheaper EVs are better for adoption. He holds it up as an example of misleading clickbait headlines.

11. BYD's 1.5 megawatt flash charging. BYD has deployed a vehicle capable of charging from 10% to 97% in 9 minutes using 1.5 megawatt flash chargers, which are already in commercial deployment, not just in a lab. The vehicle costs approximately $22,000 USD. Brian acknowledges this is impressive and fills a real need for some drivers, while clarifying Tesla has not been the speed leader in charging for 3 to 4 years — the Supercharger network's advantage is ease of use and reliability, not peak speed.

12. China now leading in EV safety regulation. Contrary to narratives that Chinese EVs are dangerous or unregulated, Brian notes China now mandates fireproof batteries with a minimum 30-minute thermal runaway window and has established door latch standards. He calls it "embarrassing" that the US is ceding regulatory leadership in EV safety to China.

13. BYD in Brazil securing 100,000 vehicle orders from Argentina and Mexico. Mexico imposed a 35% tariff on Chinese EVs to protect its automotive partners, but BYD's Brazilian operation is not subject to that tariff. The BYD Seal is priced approximately one-third less than a Tesla Model 3, which Brian describes as a compelling value proposition.

14. Honda facing first loss in 70 years due to EV write-downs. Honda is stepping back from EV commitments and reportedly facing its first net loss in seven decades, partially attributed to write-downs on EV investments. Brian ran into a YouTube ad for the Honda Aila EV, a model that had already been cancelled, calling the continued ad spend baffling.

15. Tesla Model Y three-row (YL/Juniper) opens orders in Australia and Korea. Pricing starts at 749,000 Australian dollars (approximately $54,000–$58,000 USD), about 13% more than the current dual-motor long-range Model Y. Brian notes this tracks with China pricing at roughly 8% above the long-range Model Y and describes it as the most affordable three-row electric SUV currently available. He also mentions Australia is getting vehicle-to-load functionality.

16. Tesla Semi still in pilot program phase. The Semi, first revealed in 2017, remains in limited pilot programs with trucking companies rather than scaling commercially. Brian defends this pace, explaining that trucking companies must independently verify range claims, reliability, and operational continuity before committing fleets. Moon Transport entering a pilot was cited as the latest example.

17. Tesla Australian graphite supplier contract dispute extended again. Sierra Resources and Tesla have pushed a resolution deadline to June 1st for a cathode material supply dispute. Brian considers it unlikely to be a major issue and expects resolution.

18. Rivian R2 vs. Tesla Model Y comparison. Brian notes the comparison is somewhat apples-to-oranges: the R2 has about 3.5 inches more ground clearance and more total storage. Pricing overlaps between $41,000 and $46,000 depending on configuration. Brian's main practical concern about the R1S he tested was the lack of a native NACS plug, which he called a dealbreaker. The R2 will include native NACS.

19. 440,000-mile Tesla Model S battery longevity story. A 440,000-mile Model S needed its first battery replacement at 413,000 miles. Brian uses this to counter the narrative that battery replacement costs will financially destroy EV owners, arguing that at those mileages, ICE vehicle engines and transmissions typically fail outright with no economic case for replacement regardless of cost.
