Tesla faces exposure of up to $14.5 billion across at least 21 active litigation tracks spanning seven legal categories, according to an analysis compiled by Electrek. The article argues the legal situation is accelerating rather than stabilizing, with the most consequential lawsuits still ahead.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **Benavides verdict reshapes litigation strategy:** A Miami federal jury in August 2025 found Tesla 33% liable for a fatal 2019 Autopilot crash and awarded $243 million in damages, including $200 million in punitive. Tesla had rejected a $60 million pre-trial settlement. Judge Beth Bloom rejected Tesla's appellate arguments in February 2026. Since the verdict, Tesla has quietly settled at least four additional Autopilot crash lawsuits. The article estimates 50–60 fatal crashes involving Autopilot or FSD are documented in NHTSA reports and TeslaDeaths.com, putting aggregate crash-related exposure at $1 billion to $5 billion — with hundreds of additional non-fatal suits also in the pipeline.

2. **FSD marketing class action and "all hardware needed" fraud:** A California federal judge certified a class action in August 2025 covering buyers of Enhanced Autopilot or FSD packages dating back to October 2016, with a "full-refund theory" potentially worth hundreds of millions. Separately, Musk admitted on an earnings call in January 2025 that HW3 computers — installed in roughly 4 million vehicles — would need physical replacement to achieve promised self-driving capability. As of publication, Tesla has no hardware retrofit program, refund policy, or timeline for HW3 owners. A "v14 Lite" software variant promised for Q2 2026 has not materialized. A December 2025 California DMV ruling found Tesla's use of "Full Self-Driving" is "actually, unambiguously false."

3. **NHTSA investigations escalating:** An active NHTSA investigation covers 2.88 million vehicles for FSD-related traffic violations — running red lights, wrong-way driving, and entering incorrect lanes. A separate engineering analysis (the stage preceding a mandatory recall) covers 3.2 million vehicles for FSD performance in reduced visibility conditions. Tesla is also under investigation for allegedly reporting FSD crashes months late.

## Robotaxi
1. **Securities fraud class action tied to robotaxi launch failures:** Morand v. Tesla, filed in August 2025, alleges Tesla and Musk inflated the stock price by concealing self-driving risks related to the robotaxi program. The class period covers April 2023 to June 2025. The trigger was Tesla's public robotaxi tests in late June 2025, during which vehicles reportedly sped, braked suddenly, drove over curbs, entered wrong lanes, and dropped passengers in the middle of multi-lane roads. Tesla's share price fell 6.1% over two trading days, erasing approximately $68 billion in market cap. Settlement exposure is estimated at $1 billion to $5 billion if the class is certified.

## Financials
1. **Aggregate exposure and cash position:** Total litigation exposure ranges from a conservative $2.7 billion to $14.5 billion across all active tracks. Tesla holds approximately $40 billion in cash, meaning even the high-end estimate does not threaten solvency. However, the article notes the company is already facing declining sales and earnings approaching negative territory, making even a realistic $3–5 billion liability a meaningful drag.

## Electric Vehicles
1. **Fremont factory racial discrimination and workplace lawsuits:** Plaintiffs' attorneys have filed over 500 individual racial discrimination lawsuits against Tesla stemming from Fremont factory conditions, with plans to file more than 900 total. At estimated settlements of $500,000 to $2 million per plaintiff, aggregate exposure reaches $200 million to $1.2 billion. The EEOC's separate lawsuit over systematic racial harassment is in mediation with a June 2026 deadline. Additional suits include at least six sexual harassment claims and a $51 million suit from a technician injured by a factory robot. Consumer fraud suits cover range inflation algorithms and alleged odometer manipulation. Antitrust/right-to-repair exposure with treble damages is estimated at $100 million to $500 million.

## Editorial Stance
Electrek argues Tesla's "corporate puffery" legal defense was always embarrassing and is now demonstrably failing, and characterizes the HW3 situation as a "pattern of deception" that affected millions of purchasing decisions. The author contends the current litigation wave reflects the early 2018–2020 FSD era and that a larger second wave from the 2021–2025 mass FSD deployment has barely begun.
