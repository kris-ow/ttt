Futurazza host Brian delivers a critical analysis of Finnish startup Donut Labs and their claims of having developed the world's first production-ready all-solid-state battery for electric vehicles. The video examines new independent lab results released by Donut Labs, arguing that those results validate almost none of the company's most extraordinary claims and may actually suggest the tested cell is a conventional lithium NMC battery in disguise. Brian draws on expert commentary and technical journalism to make the case that Donut Labs is either incompetent, deceptive, or both.

1. Donut Labs claims to have beaten Toyota, BYD, and CATL in the solid-state battery race. The Finnish startup has no proven track record in battery manufacturing, a small team, and limited funding compared to the major players who have worked on solid-state technology for decades. The claim that a startup of this size achieved what those giants could not is treated as a foundational red flag throughout the video.

2. The company's full list of claimed specifications is implausibly comprehensive. Donut Labs says their battery charges in 5 minutes, lasts 100,000 cycles, delivers 400 Wh/kg energy density, is immune from thermal runaway, uses no conflict or precious materials, and is available at low cost. Brian's core argument is that claiming to have simultaneously solved every known battery problem — cost, speed, longevity, safety, and materials — is not credible, especially from a small startup.

3. VTT, Finland's state-owned technical research center, conducted the independent tests — but was only asked to test fast-charging performance. VTT tested a single 94 Wh pouch cell across seven charging sessions. Critically, VTT was not asked to verify whether the cell was actually solid state, was not asked to examine the chemistry, did not measure the cell's weight or dimensions, and performed fewer than a dozen charging cycles rather than anything approaching the claimed 100,000.

4. The fast-charging results, while real, are not exceptional by current standards. At 5C the cell reached 80% charge in under 10 minutes and 100% in about 13 minutes with a peak temperature of 47°C. At 11C it hit 80% in 4.6 minutes and 100% in under 8 minutes. Brian points out that BYD's new blade battery charges at 8C, and that high-performance NMC batteries available today already match this profile, meaning the results do not demonstrate anything beyond existing lithium battery capability.

5. One test had to be stopped prematurely due to overheating. In a single-sided heat-wicking test, the cell reached 90°C, exceeding safety parameters and causing VTT to halt the test early. This contradicts Donut Labs' claims of thermal runaway immunity and raises questions about real-world safety behavior under stress.

6. The charging profile looks electrically identical to an NMC lithium battery. Multiple experts cited in the video noted the charge curve is indistinguishable from a standard NMC cell. VTT's own report language states they tested "what the customer presented as a solid state battery" — explicitly declining to confirm it actually was one. Electronic Design characterized the cell as a potential "NMC Trojan horse."

7. Professor Shirley Mung (University of Chicago's Pritzker School of Molecular Engineering) stated that anyone who refuses to reveal chemistry "cannot be the real deal." She also noted that her own lab has demonstrated 20C charging at lab scale, underscoring that single-cell lab results mean nothing until translated to pack-level real-world performance — which Donut Labs has not demonstrated.

8. Basic measurements that would validate the energy density claims were never taken. Professor Andy Terrudic, an electrical engineering professor, is cited pointing out that the VTT report omits cell mass and dimensions — measurements a 10-year-old could perform. Without these, the headline claim of 400 Wh/kg cannot be evaluated at all. Tests were also run with the temperature chamber door cracked open, and no cell confinement or swelling measurements were made.

9. The company's founder has a prior history of extraordinary and undelivered claims. Brian references a previous video in which he covered the founder's history of claiming to have built the world's first "true artificial general intelligence" — a claim that similarly went nowhere. This pattern of overclaiming is presented as a character data point.

10. The product is advertised as available for purchase today, but cannot actually be purchased. The website stated the battery was commercially available and that it would be in motorcycles by April 1st. Brian plans a follow-up video on that date. He predicts the goalposts will shift — the company will claim delivery to an in-house affiliated motorcycle company and offer reservations to outside customers while never actually delivering product to independent buyers.

Brian's conclusion is that the odds of Donut Labs having a genuine breakthrough are "desperately unlikely" but not mathematically zero. He allows for the possibilities of fraud, sincere incompetence, or delusional overconfidence, but says none of those scenarios changes the outcome for investors or the public. He frames the pattern as one repeated dozens of times over the past 20 years in battery technology hype cycles.
