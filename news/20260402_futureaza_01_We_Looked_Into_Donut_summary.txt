This video features a conversation between host Brian of Futurazza and Jordan Gizigi from The Limiting Factor, a battery technology analyst. The discussion centers on Donut Labs, a startup that promised delivery-ready solid-state batteries with extraordinary performance specs by Q1 2026 — a deadline that came and went without delivery. The core argument is that Donut Labs has engaged in selective, misleading marketing by showcasing tests designed to highlight strengths while concealing critical weaknesses, and that their claims far outpace what the technology can currently deliver.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Donut Labs' missed Q1 deadline and extraordinary claims:** Donut Labs had publicly promised a battery product ready in customer hands by Q1 2026, with specs announced at CES including 400 watt-hours per kilogram energy density and five-minute charging for electric motorcycles. The deadline passed without delivery. Jordan's interpretation is that these specs represent an "OR" list — under specific laboratory conditions, each spec might be achievable individually, but achieving all of them simultaneously in a real-world product is a fundamentally different challenge.

2. **Cycle life testing concerns:** In a recent test, Donut Labs cycled a battery cell that had been brought up to 100°C, and after approximately 10–50 cycles (the exact number was uncertain in the conversation), the cell was only providing 50% capacity. Jordan characterized this as falling well short of the CES advertised performance. Donut Labs then announced that the battery tested was not the "final" battery and that a version two with much better performance is coming — before the first version had even completed testing.

3. **April Fools' Day video and goalpost-shifting:** On April 1st, 2026, Donut Labs released a video in which the founder blurred the lines between joke and seriousness, citing the April Fools' context. He claimed cycle life data wouldn't be believed anyway because the battery shows over 100% Coulombic efficiency in early cycles — a well-understood and normal electrochemical mechanism that Jordan says no battery expert would find suspicious or impossible. The founder also suggested a version three cell may be in development, indicating an indefinite continuation of the marketing cycle.

4. **Lab-to-market gap and the solid-state challenge:** Jordan used the analogy of handcrafting shoes versus factory production to explain why lab results don't translate to commercial products. Real-world batteries must perform across all temperature and weather conditions, within a pack of hundreds of other cells, for over a decade. He referenced QuantumCape as a well-funded example — originally targeting around 400 Wh/kg and now delivering just above 300 Wh/kg, approximately seven years after their original announcement, despite billions in funding.

5. **Semi-solid state batteries and MG4:** The conversation distinguished between fully solid-state and semi-solid state batteries. Semi-solid state uses a small amount of liquid or gel electrolyte between layers to solve the interface pressure problem inherent in fully solid-state designs. The MG4 was cited as the world's first mass-market EV with a semi-solid state battery, now launching in Europe, as evidence that some form of the technology is reaching commercialization — just not at the performance levels Donut Labs claims.

6. **Sodium-ion and LFP as counterexamples:** The hosts discussed how battery technologies like LFP and sodium-ion were once dismissed as unworkable, yet both have achieved commercialization — LFP is now mainstream, and sodium-ion batteries are appearing in passenger vehicles in China with growing adoption. This framed the argument not as "solid-state is impossible" but as "the timeline and performance claims are implausible at this scale and speed."

7. **Donut Labs' possible role as middleman:** Jordan speculated that Donut Labs may not actually be manufacturing the batteries themselves, but rather purchasing lab samples from another manufacturer and acting as a middleman between that manufacturer and Verge (an electric motorcycle company). This would explain both the hype and the lack of production infrastructure.

## Market & Competition

1. **Solid-state as a long-term gradual transition:** Jordan assessed that solid-state batteries may achieve meaningful commercialization in roughly 10–15 years, but even then the transition away from liquid electrolyte batteries will be gradual rather than a sudden disruption. Each battery technology comes with trade-offs, and improvements across the industry are the result of tens of thousands of scientists and engineers making incremental molecular-level changes over time.

2. **Miracle battery announcement frequency:** Jordan noted that miracle battery announcements appear in the media roughly every few months — sometimes two to three times per week when monitoring industry news closely. Every battery startup must claim superior performance to attract investment, making extraordinary claims structurally incentivized even when the underlying technology doesn't support them.
