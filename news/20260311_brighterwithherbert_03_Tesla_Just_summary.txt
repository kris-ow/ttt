The video is a discussion between host Herbert and economist/tech entrepreneur Joe Bacti about major developments across three robotaxi competitors: Tesla, Waymo, and Amazon Zoox. The central question is whether Tesla is on the verge of launching unsupervised robotaxi service, and how each company's moves signal their strategic positioning in what both hosts describe as a rapidly intensifying race. The conversation was recorded in early March 2026, with the hosts framing April as a potential inflection point.

TESLA — REMOVING AI SAFETY OPERATOR ROLES IN CALIFORNIA

1. Tesla has stopped posting AI safety operator job listings for all California cities, while still hiring for those roles in Virginia, Florida, and Texas. This is interpreted as either a very positive sign (Tesla no longer needs supervised drivers because it's approaching unsupervised operation) or a negative sign (Tesla is retreating from California entirely). Joe argues the positive interpretation is more probable based on the math of California's regulatory requirements.

2. California's regulatory framework requires three phases of proof that vehicles can drive themselves before unsupervised operation is permitted. Joe calculated that with 100 robotaxis, this process takes roughly three months depending on daily mileage. Tesla has approximately 355 vehicles tracked in the robotaxi tracker, and likely over 500 according to statements from Elon Musk and Tesla's Ashok, meaning they may be nearing completion of required miles as early as April 2026. The tracker is considered an undercount because it excludes employee-driven Tesla test vehicles.

3. Over 30 Cybercabs were spotted via drone footage at Giga Texas being loaded onto transport trucks. They have steering wheels, which is required to drive them onto the trucks, and Joe Techmire (who filmed the drone footage) believes they are being distributed to cities around the country for expanded testing. Some of these Cybercabs were also spotted at a crash testing facility nearby, which Herbert frames as a positive sign indicating production readiness and final certification work.

4. A separate analyst, Cernan Basher, published a finding that Tesla does not need a federal exemption for the first 2,500 autonomous vehicles if they can pass standard safety certification and crash testing. This explains why Tesla is conducting so much crash testing — passing those tests removes the numerical cap on how many Cybercabs can be deployed without steering wheels. Herbert notes this is why Tesla shifted away from a more futuristic form factor to the Cybercab design, which resembles a conventional car and can pass standard crash certification.

5. The hosts debate whether Tesla will meet its stated goal of launching in seven new cities before the end of June 2026. No new cities have been added as of the time of filming (March 2026), and they acknowledge this is a red flag worth watching. However, Joe argues that adding cities is operationally straightforward once FSD unsupervised is cleared; the harder and more decisive question is whether FSD 14.3 performs well enough to trigger unsupervised deployment.

WAYMO — $130 MILLION CEO BONUS AND STRATEGIC OUTLOOK

6. Alphabet approved a $130 million performance bonus for CEO Sundar Pichai tied specifically to Waymo's performance, with the potential to reach $260 million depending on Waymo's per-unit value increase over a three-year evaluation period. The hosts interpret this as a clear signal that Alphabet is deeply committed to Waymo and willing to invest at scale, regardless of current unprofitability.

7. Joe's theory on Waymo is that it will be the number-two player in the robotaxi market but that its current tech stack — described as a "crappy half-manual mapping thing" relying on LiDAR — is a long-term liability. He argues Waymo is deliberately building out 9 out of 10 components of the operational infrastructure (regulations, fleet management, rider support, city approvals) while knowingly deferring the technology pivot. The plan is to eventually replace the LiDAR-dependent stack with a neural net, vision-only approach — something Waymo researchers have already been exploring internally.

8. Waymo's strategy of absorbing enormous losses is framed as rational because it's outsourcing a large share of those losses to outside investors, limiting Alphabet's direct exposure. Joe estimates the total loss burn could be tens of billions of dollars before the business model works, but says Google is effectively socializing much of that risk. A video clip of a Waymo vehicle getting dangerously stuck mid-turn attempting an unprotected left turn is shown as evidence that the LiDAR-heavy stack doesn't solve core intelligence problems — both hosts note that LiDAR is useless when the fundamental issue is anticipatory judgment and contextual understanding, not object detection.

9. Waymo received a shipment of its sixth-generation robotaxis (built by Zeekr in China) and installs its own hardware at a factory in Arizona, adding cost due to import tariffs. The hosts note Waymo is capped at around 500 vehicles per city due to unit economics — exceeding that number makes the operation even more unprofitable.

AMAZON ZOOX — EXPANSION TO DALLAS AND PHOENIX

10. Zoox announced expansion of testing to Dallas and Phoenix, bringing its total U.S. testing footprint to 10 cities. It also launched a new "Fusion Center" command hub in Scottsdale, Arizona to manage fleet operations, remote guidance, and rider support. However, the hosts emphasize that this is testing only — Zoox is not yet operating commercially in most cities, and in Las Vegas it runs a fixed loop route rather than a full on-demand service.

11. Zoox's fundamental obstacle is NHTSA approval. Its purpose-built vehicle (facing passengers toward each other, with no traditional crumple zone) failed to gain full certification. NHTSA questioned how airbags function and how passenger safety is demonstrated in a crash. Zoox operates under an exemption capped at 2,500 vehicles, which makes commercial scaling impossible in its current form. Amazon is building a factory with a target of 10,000 units, but without NHTSA certification, those vehicles cannot be put on public roads. Joe emphasizes that lacking OEM manufacturing capability and automotive safety engineering expertise is a structural disadvantage that no amount of capital easily solves.

BROADER COMPETITIVE FRAMING

12. Joe argues that Tesla is the only company with all the necessary components in place: autonomous driving technology, OEM manufacturing, the ability to produce vehicles at a cost of goods well below $20,000, and existing infrastructure. He calculates that if a competitor pays $20,000 more per vehicle and wants to manufacture 10 million units, that alone represents $200 billion in additional capital expenditure — making the business model unworkable without vertical integration.

13. On timing and Tesla stock implications, Joe says there is roughly a 90% probability that the robotaxi inflection point occurs in 2026, but the difference between an April launch and a November launch is enormous for investors — he suggests the stock could range from $300-$400 on the low end to $800-$1,200 on the high end depending on when and whether unsupervised scaling begins. FSD version 14.3, expected around late February but slightly delayed, is identified as the likely decisive software step.
