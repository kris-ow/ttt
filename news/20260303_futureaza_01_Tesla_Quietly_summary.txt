Tesla quietly opened orders for a more affordable Cybertruck variant — the all-wheel drive dual motor model priced at $59,990 plus delivery (approximately $62,000 all-in). Host Brian of Futureaza discusses the implications of this pricing, a long-awaited software feature being activated, and what the surge in orders signals about Tesla's strategy. The conversation is co-hosted with Mark from "The Tesla Life," which airs Wednesdays at 4:00 p.m. Pacific / 7:00 p.m. Eastern.

Key Points:

1. Tesla is activating active noise cancellation (ANC) on existing Cybertrucks via a software update. The hardware has always been present in the vehicle but was never turned on. Tesla has a well-documented history of shipping vehicles with dormant hardware and unlocking features later — examples include matrix adaptive headlights on the 2023 Model Y (enabled roughly 1.5 to 2 years after purchase once US road regulations permitted them) and Sentry Mode security camera recording on a 2018 Model 3, which came live about 8 to 12 months after the car shipped with cameras already installed.

2. The more affordable $60,000 Cybertruck will NOT receive the active noise cancellation feature. This is because that trim has fewer speakers, and some of those speakers are required for the ANC system to function. Brian frames this pragmatically — for a $20,000 savings compared to higher trims like the Cyber Beast Foundation Series, the trade-off amounts to a "first world problem" and may not be a dealbreaker for most buyers.

3. Delivery times for the new dual-motor Cybertruck have slipped significantly. As of the recording date (around February 27, 2026), the estimated delivery window had already moved to April, and reports were emerging that some US regions were seeing delivery estimates extend nearly a full year — approximately 14 months out. Brian speculates this may be an overestimate and that Tesla will likely accelerate production rather than let customers wait that long and risk order cancellations.

4. The $59,990 introductory price was only available for a limited 10-day window, ending February 28, 2026. Tesla's website explicitly warned the price would increase after that date. Referral bonuses (previously $1,000 off) were also removed for this trim, though free Full Self-Driving trial access reportedly remained. By the video's publication date in March, the window had already closed.

5. Brian predicts the post-February 28 price will land between $64,990 and $67,990, likely $65,000 to $68,000 — and says he would be shocked if it crossed $70,000 or if the trim were discontinued. The key question both hosts raise is whether demand holds at the higher price point, which will determine whether Tesla adds production capacity (such as a full additional shift, which could cut delivery times in half).

6. The $60,000 all-wheel drive model compares favorably to Tesla's original stated target pricing. The dual-motor Cybertruck was originally announced at $49,990. Adjusted for inflation, that would be approximately $63,000 today, making the actual $60,000 price roughly $3,000 below the inflation-adjusted figure. The main things missing from the original spec are air suspension and leather upholstery (replaced with fabric/cloth).

7. Both hosts speculate on who is driving the order surge. Mark believes it's predominantly individual buyers — specifically people who had been waiting for a rumored rear-wheel-drive $40,000 Cybertruck that never materialized, and who settled on this as the closest affordable option. Brian raises the possibility of fleet buyers — contractors, government agencies, or businesses purchasing multiple units — noting that fleet purchases of even 10 vehicles would be significant given the Cybertruck's sales volumes.

8. Brian draws a broader parallel to Elon Musk's "move fast" company culture. He cites the Boring Company beginning tunnel work in Nashville essentially the moment permits were approved, and Giga Berlin's tree clearing of 600 to 700 acres completed in approximately a week and a half after permits cleared. The implication is that Tesla will similarly ramp Cybertruck production quickly once the demand signal is confirmed.

Notable quotes and data:

"For the $20,000 discount, it's going to be as noisy as the people who've been driving $120,000 Cyber Beast Foundation Series models. I don't know of a more first-world problem than that."

"Some regions of the US are going to see it be extended almost a year out now" — referring to delivery timelines, which Mark confirms is "14 months."

"If you're sitting on the fence, it's time to pull the trigger now — or forget about it."

On Boring Company's Nashville tunnel: "We're literally waiting for them to say yes. We got a yes, dig, go." The company reportedly responded to press that they were "already 2 and a half feet in."

Conclusion: The $59,990 Cybertruck dual motor appears to have been a genuine demand catalyst for Tesla, with delivery backlogs rapidly stretching well past a year in some regions. The hosts expect the price to rise modestly, production to accelerate, and the fleet/individual buyer mix to clarify the long-term viability of this trim. The ANC software unlock is a meaningful upcoming upgrade for existing higher-trim Cybertruck owners, continuing Tesla's practice of improving vehicles post-purchase.
