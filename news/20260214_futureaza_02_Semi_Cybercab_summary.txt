This is episode 133 of the Tesla Beat, a weekly live stream hosted by four commentators (John, Joe, Brian, and Hans) covering Tesla, AI, and Elon Musk's broader company ecosystem. The episode aired the day before Valentine's Day and covers a range of topics from the Tesla Semi's updated specs to XAI's explosive growth metrics and FSD navigation issues. The hosts are enthusiastic Tesla bulls who blend detailed technical analysis with casual banter.

KEY POINTS:

1. Tesla Semi specs officially updated on Tesla's website. The standard range variant delivers 325 miles with a full 82,000-pound payload, while the long-range version hits 500 miles at the same weight. Hosts note that real-world range will be even better since trucks rarely operate at maximum payload.

2. Tesla Semi curb weight is competitive with conventional Class 8 trucks. The Semi weighs 20,000–23,000 pounds compared to a typical Class 8 average of 17,000–18,000 pounds, and EVs receive an additional 2,000-pound road weight allowance, making the Semi effectively within range of diesel competitors. Brian noted Mercedes-Benz engineers had previously said such a truck was physically impossible, yet Tesla achieved 1.7 kilowatt-hours per mile, beating their own 2 kWh/mile target by 15%.

3. Pricing context: estimated around $260,000–$290,000 depending on order volume and timing, up from the original 2017 price estimate of $180,000. However, the hosts argue this is still dramatically cheaper than competing electric Class 8 trucks, some of which cost $400,000 or more and offer only 150 miles of range. Total cost of ownership — lower fuel costs, regenerative braking eliminating brake wear, and future insurance savings from active safety features — is the primary commercial selling point.

4. Fleet operator economics will drive Semi adoption. Hans emphasized that the purchasing decision comes down purely to total cost of ownership. If the Semi wins on that metric, it will sell at whatever production capacity Tesla can sustain. Driver quality-of-life improvements are a bonus but not the decision driver for fleet buyers like PepsiCo, which is already operating Semis on routes such as Sacramento to Bakersfield without needing to recharge mid-route.

5. FSD version 14.2.2.4 is widely deployed but has a notable navigation regression. Multiple hosts reported the system taking poor routes, attempting U-turns, and selecting roads near rail lines that it then cannot navigate. Hans theorized this stems from Tesla migrating routing logic from traditional "software 1.0" map code to neural network-based ("software 2.0") routing, creating instability at the interface. Speed profile issues were also noted: in one instance, Chill mode held exactly at the speed limit while Standard mode wanted to go 72 mph in a 55 mph zone.

6. Cybercab production and deployment outlook. Footage of approximately 20 Cybercabs lined up has been circulating. Brian explained that production is not the bottleneck — Tesla has re-engineered the manufacturing process from scratch using the "unboxed method" to eliminate human assembly steps, learning from Model 3 production hell. The constraint will be logistics of deployment: Tesla will likely do small bursts, diagnose issues, reconfigure, and repeat with increasing scale.

7. XAI all-hands meeting revealed striking growth figures. XAI generated 6 billion images on its platform in the last 30 days, compared to approximately 50 million reported by Google for a comparable period. The TVA approved a deal to supply XAI with up to 300 megawatts of power — roughly a city's worth of electricity — for its Memphis, Tennessee data center. A Missouri facility is also being built for additional power capacity.

8. XAI and SpaceX merger framing. Hans argued the long-term AI race will come down to a two-horse contest between Google and XAI, with the decisive advantage being energy and compute access rather than model benchmarks. The merger with SpaceX gives XAI a structural edge in both. Hans also predicted Grok 4.2 and Grok 5 releases in the not-too-distant future, and noted that competing frontier models — Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.6 and OpenAI's Codex 5.3 — are strong but XAI is closing the gap.

9. Model Y ranked California's best-selling new vehicle in 2025 for the fourth consecutive year. Sales figures showed 110,000–120,000 units registered, compared to second-place Toyota RAV4 at 65,000 units. Hosts noted that overall vehicle sales are declining industrywide, making Tesla's continued dominance more remarkable. Brian pointed out that even on Reddit — described as one of the most anti-Elon spaces online — the $5,000 price cuts a couple of years ago turned skeptics into buyers, reinforcing that purchasing decisions are driven by value, not sentiment.

10. AI industry job displacement is accelerating. The hosts noted Amazon has laid off approximately 30,000 employees over the past four months, with the rumored internal rationale being reallocation of capital toward GPU compute for AWS growth, not cost-cutting per se. The broader point made is that the AI transition mirrors the horse-to-automobile shift — painful for displaced workers with no immediate alternative, but ultimately irreversible.
