This video from Munro Live features Sandy Munro's team conducting a detailed exterior teardown analysis of the 2026 Cadillac Escalade IQ, in partnership with A2Mac1, which provided disassembled physical components for close examination. Troy, Munro's exterior components expert, leads the walkthrough alongside Sandy, explaining manufacturing processes, material choices, fit and finish observations, and cost trade-offs across the front fascia, grille, lighting elements, chrome trim, and wheel well liners.

## Market & Competition

1. **Cadillac Escalade IQ Price Point and Premium Ambition:** The 2026 Cadillac Escalade IQ carries a price tag of $155,000, which Troy notes is reflected in the extensive use of illuminated elements, tinted chrome, and high-finish exterior components throughout the front fascia. The design priorities appear heavily studio-driven, with premium aesthetics consistently winning out over manufacturing simplicity or cost efficiency.

2. **Front Fascia Fit and Finish Issues:** Troy identifies visible misalignment in the upper chrome components on both sides of the vehicle, which he attributes to a structural design decision: the center section of the front fascia is mounted to the hood (frunk lid), while the outer sections are body-mounted. With approximately an 8mm tolerance between the hood and body, misalignment across that seam is an inherent consequence of the design, and Troy notes this can be observed on Escalade IQs in the field generally.

3. **Two-Shot Injection Molded Grille Construction with Laser Ablation:** The center grille panel, which many observers at auto shows mistook for a sticker, is actually a two-shot injection molded component — a clear PC outer layer over a black PC/ABS inner layer. The decorative pattern is created by laser ablating (etching) through the black layer to expose the clear, and then painting the B-side in silver/gray to create the visual depth effect. Troy compares the technique to painting on the back of a piece of glass. Sandy questions whether an in-mold film process could have achieved a similar result at lower cost, noting that painting introduces higher scrap rates and expensive paint line infrastructure. Troy acknowledges this is an active area of industry exploration, particularly for exterior applications.

4. **Illuminated Grille Elements and LED Sealing:** The outer lit sections of the grille share a similar two-shot PC/PC-ABS construction with laser ablation, but add an LED lighting assembly. A large rib runs across the back of the lens component, forming a glue channel that seals the LED housing to the lens, preventing water intrusion. A tinting paint coat is applied to the B-side to make the lens more opaque rather than fully clear, controlling how the light diffuses.

5. **Tinted Chrome Trim — Process and Durability Concerns:** The outer chrome trim elements are traditional electroplated ABS (acrylonitrile butadiene styrene) parts, with a tint coating applied over the chrome to give a darker, more luxurious appearance. Troy explains that the electroplating process chemically etches the butadiene (rubber) layer of ABS to allow chrome adhesion, and that geometry is highly constrained — deep draw areas receive less chrome coverage, and curved or deeply contoured shapes can distort during plating ("like frying a potato"). He raises a durability concern about the tinted chrome trim being positioned in a stone-strike zone low on the fascia, noting that rock chips can cause the tint coating to peel, revealing raw chrome or bare plastic underneath.

6. **Wheel Opening Moldings — Cost and Corrosion Trade-offs:** The Escalade IQ uses body-color-matched wheel opening moldings (WOs), which Sandy notes he would prefer to eliminate from a cost standpoint. Troy explains that beyond aesthetics, these moldings serve a corrosion mitigation function — by eliminating the tight bend at the bottom of the fender where dirt and moisture accumulate, they reduce the onset of corrosion typically seen around wheel openings on older vehicles.

7. **Wheel Well Liner Material Trade-offs — Textile vs. Injection Molded vs. Thermoformed:** The vehicle uses textile (PET fabric) wheel well liners, which Troy notes are more expensive than vacuum-thermoformed plastic liners but less expensive than injection molded liners. Sandy points out that injection molded liners — often perceived as "cheap plastic" — are actually the most expensive of the three options. Trade-offs are discussed at length: textile liners offer better NVH (noise, vibration, harshness) performance and weight savings at the factory, but accumulate mud and snow over time, negating the weight benefit and generating customer complaints. Thermoformed liners require secondary waterjet trimming to achieve final geometry, making them more labor-intensive than injection molding, where the final part edge is set in the mold itself.

8. **Cadillac Design DNA — Vertical Lighting Heritage:** Sandy closes the walkthrough with a historical design note, explaining that Cadillac's signature narrow, vertical lighting treatment traces directly to the tail fins of 1950s Cadillacs. As physical fins disappeared through the 1960s and 1970s, the vertical light signature was preserved and carried through to the present day. He notes that the current CT5 and CT4 are beginning to transition away from this toward L-shaped and angular light signatures, marking a potential shift in the brand's visual identity.
