This video from the channel "jobhakdi," hosted by Jo Bhakdi, provides a rapid analysis of the newly released FSD version 14.3, examining what the update contains, why it matters for Tesla's robotaxi ambitions, and what it means for the stock. The core thesis is that while FSD 14.3 is a significant and foundational update — not a trivial one — it is not by itself the singular trigger for robotaxi deployment; rather, the decisive indicator remains whether unsupervised Cybercab production and scaling begins in April or May 2026. Jo Bhakdi frames the next six weeks as a critical window that will either validate or break the Tesla robotaxi investment thesis.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD 14.3 Released with Major Technical Improvements:** Tesla officially released FSD (Supervised) version 14.3, describing it as a broad and significant update. The most headline-grabbing improvement is a 20% faster reaction time, achieved by rewriting the AI compiler and runtime from the ground up using MLIR. Jo Bhakdi calls this "a very big deal," noting that faster reaction time across the board for an autonomous system responsible for human safety is critically important.

2. **Neural Network and Vision Encoder Upgrades:** The update includes reinforcement learning applied to the FSD neural network, enabling improvements across a wide variety of driving scenarios through a self-reinforcing learning loop across Tesla's entire fleet. The neural network vision encoder was also upgraded, specifically improving performance in rare and low-visibility conditions such as rain and sun glare, as well as strengthening 3D geometry understanding and expanding traffic sign recognition.

3. **Robotaxi-Focused Feature Set:** Several specific improvements in 14.3 point toward robotaxi readiness rather than supervised driver assistance. These include improved parking location pin prediction for complex environments like shopping mall lots, increased decisiveness in parking spot selection and maneuvering, enhanced response to emergency vehicles, improved handling of school buses, and better handling of small animals and unusual objects leaning into the vehicle path. Jo Bhakdi explicitly interprets this feature set as evidence of a strong internal focus on robotaxi use cases, noting that many of these features are trivial when a human driver is present but become critical when there is no one in the vehicle.

4. **Early Tester Impressions — Positive but Measured:** Early testers shared mixed-to-positive feedback. Tester Nick Cruz highlighted the faster reaction time as the standout improvement, noting the system finds parking spaces quicker, reacts at traffic lights faster, and performed "pretty much perfectly" in about 10 parking tests at a busy Costco lot. He also noted improved following distance with more space left to the car ahead. Tester Soya Merit noted more comfortable highway follow distance, quicker takeoff from stop signs, and reduced highway lane change hesitation, but said the version needs some smoothing out and characterized it as "setting the stage for something larger to come" with further reasoning capabilities expected in future updates. Jo Bhakdi summarizes these as important but not definitively game-changing reviews.

5. **14.3 as Foundation, Not Final Trigger:** Jo Bhakdi pushes back on the idea — attributed to Elon Musk's framing — that 14.3 is "the last missing piece" of autonomous transport. He believes Elon is "maybe hyping this a little bit too much." His view is that 14.3 is a very important foundational improvement that lays the groundwork for robotaxi, but that the real bottleneck for robotaxi rollout was never solely the software version — it is the convergence of many incremental improvements across software, hardware, and production readiness.

## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab Production Start in April Is the Decisive Catalyst:** Jo Bhakdi states that his primary indicator for the robotaxi thesis has always been Cybercab production, specifically that mass production was targeted to start in April 2026. He frames this as the moment where Tesla must scale or risk looking like it wasted significant capex. The current robotaxi fleet tracker shows approximately 560 unsupervised vehicles, well short of his earlier prediction of 1,800, which he acknowledges is clearly not being met.

2. **Six-Week Window: April–May 2026 Is Make-or-Break:** Jo Bhakdi outlines a clear binary scenario for Tesla investors over the next six weeks (through end of May 2026). If unsupervised Cybercab scaling begins — even starting with just 10, then 20, then 40, then 80 units in an exponential ramp — he expects a major stock rally well beyond $500 per share. If unsupervised scaling does not begin by end of May, he predicts a deflation period for Tesla stock because the timeline becomes unknown and the thesis anchor breaks.

3. **Thesis Break Condition Defined:** Jo Bhakdi explicitly states that his thesis — that Cybercab is a reliable indicator of autonomous scaling — will break if unsupervised Cybercab deployment does not begin by end of May 2026. He allows for up to one month of delay from the April target (i.e., into May), but says beyond that, the investment case for near-term robotaxi becomes speculative and the stock will reprice downward. He notes this may be an unjust market reaction if deployment simply slips to June, but argues that is how the market will behave.

4. **Exponential Ramp Logic:** Jo Bhakdi argues that the starting number of unsupervised Cybercabs does not matter — what matters is that the ramp begins, because once started, growth will be exponential. He suggests that the starting point could be "multiple dozens" and will scale from there. The key investment signal is the inflection point of initiation, not the absolute fleet size at launch.

He frames Tesla's improvements in parking decisiveness and reaction time as directly addressing the kinds of operational failures associated with Waymo's public deployments.
