This is episode 135 of the Tesla Beat, a weekly roundup show on the futureaza channel hosted by John, Brian, and Joe, with recurring co-host Hans joining for what is announced as one of his final regular appearances. The episode covers a busy week of Tesla news including Cybertruck pricing developments, Giga Berlin's future, FSD international expansion, Optimus production scaling, the Boring Company's Nashville progress, and Tesla's lawsuit against the California DMV. Tesla stock is noted as sitting around $405, down roughly 7% on the day, in line with broader market weakness.

1. Elon Musk's internal Berlin video. An internally produced video was released on X in which Elon answered questions from Giga Berlin employees. The hosts note that some questions appeared to genuinely surprise Elon, suggesting the questions were curated by a Tesla team rather than hand-picked by him. The core message was that Tesla remains committed to Germany, wants to expand, but is constrained by European regulation. The hosts argue that critics focusing on Berlin's declining sales volume are misreading the situation, since Berlin is not Tesla's production bottleneck, making its output figures low signal-to-noise for evaluating Tesla's overall health.

2. FSD expansion to the Netherlands and beyond. FSD supervised driving is set to launch in the Netherlands on March 20th, and has already officially begun in the UAE. The hosts also note FSD is active in Korea, Australia, and China. Brian frames FSD approval in the Netherlands as significant because EU countries tend to follow each other's regulatory lead, and FSD is described as "the gateway drug to robo taxi," which is Tesla's ultimate goal in Europe. The hosts criticize European media and analysts for ignoring FSD, robo taxi, Megapack, Optimus, and Solar when evaluating Tesla's European position.

3. Cybertruck demand surge at $60,000. The price drop to $60,000 for a dual-motor Cybertruck triggered what the hosts describe as the highest order volume since the truck's launch. Deliveries are now backed up to April 2027. The hosts expect the price to rise once the promotional window closes, with guesses landing around $65,000–$69,420 (the latter offered as a joke). Joe and Brian both emphasize this validates the thesis that demand is highly price-elastic for the Cybertruck. Brian notes the average new truck sells for $66,000 and the Ford Raptor has climbed from $47,000 in 2019 to roughly $80,000+, making the Cybertruck competitively priced with far superior technology. The hosts argue that as Tesla continues down the cost curve, it will increasingly undercut legacy truck makers whose manufacturing costs are rising, not falling.

4. Tesla's Giga Texas north campus expansion. Based on reporting from Joe Tegtmeyer, who recently toured the Cyber Cab production line, the north campus expansion at Gigafactory Texas is widely expected to house Optimus production. Brian states that pilot-scale Optimus production will begin at Fremont (replacing the Model S/X lines when they end in June), with the Texas facility targeting up to one million units annually, with Elon having referenced 100 million units as a longer-term capacity goal. Brian also speculates Cortex 3 (the next AI training data center) is a likely tenant, and raises the possibility of solar panel manufacturing given the 100% import tariff on Chinese-made cells, which effectively doubles the price of competing Chinese panels in the US market. Chip fabrication is considered unlikely given a recent fab cost cited at $165 billion.

5. Model Y L safety and global rollout. Tesla released footage of the Model Y L featuring new curtain airbags covering rear windows for second-row protection and an additional airbag in the third row, along with heated third-row seats and dedicated ventilation — features notably absent from the standard Model Y's afterthought third row. Regulatory approvals for the Y L have been seen in Australia and Germany. Brian makes a strong case for bringing it to the US, pointing to surplus production capacity in Austin and a large American market for three-row SUVs. The hosts also muse about a potential Cyber SUV, arguing it would not need stainless steel and could reuse front castings and battery packs from the Cybertruck platform.

6. Tesla sues California DMV over false advertising ruling. Tesla's legal team is challenging the California DMV's ruling that Tesla engaged in false advertising regarding Full Self-Driving. The key legal argument is that the DMV never demonstrated actual consumer confusion about whether the cars required a human at the wheel. The hosts praise the legal framing as a clever and narrow argument, noting Tesla has a strong track record in litigation. The case is expected to take years but could establish important legal precedent for how autonomous driving features are regulated and described in California.

7. Boring Company tunneling in Nashville. The Boring Company received permits for Nashville tunnels and immediately began drilling — reportedly already 2.5 feet in before the ink was dry. Hans highlights that permitting is the main bottleneck, not engineering, since tunnel entrances and exits must interface with existing urban infrastructure. Brian recalls how Tesla used the same "go the moment you get permission" approach at Giga Berlin's land clearing, bringing in logging contractors from Ukraine and the Czech Republic and clearing hundreds of acres in three days. The hosts also note that underground property rights are legally undefined in a way that airspace is not, which is both a regulatory challenge and an opportunity.

8. Uber's autonomous vehicle marketplace play. Uber announced a new platform for autonomous vehicle operators to list rides on the Uber network. Brian dismisses this as a company "grasping at straws," noting Uber sold its own AV development for scrap and has no proprietary technology to offer. He argues Uber's business model — extracting margin from both drivers and customers while owning neither cars nor employees — is fundamentally threatened by Tesla's robo taxi network and Waymo's growing direct consumer presence.

9. Lamborghini abandons EV plans. Lamborghini's CEO cited weak EV demand as justification for shelving its electric vehicle program. Brian reframes this as Lamborghini failing to make an EV compelling at their price point, not evidence that people don't want EVs. He also suggests it reflects a bet that Tesla's Roadster 2 will never materialize as a performance competitor.
