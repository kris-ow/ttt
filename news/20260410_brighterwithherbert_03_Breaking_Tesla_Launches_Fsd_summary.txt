This video is a live breaking news stream covering the formal approval of FSD Supervised in the Netherlands by the RDW (Dutch Vehicle Authority), marking the first official FSD approval in continental Europe. Host Herbert is joined by Jeff Lutz and Amsterdam-based Tesla enthusiast and advocate Kees Roolvink Schapendonk, who helped lobby for the approval and was celebrating at midnight when the news broke. The core argument is that the Netherlands approval is a historic domino that could trigger rapid mutual recognition across all 27+ EU member states, dramatically expanding Tesla's FSD market.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD Supervised formally approved in the Netherlands by the RDW:** Tesla Europe announced that FSD Supervised has received formal regulatory approval from the RDW (the Dutch type approval authority) and will begin rolling out in the Netherlands shortly. The RDW's official statement emphasized that "due to the continuous strict monitoring of the driver in the vehicle, the system is safer than other driver assistance systems," and noted they had "thoroughly researched and checked the system for more than a year and a half." The approval was granted under an exemptionary article that allows regulators to fast-track novel technology not yet captured by existing regulatory frameworks.

2. **Netherlands approval as a gateway for EU-wide rollout:** Because the RDW is the governing type approval body in Europe — every vehicle with an E4 stamp from the RDW is approved Europe-wide — the Netherlands approval creates a mutual recognition pathway for all other EU member states. Tesla itself cited a summer 2026 timeline for broader EU rollout, with Germany, France, Italy, Belgium, Austria, and the Nordics expected to follow. A fast-track timeline of 4 to 8 weeks for some countries was discussed, with medium and slower tracks also possible depending on national discretionary authority.

3. **Regulatory significance — EU and UNECE must now respond:** Kees explained that the exemption granted to Tesla by the RDW places Tesla's FSD above the existing regulatory framework, meaning both the EU (European Commission) and the UNECE (United Nations Economic Commission for Europe) are now compelled to respond and update their own regulations to accommodate the technology. This was characterized as a "big baller move" by Tesla, effectively forcing supranational regulatory bodies to follow suit.

4. **Current global FSD Supervised approval footprint:** As of the video, FSD Supervised is approved in the United States, Canada, Mexico, Puerto Rico, Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, and now the Netherlands. Supervised trials are already launched in Abu Dhabi as of March 1, 2026. A partial approval for China has occurred, with a full approval anticipated soon based on signals in the software. Israel's transport minister has publicly stated FSD approval is coming soon, and Japan is also expected to follow.

5. **FSD version used in Netherlands testing and approval:** Kees noted that during Tesla's final ride-along session the Friday before approval, he observed a version change from prior weeks and spotted on-screen notifications never seen in US versions, which he believes were specific RDW requirements added during the testing period. He suggested Europe will receive a slightly adjusted version of what is available in the US but still very similar. Jeff Lutz noted that the 14.2 series has been "really stable" in the US for several months. An FSD version referenced as 14.3 was mentioned as being even more responsive, though the regulatory testing was conducted on older versions.

6. **Tesla's multi-year EU testing program:** Kees noted that Tesla has been testing FSD in approximately seven to nine European countries over the past two years, covering varying road conditions, signage differences, and merging behaviors across countries like France, Germany, Spain, and Italy. He reported that FSD performed consistently across these countries, with road harmonization since the 1950s making European driving environments more similar than different.

7. **Legacy OEM contrast:** Kees drew a pointed contrast between legacy automakers who loudly promised full autonomy by 2025 back in 2019–2020 and have since repeatedly retracted those claims, versus Tesla, which he credited as "the only company that followed through and stayed persistent on their goal." He gave particular credit to Tesla's policy team for grinding through years of regulatory resistance.

8. **Border-crossing notification feature:** Kees noted that Tesla added an in-vehicle on-screen notification that alerts drivers when they are about to cross a national border within Europe, warning that FSD Supervised functionality might deteriorate. He attributed this feature to the ongoing patchwork of national approvals and said it will likely remain in place for some time as other countries work through their own approval processes.

9. **Estimated Tesla fleet in Europe eligible for FSD:** Jeff Lutz estimated approximately 1.2 million Teslas sold in Europe since 2020, potentially rising to 1.5 million if going back further — representing the installed base that could immediately access FSD Supervised as the rollout expands across EU member states.
