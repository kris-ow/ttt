This video from farzad-fm draws a historical parallel between the 19th-century Luddite movement and the 2026 AI data center moratorium bill introduced by Senator Bernie Sanders and co-sponsored by Congresswoman Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez. The host's core thesis is that attempts to stop transformative technologies through legislation have never succeeded historically — they only relocate the technology elsewhere — but that the political pressure such movements generate can still produce meaningful worker protections. The video is primarily a historical, political, and economic essay rather than a Tesla- or EV-specific analysis, though the host briefly mentions being heavily invested in Tesla and AI companies.

## Market & Competition

1. **The Sanders-AOC AI Data Center Moratorium Act:** On March 25th, 2026, Senator Bernie Sanders introduced the AI Data Center Moratorium Act, co-sponsored in the House by Congresswoman Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez. The bill does not regulate how AI is used, does not create worker retraining programs, and does not impose taxes on automation — instead, it bans the construction of new AI data center buildings. The host argues this is equivalent to trying to stop the automobile by banning roads, and that the technology would simply relocate to countries without such restrictions.

2. **Sanders' Broader Policy Proposals:** Beyond the moratorium, Sanders has proposed a "robot tax" on companies that replace workers with automated systems, a 32-hour work week with no reduction in pay, 20% worker ownership of stock in large corporations, and 45% of corporate board seats allocated to employees. Sanders' office released a report in October 2025 claiming approximately 100 million U.S. jobs — roughly two out of three jobs in the American economy — are at risk from AI. The host distinguishes these broader proposals as legitimate subjects for debate, unlike the moratorium itself.

3. **Global AI Competition and the Relocation Argument:** The host argues that a U.S. moratorium would not slow AI development by even a single day, as China is investing hundreds of billions of dollars in AI through 2030 and has made AI dominance a national priority. The UAE has launched a national AI strategy and is building large data center campuses, Singapore is offering tax incentives and fast-track permits to position itself as Southeast Asia's AI hub, and even El Salvador is courting AI companies with zero-tax incentives. The host contends that restricting U.S. data center construction would simply shift AI model training to Shenzhen or Abu Dhabi instead of Virginia or Texas.

4. **Historical Pattern of Technology Suppression:** The host walks through four historical examples where restrictions on transformative technologies backfired. England's Red Flag Act of 1865 required a person to walk ahead of automobiles with a red flag, limited speeds to 2 mph in towns, and was not fully lifted until 1896 — by which time France had captured nearly 50% of the global automobile market and Britain lost its lead in the automobile revolution. American farmers' anti-automobile legislation in rural communities was similarly futile; within 25 years, farmers became the most enthusiastic adopters of cars and tractors. The U.S. halt on nuclear plant construction after Three Mile Island in 1979 resulted in over 100 reactor cancellations, and by 2026 the U.S. is scrambling to restart nuclear capacity for AI data center power — with Microsoft signing a deal to restart the actual Three Mile Island plant. The EU's restrictions on GMO crops led to productivity disadvantages relative to the U.S., Brazil, and Argentina, and the EU became dependent on importing the very GMO crops it had banned.

5. **Energy Costs and Wealth Concentration as Real Concerns:** The host acknowledges that the concerns motivating the bill are legitimate. A typical large AI data center consumes around 100 megawatts of electricity — equivalent to powering approximately 80,000 homes — and in some communities these facilities are genuinely driving up electricity prices for residents. The Trump administration has reportedly asked hyperscalers to subsidize additional power costs so electricity prices do not increase for local residents. The host also acknowledges that AI benefits are flowing disproportionately to those who already own capital, noting his own AI expenditure is approximately $2,000 per month and yields significant productivity gains that most people cannot replicate.

6. **Labor Economics Data on Automation Displacement:** The host cites MIT economists Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo, who published data showing that between 1947 and 1987, automation displacement and reinstatement of new jobs were roughly balanced — displacement destroyed approximately 0.48% of labor demand per year while new tasks created approximately 0.47% per year. Since 1987, however, displacement accelerated to approximately 0.7% per year while reinstatement slowed to approximately 0.35% per year, producing a compounding gap that adds up to roughly a 10% net reduction in labor demand over 30 years. Research also found that each additional industrial robot per 1,000 workers reduced the employment-to-population ratio by 0.2 to 0.34 percentage points. Acemoglu, who won the Nobel Prize in economics, stated in 2024 that AI is being used "too much for automation and not enough for providing expertise and information to workers," and estimates only about 5% of human tasks will be fully replaced by AI over the next decade.

7. **The Luddite Legacy and What Comes After Failed Bills:** The host argues that the original Luddites were not technophobes but highly skilled workers — stocking frame knitters, croppers, and shearers — who had exhausted every legal avenue before resorting to machine-breaking because trade unions had been made illegal under the Combination Acts of 1799 and 1800. Their unrest contributed to the repeal of those acts in 1824, legalizing trade unions for the first time. This led to the Factory Act of 1833 (banning child labor under age 9 in textile mills, limiting children aged 9-13 to 8-hour days, and establishing government inspectors), and the Ten Hours Act of 1847. The host's conclusion is that the Sanders-AOC moratorium will fail to pass — given that too many states want data center jobs and tax revenue — but that the political pressure it generates may produce meaningful downstream policy such as worker retraining funds, data center energy offset requirements, or tech company taxes funding transition support.
