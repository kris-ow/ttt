Tesla announced the expansion of its "Robotaxi" service to Dallas and Houston — its first markets beyond Austin and San Francisco — but provided virtually no operational details on fleet size, supervision status, or pricing.

## Robotaxi
1. **Houston and Dallas launch with minimal geofences:** The Houston service area covers approximately 25 square miles; the Dallas zone appears centered around the Highland Park area. For comparison, Tesla's Austin geofence now spans roughly 245 square miles, but that took nearly a year to grow from an initial 20-square-mile footprint. Tesla's announcement on X consisted solely of two map images and the line "Robotaxi now rolling out in Dallas & Houston," with no disclosure of vehicle count, supervision status, or timeline for geofence expansion.

2. **Austin operation remains small and mostly supervised:** As of the article, Tesla's Austin fleet totals roughly 80 vehicles, but only 4–12 are operating unsupervised (without a human safety monitor) at any given time, and most of the full fleet is not operating simultaneously. The remaining vehicles carry safety monitors, and all are remotely supervised by Tesla staff.

3. **Musk's prior promises vs. reality:** Musk had predicted 500 vehicles in Austin and over 1,000 in the Bay Area by end of 2025; actual figures were approximately 42 and 130, nearly all supervised. His claim that the service would cover half the U.S. population by end-2025 also did not materialize.

## Autonomous Driving
4. **Safety record and limitations:** Tesla has reported 15 crash incidents to NHTSA since its Austin launch, with crash rates approximately 4–9 times worse than human drivers depending on the benchmark. Tesla's own data shows crash rates 3x worse than humans even with a safety monitor present. One July 2025 crash was later upgraded to include a hospitalization without public disclosure. Tesla redacts all crash narratives in NHTSA filings as "confidential business information," unlike Waymo, Zoox, Aurora, and Nuro. The service still does not operate in rain — a notable limitation given Houston averages over 100 rainy days per year.

## Market & Competition
5. **Waymo already operating at scale in both cities:** Waymo has been operating fully driverless robotaxis in Houston and Dallas since February 2026. The company delivers 500,000 paid rides per week across 10 U.S. cities, operates approximately 2,500 active robotaxis nationwide, and is targeting 1 million weekly rides by year-end 2026. Independent research cited in the article shows Waymo reduces serious-injury crashes by 91% compared to human drivers. Waymo is scaling toward public availability in both Texas cities through its partnership with Avis Budget Group.

## Editorial Stance
The author characterizes the announcement as a marketing event rather than a genuine operational milestone, arguing that Tesla is expanding geographically while its Austin operation "remains tiny and troubled," and that the lack of real operational data — fleet size, miles driven, safety metrics, ride availability — makes these launches closer to stock-pumping hype than substantive progress.
