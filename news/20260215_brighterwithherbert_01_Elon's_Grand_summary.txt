Key Points:

1. Voting control is the central thread tying everything together. In January 2024, Musk publicly stated he wanted 25% voting power at Tesla before committing further to AI and robotics development, warning he would build those products elsewhere if denied. In November 2025, Tesla shareholders approved a compensation package granting him over 423 million additional shares, taking his stake from roughly 13% to 25%. At XAI he already held approximately 79% ownership, and at SpaceX he holds around 42%. Bloomberg reported that the SpaceX IPO will feature dual-class voting shares, meaning Musk could hold preferred shares worth 10 to 20 votes each while selling large portions of the company to public investors — the same structure used by Google and Meta. Every major company in the Musk portfolio is structured so he retains unilateral decision-making authority.

2. The SpaceX-XAI merger, completed February 2, 2026, creates a single $1.25 trillion entity. SpaceX was valued at $1 trillion and XAI at $250 billion. The combined company now includes SpaceX rockets and launch operations, Starlink satellite internet with 10 million customers, XAI's Grok AI models, X (formerly Twitter) with 600 million users, and the Colossus Supercomputer in Memphis. Herbert argues the merger serves three purposes: it creates a compelling narrative for IPO investors (space-based AI), simplifies governance by consolidating everything under one board and shareholder base, and establishes genuine operational synergies between rockets, satellites, and AI. The planned IPO could value the combined entity at $1.5 trillion, potentially the largest public offering in history, raising $30 to $50 billion.

3. Musk's stated rationale for the merger is that space-based AI data centers will be the cheapest computing option within two to three years. The Colossus data center in Memphis already consumes nearly 1 gigawatt of power — equivalent to a nuclear power plant's output — and AI energy demands keep growing. His proposed solution is to place computing infrastructure in orbit, where solar power is abundant and cooling is easier. Herbert acknowledges this is likely not a near-term reality, given the extreme difficulty of building orbital data centers, but notes it functions as an effective IPO narrative and represents a genuine long-term directional bet.

4. XAI reorganized into four specialized teams on February 11, 2026. The teams are: Grok Voice (led by Aman Madan, focused on conversational AI), Coding (led by Manuel Croce, focused on AI-generated software — with an internal claim of approaching AGI for programming tasks), Imagine (led by Guadong Jang, focused on image and video generation, reportedly producing 50 million videos per day and 6 billion images per month), and MacroArt (led by Toby Poland, focused on AI agents that can control computers and perform any task a human can do on a screen). Herbert notes that MacroArt already reported AI designing rocket engines at SpaceX autonomously, not just assisting human designers. Each team maps directly onto Tesla use cases: voice for in-car and Optimus interaction, coding for software acceleration across all Tesla systems, image/video for FSD and Optimus simulation training data, and MacroArt for factory automation.
