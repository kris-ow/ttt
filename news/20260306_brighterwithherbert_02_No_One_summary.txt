Tesla's global expansion of Full Self-Driving (FSD) and robotaxi infrastructure is accelerating across multiple continents simultaneously, with hiring patterns, regulatory signals, and sales data all pointing to rapid scaling. Host Herbert and venture capital investor Larry Goldberg examine a cluster of developments that occurred within roughly 24 hours, arguing that taken together they reveal Tesla is much further along in its global rollout than most observers realize. The central thesis is that Tesla follows a consistent pattern of quiet infrastructure-building — hiring, factory expansion, regulatory groundwork — followed by sudden large-scale deployment.

Key Points:

1. Tesla is hiring vehicle operators in multiple European countries on one-year fixed-term contracts. New job postings list full-time vehicle operator positions in Croatia, Greece, Portugal, Slovenia, and the Netherlands. These roles are interpreted as data-gathering and regulatory approval positions — drivers tasked with accumulating FSD mileage to satisfy local regulators. Elon Musk has stated he expects FSD approval in the Netherlands around March 20, 2026, but the hiring across four additional countries signals Tesla anticipates a broader European rollout soon.

2. A separate Berlin job posting for an electrical design engineer for "robotaxi autonomous vehicles" signals deeper FSD integration in Europe. Larry Goldberg notes this role likely involves installing, testing, and validating FSD software in cars built at Giga Berlin, regardless of whether robotaxi service launches in Germany imminently. This is characterized as part of Tesla's infrastructure-building approach — the engineering talent must be in place locally before deployment can scale.

3. Giga Berlin's works council election produced a significant setback for the IG Metal union. IG Metal's share of the works council dropped from 40% in 2024 to 31% in 2026, while an independent group called Giga United received over 40% of votes. Elon Musk had stated publicly that if employees and stakeholders supported expansion, he planned to make Giga Berlin the largest factory in Europe, adding Cyber Cab, Optimus, and Tesla Semi production. Goldberg cautions the vote happens annually and notes the role of a newly influential bloc of Polish workers as a variable to watch, but overall views the result as a green light for acceleration.

4. Giga Berlin's expansion potential includes batteries, Cyber Cabs, Tesla Semi, and eventually Optimus. Goldberg argues the factory could immediately begin manufacturing batteries using Tesla's dry cell system, which would significantly improve gross margins. He views Cyber Cabs as particularly well-suited for the European market and expects Tesla Semi volume production by end of 2026, with that capability transferable to Europe rapidly.

5. Japan is close to FSD approval, with testing underway since August 2025. Japanese news outlet Nikkei and other domestic media reported Tesla Japan has been conducting FSD test runs on Tokyo public roads for several months, initially with a Model 3 and now adding Model Y. Tesla aims to have FSD approved in Japan within 2026. Japan sold approximately 10,000–11,000 Tesla vehicles last year, up 50% year-over-year, and Tesla holds roughly 30% of Japan's EV market. FSD approval is expected to spur both Tesla sales and pressure Japanese automakers like Toyota and Nissan to respond.

6. South Korea has become Tesla's third-largest sales market globally, behind only China and the United States. South Korea outsells Japan by roughly 3-to-1 in Tesla units despite having less than half Japan's population. The Model Y is the top-selling vehicle in all of South Korea. Tesla owners in South Korea have already driven over 5 million miles (8 million kilometers) using FSD Supervised in just 100 days of availability, demonstrating high adoption rates in a tech-forward market.

7. Ireland signed a statutory instrument legalizing Level 2 autonomous vehicles on public roads. Transport Minister Shan Canny's measure allows vehicles with advanced driver assistance systems to be legally operated with hands temporarily off the wheel in limited circumstances. While Tesla still needs separate regulatory type approval to commercially launch FSD Supervised in Ireland, Goldberg views this as a likely pathway for approval and notes that European countries tend to follow each other's lead once one nation moves first.

8. Hong Kong's Tesla sales are strong, with Model Y and Model 3 leading private vehicle registrations in February 2026. This is cited as further evidence of Tesla's global momentum, with the refreshed Model Y specifically credited for renewed consumer interest.

Conclusions and Recommendations:

Goldberg and Herbert argue Tesla's second half of 2026 will be a visible inflection point as infrastructure laid over the past two or more years begins producing deployable robotaxi and FSD services in new markets. They caution that European regulatory timelines frequently slip — the March 20 Netherlands date may move to April, June, or later. The key analytical framework offered is to track job postings, factory votes, and regulatory filings as leading indicators rather than waiting for official announcements. Herbert plans a South Korea visit in July 2026 and notes the country as a bellwether for Tesla's Asia strategy outside China.
