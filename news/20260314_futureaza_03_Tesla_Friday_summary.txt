Episode 137 of the Tesla Beat weekly roundup covers a dense week of Tesla and Elon Musk ecosystem news, including stock performance, regulatory developments, new vehicle rollouts, competitor struggles, and long-term strategic speculation about a potential SpaceX-Tesla merger. Host John is joined by regular contributors Brian and Joe, plus new rotating co-host Matthew DR, a longtime Tesla investor and software entrepreneur with deep contacts inside the company. The panel's overall tone is bullish on Tesla's long-term positioning while acknowledging near-term headwinds in stock price and product timing.

1. Tesla stock is down 10% year-to-date, trading at $396, while the S&P 500 is down roughly 2-3% over the same period. The panel attributes broader market weakness partly to an ongoing war in Iran. Matthew notes that most of Tesla's upside is being priced on robotaxi and autonomy, and until Cyber Cab units are actually deployed and usable by the public without long waits, significant stock movement is unlikely. He has not traded options since approximately December and is holding shares only.

2. Tesla received regulatory clearance to exchange its XAI investment for SpaceX equity. The panel frames this as fulfillment of Elon's earlier promise that Tesla shareholders would eventually benefit from the Twitter/X acquisition chain. Brian argues the synergies between SpaceX, XAI, and Tesla are genuine — particularly the concept of space-based AI data centers, which he says become financially viable once Starship launch costs and compute prices hit certain crossover points. He dismisses skeptics as failing to understand that it is a spreadsheet problem, not a fantasy.

3. A planned April Roadster unveil and Cyber Cab production announcement are anticipated but viewed skeptically by the panel. Matthew notes that Tesla events announced more than a few weeks out have a consistent history of slipping — citing the "8/8" robotaxi event that moved to "10/10" — and says he will not buy options calls or sell puts until a firm date is confirmed. Brian agreed the Roadster on April 1st is unlikely to happen on schedule.

4. The Tesla Model Y Long (YL), the six-seat variant that launched in China, has officially arrived in Australia and New Zealand. The panel sees this as an indicator of broader global rollout, with John speculating a US arrival could come in Q3 or Q4. He notes Tesla appears to be deliberately avoiding cannibalizing remaining Model X sales before introducing the YL domestically.

5. A company called "Macro Hard" (described as the opposite of Microsoft, referencing the digital optimist handle) announced deployment of digital Optimus units at Tesla Superchargers, which collectively have 7 gigawatts of available power. The units also reportedly work on all AI4-equipped Tesla vehicles for office-type tasks while the car is parked. Matthew ties this to the earlier revelation from a fired XAI employee who stated the company was effectively building the brain for Optimus, calling it a development that was "telegraphed" to investors.

6. The Cyber Cab's self-cleaning camera system was publicly demonstrated for the first time, showing simultaneous operation of side repeater washers, B-pillar washers, and rear camera washers. Brian reports from an interview with Joe Tagmire that the washer fluid reservoir in the Cyber Cab is the largest he has ever seen in a vehicle. The panel pushes back against critics who evaluate the Cyber Cab as a personal vehicle — Matthew compares it to a city bike share unit versus a personal bicycle, emphasizing it is purpose-built for maximum autonomy efficiency, not personal ownership comfort. It is designed to self-close doors, self-clean cameras, and wirelessly self-charge.

7. Honda reported its first annual loss in nearly 70 years as a public company, recording a $15.7 billion write-down tied to its EV business for the fiscal year ending March 2026. The panel interprets this as effectively the cancellation of Honda's EV sub-brand, a retreat back to hybrids. Matthew argues legacy OEMs face a structural trap: those that invested heavily in EVs took massive write-downs, while those like the Japanese brands that invested little missed the transition entirely. He adds that even if a legacy OEM built a Tesla-quality EV, they would still face a 15% dealership cut that makes them mathematically unable to compete on margin.

8. Tesla is exhibiting at China's major appliance and electronics expo in Shanghai, showing Optimus, Cybertruck, Model YL, and Model 3. Brian, who recently obtained a Chinese driver's license and drove in China, notes Tesla continues to sell strongly despite being 30-100% more expensive than compelling domestic Chinese alternatives, because quality and software differentiation are recognized by buyers. He emphasizes that Tesla's flat China market share reflects factory capacity limits, not demand weakness.

9. CA Logistics partnered with Tesla to pilot the long-range Tesla Semi on the US West Coast, achieving up to 500 miles of range on a single charge under load. Brian references a Canadian real-world study (not a controlled test) where fleet operators running routes for two to three months found the electric Cascadia — which costs over 500,000 Canadian dollars — still made financial sense versus ICE alternatives. He argues the Tesla Semi, priced far below that, makes the competitive case even stronger on a pure dollars-and-cents basis.

10. Elon spoke at a conference and confirmed Optimus V4 design is locked down and looking strong. Joe notes leaked images of what appears to be Optimus 3 (called "Athena") look like a person in a suit. The panel argues Tesla's manufacturing scale advantage — the same principle behind the Ford Model T's dominance — is the key differentiator versus the many other companies now building humanoid robots.

11. On a potential SpaceX-Tesla merger, Matthew says it is inevitable long-term due to overlap and conflict-of-interest risks, but argues the timing is wrong now. SpaceX has risen from roughly $100 billion to near Tesla's current valuation over four to five years, while Tesla has been relatively flat. He believes Tesla shareholders would need at least two-thirds of any merged entity for it to be fair, and that a merger should wait until robotaxi is deployed and Tesla's autonomy value is unlocked. Brian partially pushes back, noting SpaceX is also on the verge of its own major unlock via Starship's payload capacity and potential space-based data centers.
