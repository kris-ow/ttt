This episode of Brighterwithherbert covers a cluster of Tesla and Elon Musk announcements from the weekend, spanning CyberCab production progress, Full Self-Driving software upgrades, robotaxi fleet growth, hiring expansion, and a surprise Cybertruck pricing move. Host Herbert is joined by Jeff Lutz, a multi-decade supply chain executive and former chief quality officer who now runs a high-tech manufacturing consulting firm. The discussion blends news reporting with supply chain and operational analysis.

1. CyberCab production vehicles spotted at Giga Texas. Drone footage captured by Joe Techmire showed three CyberCabs outside the Giga Texas facility, confirmed as production versions based on tire stickers and wheel designs matching those used in the production area. The vehicles had no steering wheels and no side mirrors, consistent with the final production configuration, and were observed near the crash testing facility, suggesting they are being prepared for structural and reliability validation ahead of the April start of production.

2. The pre-SOP manufacturing process explained. Jeff Lutz described the current phase as a convergence period between first-off-production and start of production, during which small "steel safe" changes are made to supplier tooling and factory integration simultaneously. These changes are batched into "recipe" builds — a small number of cars assembled under a defined set of modifications — which are then run through testing to chip away at remaining issues before the line rate is formally increased at SOP.

3. Voice commands coming to FSD; Grok integration underway. Elon Musk confirmed that FSD will soon accept natural language voice commands, allowing drivers to say things like "find a parking spot near the door" or "turn right at the next block" rather than using manual inputs. Jeff framed this as Tesla potentially being the first automaker to deploy a real large language model integrated into vehicle controls, with Grok already partially integrated; he expects Tesla's implementation to have lower latency and tighter safety guardrails than competitors, preventing commands that would cause unsafe maneuvers at speed.

4. FSD Supervised already recognizes hand gestures. Both Tesla Europe and Elon Musk confirmed that FSD Supervised responds to hand gestures from people outside the vehicle. A video from the Netherlands showed a CyberCab-era Tesla squeezing through a tight lane as the car responded to a person's hand signal. Jeff noted he has personally observed the system responding to police officers directing traffic, with the car waiting and then proceeding when the officer signals.

5. Robotaxi fleet at 395 vehicles, 8 unsupervised. The total fleet grew from roughly 390 to 395 over just a few days, with 306 cars operating in the Bay Area and 89 in Austin. The subset of fully unsupervised vehicles grew from 7 to 8, representing 18% of the 44-vehicle active fleet. Jeff noted that for Tesla to meet Elon's earnings call commitment to roughly double the fleet by end of quarter, they would need to reach just over 500 total — meaning a significant push is still required this week.

6. Why fleet growth is deliberately slow. Jeff explained that Tesla's internal constraint is its own probabilistic safety model: as fleet density increases, the probability of an incident rises unless software capability improves faster than the density. The team is actively crushing edge-case bugs before expanding further. A secondary constraint is operational capacity — charging logistics, vehicle cleaning, and service infrastructure all need to scale in parallel with the number of cars.

7. Misleading January crash headlines flagged as a reputational risk. Five low-speed incidents in January — all under 4 miles per hour and all parking-related — were reported by media as crashes, with some outlets characterizing Tesla as four times more dangerous than human drivers. Herbert and Jeff argued these were minor contact events, not meaningful safety failures, but expressed concern that the narrative is spreading and could deter riders from using the service. Both called on Tesla's PR team to respond more aggressively, noting that silence risks the misleading headlines becoming accepted public knowledge.

8. Hiring now spans 14 cities. Tesla posted a new Robotaxi Operations Manager role in Tampa, Florida, and additional AI Safety Operator positions in Vienna, Virginia, bringing the total number of cities where these roles are being filled to 14, up from 12 the prior week. Cities now include locations like Draper, Utah and Peabody, Massachusetts that had not previously been discussed. Jeff noted that many of these roles are full-time, suggesting employees will be transitioned into other service functions once the supervised phase gives way to fully unsupervised operations.

9. New Cybertruck trim at $59,990 offered for 10 days only. Tesla launched a lower-cost Cybertruck configuration at $59,990, with 325 miles of range, 7,500-pound towing capacity, and rear-wheel steering, but with notable deletions including smaller wheels, no rear screen, no vault storage compartment, and cloth seats. Elon stated the price is only available for 10 days and that continuation depends on demand levels observed during the window.

10. Jeff's supply chain read on the Cybertruck pricing. Jeff interpreted the 10-day window as likely serving two simultaneous purposes: clearing upstream piece-part inventory (what he called excess and obsolescence management) tied to supplier commitments, and price discovery to determine whether meaningful demand exists at the $60K level. If demand proves strong and sustained, Tesla could recalculate factory fixed-cost absorption and decide to make the price permanent. If not, Jeff speculated Tesla may redirect Cybertruck production toward an autonomous delivery or "robo-truck" use case, given Cybertruck's prior underperformance against sales forecasts at higher price points.
