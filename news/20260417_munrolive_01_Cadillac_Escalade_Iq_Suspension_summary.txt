This video from Munro Live features Kevin conducting a high-level chassis and suspension review of the 2026 Cadillac Escalade IQ electric vehicle, priced at just over $150,000. The review compares the Escalade IQ's underbody architecture extensively to the Silverado EV and GMC Hummer EV, noting strong similarities across the Ultium platform family. A more detailed teardown video is promised once a fully disassembled unit arrives from A21.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Platform Consistency Across GM Ultium Lineup:** Kevin notes that the front suspension of the Escalade IQ — including aluminum control arms, strut pickup points, steering rack placement, and the front drive module — appears to be nearly a carbon copy of the Silverado EV. This contrasts with earlier observations between the Hummer EV and Silverado EV, where differences were primarily found in the rear cradle shape and control arm orientation. The front cradle on the Escalade IQ is entirely steel, which Kevin finds somewhat surprising given that lower-volume Cadillac platforms (like the Omega-based CT5) historically made greater use of nodal castings and aluminum subframes.

2. **Vehicle Weight and Structural Scaling:** The Escalade IQ is estimated to weigh just over 9,000 lbs, and Kevin observes how this mass cascades through the entire chassis system — manifesting in oversized ball joints, large strut pickup points with dual mounts for quality verification, and very large structural cross-sections throughout. Despite being a unibody structure, the size of the structural members is described as reminiscent of full-size body-on-frame trucks, with extensive gusseting at key nodes to manage load inputs.

3. **SORB Crash Safety Architecture:** Kevin discusses the vehicle's approach to Small Overlap Rigid Barrier (SORB) crash protection, noting that the Escalade IQ's front rails splay outward but do not extend into the 25% overlap threshold in the way more "offensive" SORB countermeasures would. He theorizes that the height and mass of the Ultium battery pack, combined with how the cradle overlaps the battery and the large torque box section, may allow GM to manage crash energy through the broader body structure rather than adding dedicated front-end reinforcement — which he views as a potential weight and cost advantage, though he acknowledges the vehicle's low sales volume makes IIHS SORB testing unlikely.

4. **Air Suspension and Rear Corner Layout:** The Escalade IQ features air suspension at the rear, with the air bladder and dampener mounted separately and independently. The upper control arm has complex geometry with a tight goose-neck profile, necessitating aluminum construction to achieve the required radii. The rear also features rear-wheel steering, a forged knuckle, a large stamped steel lower control arm, and a cast ball joint mount welded in — a solution Kevin associates with Toyota's approach when stamping alone cannot achieve the required geometry. Brake dust shields are noted as rolled stamped aluminum, with Kevin observing that the aluminum-versus-steel decision for such shields is an ongoing industry debate, especially given current aluminum pricing.

5. **Manufacturing and Joining Processes:** Kevin highlights several manufacturing details of note. The underbody protection closing out the battery pack bottom uses a layered, wafer-like sandwich structure joined via laser welding — described as cleaner and more plant-preferred than MIG welding despite slightly higher cell costs. MIG welding is also present for one-sided assembly operations on the motor bay rails. Tailor-welded blanks are used to transition between harder and softer material grades along the front rails, allowing controlled deformation during frontal impacts. Dual-shot plastic ducting for the cooling system is retained, contrasting with an industry trend toward single-shot thin-wall sections. Castellization (material cutouts for weight savings, drainage, and panel nesting) is noted as a GM practice visible on some joints.

6. **Wheel Design and Customization Opportunity:** Kevin observes that the Escalade IQ's wheels appear to be cast and then machined, with painted faces, clear coating, and plastic insert accents. He notes that the current all-black treatment doesn't fully exploit the potential of this approach, and suggests that offering swappable plastic accent pieces in different finishes (e.g., gunmetal, metallic) could provide customers greater customization options at low cost — a trend Munro has been tracking across the industry.

## Market & Competition

1. **GM Platform Strategy and Volume Assumptions:** Kevin speculates that the decision to use an all-steel front cradle — rather than the aluminum castings seen on lower-volume Cadillac platforms — may reflect an original expectation of significantly higher production volumes for the Ultium-based trucks and SUVs, since the investment in stamping tooling is more economical at scale. This raises implicit questions about whether GM's volume projections for the Escalade IQ and related vehicles have been met.
