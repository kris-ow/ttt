This video covers the rapidly evolving autonomous vehicle landscape, focusing on Tesla's robotic taxi expansion, Waymo's multi-city push, and Wave AI's major funding round, all against the backdrop of a significant upcoming federal regulatory event.

Key Points:

1. NHTSA has announced a major autonomous vehicle safety public meeting on March 10th at the US Department of Transportation headquarters. The full-day event will include keynote addresses from DOT leadership, three executive panel discussions, and workshops on potential federal frameworks for safe AV development, testing, and deployment.

2. Tesla is currently operating robotaxis in the San Francisco Bay Area (with safety drivers) and Austin (approximately 8 unsupervised vehicles). The company plans to expand to seven additional cities before July, with validation vehicles spotted in Miami and multiple other regions nationwide. Tesla holds explicit regulatory approval in California, Texas, Arizona, Nevada, Florida, and Georgia.

3. Waymo now operates in 10 US cities after simultaneously launching invite-only service in Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, and Orlando — the first time the company opened multiple cities at once. Waymo has roughly 3,000 vehicles but faces a structural business model problem: analysts at New Street Research note Waymo loses money on each car beyond approximately 500 per city, limiting its ability to scale within individual markets. Waymo has raised $16 billion but remains unprofitable. Future expansion targets include Denver, London, and Washington DC.

4. Waymo's operational inefficiencies are noted, including all vehicles returning to base simultaneously at end of day rather than trickling back based on demand patterns, suggesting a lack of network-level intelligence.

5. Wave AI, a UK-based startup founded in 2017, raised $1.2 billion (some figures cited as $1.8 billion in the discussion) from Nvidia, Uber, Nissan, Mercedes-Benz, and Stellantis. Wave uses an end-to-end neural network approach similar to Tesla's and has tested in over 500 cities across Europe, Asia, and North America, integrating into 10 different vehicle types.

6. Wave's business model is distinctly different: it plans to license its AI software to automakers and fleets rather than operate its own robotaxi service or sell vehicles. This positions Wave as a non-threatening supplier for automakers who are reluctant to license from Tesla or Waymo, both seen as potential competitors.

7. A parallel development in China: Xpeng (Xiaopeng) has been announced as the FSD software provider for Volkswagen, and Huawei's system is already licensed by multiple Chinese automakers.

8. Mercedes-Benz's Level 3 autonomy feature was discussed as an example of marketing over substance — restricted to straight highways, speeds under 40 mph, daytime, and requiring a lead vehicle. Mercedes has since withdrawn the feature.

Key Takeaways:

The three emerging business models are Tesla (vertical integration, own cars and network), Waymo (buy third-party vehicles and operate a fleet), and Wave (pure software licensing). The hosts argue Tesla's slow-and-steady scaling approach, combined with lower operating costs and vertical integration, positions it to undercut Waymo on price once comparable fleet sizes are reached in shared markets. The March 10th DOT event is viewed as a historically significant regulatory moment signaling autonomous vehicles are transitioning from novelty to mainstream infrastructure.
