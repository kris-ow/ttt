Larry Goldberg, a serial entrepreneur and venture capital investor, joins Herbert to map out what they see as a coordinated convergence across Elon Musk's companies — Tesla, SpaceX, XAI, and X. The central thesis is that these companies, once disparate bets, are now interconnecting through shared first-principles engineering, and that a formal merger between Tesla and SpaceX is not just plausible but likely imminent following a SpaceX IPO. The conversation covers the near-term investment case, the longer-term moonshot timelines, and why product management — not just technology — is the real argument for consolidation.

1. The Elon ecosystem is converging from first principles. Larry argues that each of Musk's companies — Tesla, SpaceX, XAI, Neuralink, Boring — was built from first principles, and as a result they naturally interconnect. What looked like separate bets is now coalescing into a unified technological vision, spanning robots, AI, chips, satellites, and space-based compute.

2. Larry's thinking on consolidation evolved from a partial idea to a full merger thesis. He initially proposed that Musk sell shares in his private ventures into Tesla in exchange for Tesla stock — a way to improve Musk's Tesla ownership while retaining control of subsidiaries. He then realized that was insufficient and began advocating for combining all companies into a single entity, inspired by what he and Herbert witnessed at a Tesla shareholders meeting where the AI convergence across companies became visible.

3. Dave Lee proposed a holding company structure roughly four years ago — Larry now partially endorses the spirit of that idea. At the time, Larry dismissed it because the products had little relationship. After the shareholders meeting, he changed his view: the products are now deeply interrelated and a true operational merger, not just a holding structure, is the right move.

4. SpaceX merged with XAI shortly after Larry published his thesis, validating the direction. XAI had raised approximately $20–25 billion in a funding round, and SpaceX separately raised significant capital. Within weeks, Musk combined them. Larry sees this as confirmation that a Tesla-SpaceX merger is the next logical step.

5. The SpaceX IPO is expected around June 28, 2026, with a Tesla-SpaceX merger to follow within 3 to 6 months. Larry predicts a standard lockup period of around 3 months post-IPO before a merger can proceed. His estimate is that a unified single entity — encompassing at least Tesla, SpaceX, and XAI — could be reality by end of 2026 or early 2027. Neuralink and Boring Company are excluded from the near-term merger because they have not yet reached sufficient revenue scale.

6. The core argument for merger is product management coherence, not just synergy. Larry is emphatic that integrated product management — with clear lines of authority reporting to one person — is the defining factor in whether complex products succeed or fail. He points to Tesla's extreme vertical integration as proof of Musk's philosophy. Contracts between sister companies cannot replicate the decisiveness of a unified product team; competing financial priorities, supply chain decisions, and management hierarchies create compounding failure modes.

7. Space-based compute and lunar manufacturing are real but distant — at minimum 5 years, potentially 10 to 25 years out. Larry walks through the logical sequencing: experiments must begin in 2027, test satellite launches follow, meaningful space-based inference compute arrives perhaps 5 years from now, and lunar manufacturing is far beyond that. He uses Tesla's lithium refining plant in Texas — which took roughly 3 years to build just 150 miles from headquarters — as a reference point for how long similar construction on the moon would take, with far less support infrastructure and working with regolith samples measured in grams, not tons.

8. Starlink is the near-term financial engine that makes the combined entity viable right now. Larry cites analysis from the firm Markcap 33 (MAC 33) projecting that Starlink can reach $100 billion in annual revenue in a matter of years, not decades. Crucially, a very high proportion of that revenue falls directly to the bottom line, unlike vehicle or robotaxi revenue. Starlink is currently at roughly 10 million subscribers and growing faster than Tesla was growing when the Model 3 launched.

9. Starlink's V3 satellites, targeted for Starship launch this year, will transform the network's capability. Each V3 satellite carries approximately 16 to 64 times the bandwidth of existing satellites. Each Starship mission can deploy 64 V3 satellites. Even without full Starship reusability, V3 launches are projected to be economically viable. Larry's revenue trajectory: roughly $10 billion now, $15 billion next year, then rapid doubling toward $30 billion and $60 billion as V3 coverage scales.

10. Starlink is positioned to compete directly with all global telecom companies. Southwest Airlines, United, Alaska Airlines, British Airways, Emirates, and others have already adopted Starlink for in-flight connectivity. Larry believes Delta will eventually follow. He notes that Amazon's Project Kuiper is not gaining traction because it cannot secure enough launch capacity. Direct-to-cell voice capability — not just messaging — becomes possible at V3 bandwidth levels.

11. A future X edge-computing device will bypass cellular networks entirely. Larry describes a device Tesla is developing that communicates directly to Starlink satellites, eliminating telecom network overhead in both cost and latency. This is presented as another integration point between the companies that cannot be achieved as efficiently across separate corporate entities.
