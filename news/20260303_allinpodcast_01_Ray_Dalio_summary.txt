Ray Dalio returns to the All-In Podcast for a wide-ranging conversation covering the US debt crisis, gold and Bitcoin performance, AI's economic implications, and the broader historical cycles shaping geopolitics and domestic politics. The discussion is framed against the backdrop of the Trump administration's second year and ongoing failures to meaningfully reduce the deficit.

DEBT AND FISCAL SITUATION

1. The US government spends roughly $7 trillion while taking in approximately $5 trillion — a 40% spending deficit. Total debt stands at roughly 600% of annual revenue.
2. The 2026 deficit-to-GDP ratio sits at approximately 6%, far from the 3% target Dalio previously identified as a stabilization threshold. Half the $2 trillion annual deficit consists of interest payments alone.
3. The US must also roll over $9 trillion of maturing debt, creating significant supply/demand pressure in Treasury markets. Foreign buyers (about one-third of demand) face both portfolio concentration concerns and geopolitical risk that may reduce their appetite.
4. Dalio believes a "3% solution" — combining modest adjustments to taxes, spending, and interest rates — remains viable and has bipartisan support, but executing it is politically near-impossible at this stage of the cycle.

DOGE AND GOVERNMENT EFFICIENCY

5. Dalio views the DOGE effort as structurally extremely difficult — not necessarily wrong in concept, but hampered by democratic constraints, election timelines, and a polarized public that resists any painful cuts. Examples like school lunch program reductions generated backlash that undermined the effort.
6. Government fraud and waste (e.g., reported billions in fake Minnesota daycare programs) are symptoms of the cycle's late stage, not surprises — Dalio compares government efficiency to the DMV.

GOLD, BITCOIN, AND MONETARY DYNAMICS

7. Gold rose from approximately $2,900 to $5,200 per ounce since the prior conversation — driven by central banks diversifying away from dollar-denominated assets, geopolitical risk, and structural recognition of fiat currency debasement risk.
8. Dalio frames gold as the second-largest reserve currency held by central banks, not a speculative commodity. He recommends 5–15% portfolio allocation in gold regardless of directional view, purely as a diversifier when other assets decline.
9. Bitcoin underperformed gold (down roughly 25% in the same period) for several reasons: lack of transaction privacy limits central bank adoption, high correlation with tech stocks means it gets sold during liquidity squeezes, and quantum computing poses a future vulnerability.
10. Silver has monetary history but has increasingly taken on a speculative character — Dalio is cautious about it.

TARIFFS AND TRADE

11. Dalio defends tariffs as historically legitimate revenue tools — historically the primary government revenue source — and argues economists incorrectly exclude tax increases from inflation calculations.
12. Tariffs alone cannot replace income taxes; the math doesn't work and they are regressive in nature. However, they are valid as part of a broader plan to rebuild domestic manufacturing and reduce dangerous trade/capital account dependencies.
13. The Supreme Court's recent overturning of tariffs imposed under the Emergency Economic Powers Act is noted as a significant constraint.

AI AND TECHNOLOGY BUBBLE RISK

14. Dalio warns that betting on AI stocks is not the same as betting on AI technology. Historically, most companies fail even when the underlying technology succeeds — the dot-com bubble and late 1920s tech booms are cited as precedents.
15. His central thesis on AI: "AI is eating everything — and it might eat itself," meaning AI may not generate sufficient profits to justify current valuations, particularly given Chinese competition.
16. China's state-directed model treats AI like a public utility — potentially free and open-source — prioritizing productivity gains over profit. This creates a fundamental competitive asymmetry that US profit-dependent companies cannot easily counter.

POLITICAL AND SOCIAL CYCLE

17. Dalio places the US at "stage five" of a historical cycle, characterized by bad finances, large wealth/values gaps, irreconcilable political differences, and simultaneous external threats.
18. He predicts Democrats will likely retake the House in midterm elections, further gridlocking governance. He notes the US is in a condition resembling historical analogues like Julius Caesar's Rome.
19. Three conditions for a successful country, per Dalio's historical analysis: educate children well (both productively and civilly), maintain an orderly civil environment for competition, and avoid civil or international war.
20. On constitutional reform: Dalio suggests no specific rule changes would have helped — the core problem is human short-termism (the "marshmallow test"). His primary recommendation is simply to read history.

CONCLUSION: Dalio's overall assessment is that the US is on an unsustainable fiscal trajectory, the political system is too fractured to self-correct quickly, gold remains the most reliable store of value in this environment, AI presents both transformative promise and significant bubble/profit risk, and the path forward requires a strong, bipartisan leader capable of forcing difficult reforms — a person and scenario he views as extremely rare.
