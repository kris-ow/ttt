He argues that adoption scales with utility, and if Optimus proves as transformative in factories, homes, elderly care, and medical settings as the smartphone was relative to the dumb phone, the 1–10 billion figure is not unrealistic.

Elon stated that AI5 delivers 50 times more compute than the current generation. Jeff Lutz notes that Tesla has been designing its own inference chips (Hardware 2, 3, 4, and now AI5) for approximately 10 years under its in-house chip design team based in the Bay Area and Austin, giving Tesla a decade-long head start in purpose-built edge inference silicon that competitors purchasing off-the-shelf Nvidia chips cannot easily replicate.

## Market & Competition
1. Jeff Lutz makes a detailed competitive moat argument centered on chip economics. Competitors building robotaxis or autonomous vehicles using the conventional supply chain must purchase chips that TSMC manufactures at 35–40% gross margin and that Nvidia designs at 70%+ gross margin — stacking enormous cost burdens on top of their own engineering costs. Tesla, by designing its own chips and eventually fabricating them internally through Terrafab, eliminates both margin layers entirely, creating a structural cost advantage that compounds over time across cars, robots, and AI infrastructure.
2. Jeff Lutz draws a battery supply chain analogy to explain Terrafab's strategic leverage. In 2014–2015, when Tesla announced Giga Nevada in a joint venture with Panasonic, the 4680 battery program was not yet delivering at its promised cost structure. Yet Tesla's credible threat of self-sufficiency gave it enormous negotiating leverage with external battery suppliers (CATL, BYD, and others), resulting in industry-leading EV gross margins without needing to perfect the 4680 first. Lutz predicts the same dynamic will play out in silicon: even before Terrafab reaches full production (which he places no earlier than 2028–2029), the credible threat of in-house fabrication will incentivize TSMC, Samsung, and others to offer Tesla better pricing and capacity reservations.
3. The video notes that lithography machines (from ASML) are pre-purchased 36–48 months in advance, cost $200–$330 million each, and require extensive time to install, calibrate, and bring to rated speed. Jeff Lutz cautions that not all procurement conversations will be public — Elon can and does require confidentiality from suppliers — so the absence of visible orders does not mean orders haven't been placed.
