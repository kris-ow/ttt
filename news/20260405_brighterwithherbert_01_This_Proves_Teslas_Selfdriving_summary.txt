This video from brighterwithherbert features host Herbert and guest Larry Goldberg, a serial entrepreneur and venture capital investor, discussing Tesla's fundamental architectural advantage in autonomous driving — specifically that FSD runs entirely on-device without requiring internet connectivity. The core thesis is that recent outages exposing Waymo and BYD's cloud dependency, combined with admissions from the Ford and Uber CEOs, demonstrate that Tesla's self-driving gap is wider than most investors realize. The conversation also covers the end of Model S and Model X production, FSD adoption trends, and the strategic significance of the Cybercab.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **On-device FSD vs. cloud-dependent competitors:** During a recent San Francisco power outage, Waymo robotaxis froze dead in intersections because they depend on continuous internet connectivity to data centers for processing. Simultaneously, BYD's robotaxi fleet in China froze on active highways during a system outage, leaving passengers stranded. Tesla's FSD, by contrast, continued operating normally because all inference runs locally on the vehicle using Tesla's onboard AI chip. Larry Goldberg frames this as a fundamental architectural decision Tesla made from the outset — prioritizing safety, sentient capability, and user confidence — while competitors applied available technology (LiDAR, radar, high-definition cloud-hosted maps) without fully thinking through the problem they were solving.

2. **FSD milestone — 9 billion supervised miles:** Tesla's FSD supervised miles crossed 9 billion, a threshold described as significant in the context of Elon Musk's previously stated target of approximately 10 billion miles. The hosts note the 10 billion figure may have referred specifically to training miles rather than supervised FSD miles, but regardless, they estimate Tesla could reach 10 billion within a couple of months of the video's publication (around mid-2026).

3. **FSD adoption and retention data:** Citing data from analyst Ben Shippers, who uses fleet telemetry data via a test lab app, the hosts report that organic FSD subscriber retention among paying customers (excluding trials, promos, and discounts) is 90% after month one. Among retained January subscribers, FSD usage as a share of all drives rose from 38% in January to 44% in March 2026. Nearly half of all FSD drives now complete themselves without intervention. Larry Goldberg independently corroborates this, noting he now uses FSD 100% of the time, and his wife has gone from 65% to approximately 88% usage. Herbert reports 99% FSD usage.

4. **Current FSD version behavior:** Larry Goldberg references the current release as version 14.2.2.5, describing it as a meaningful behavioral shift for users. He notes that manual takeovers in his household now occur almost exclusively when choosing a preferred parking space in a parking lot, and that the car handles his tight driveway and garage turns autonomously. He also recounts his son driving a Model Y from Kerry to Columbus, Ohio and back — a roughly 7–8 hour drive each way through the West Virginia mountains in heavy snow — without a single manual takeover.

5. **Chinese influencer FSD comparison to Huawei:** A Chinese influencer who traveled from China to California to test Tesla FSD firsthand concluded that under normal weather and road conditions, Tesla's vision-based routing is "first class." He noted Tesla outperformed Huawei's smart driving system in specific scenarios, such as reversing to avoid a parked vehicle and navigating shortcuts through residential roads. He described acceleration as crisp and decisive, stated that physically removing the steering wheel would present no real obstacle at current capability levels, and expressed hope that China would soon approve Tesla FSD for domestic operation to spur L3 competition. Elon Musk replied confirming the assessment was accurate.

6. **Safety data and insurance implications:** The video cites a statistic that Tesla FSD supervised drivers experience a collision every 5.3 million miles, compared to 660,000 miles for regular drivers without any safety features. Elon Musk is quoted suggesting that insurance pricing is the true signal of safety. Lemonade is noted as willing to reduce premiums by 50% for FSD users. Larry Goldberg reports earning ongoing discounts from Allstate because FSD produces driving behavior that scores well on Allstate's safe-driving data tracking program. Jeff Lutz is cited as reporting that Tesla insurance premiums fell to the point where savings cover the cost of his FSD subscription after he began using FSD 99% of the time in Chicago.

## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab as cost transformation catalyst:** Larry Goldberg argues that the Cybercab is the "magic bullet" for fleet economics, characterizing it as reducing per-mile costs from approximately $1.50 per mile to $0.25 per mile. He references analysis from Cern Basher and ARK Invest as supporting this view. He emphasizes that the Cybercab and its associated autonomous service infrastructure are specifically designed for automated fleet management, and that simply converting existing Tesla vehicles into a managed fleet is not economically viable from a competitive standpoint.

2. **Uber CEO's position on Tesla partnership:** The Uber CEO (Dara Khosrowshahi) stated in a recent interview that Uber already has tens of thousands of Teslas on its platform with some drivers using FSD, and that Uber would "love to have those Teslas on our platform" once the camera-only approach achieves unsupervised safety standards. He confirmed by 2029 Uber aims to facilitate more autonomous and robotaxi rides than anyone else in the world, but left open whether a formal Tesla partnership would materialize. Larry Goldberg argues Tesla should consider selling Cybercabs to Uber given production scale, though he flags that Uber lacks the service infrastructure to manage large electric fleets globally — a gap he sees as Uber's key weakness in the robotaxi transition.

3. **Tesla's own robotaxi app and X integration:** Herbert notes Tesla is clearly building its own robotaxi app and does not anticipate demand being a constraint as Tesla expands globally. He draws a parallel to WhatsApp integrating a robotaxi service directly into its app in China, arguing that X (formerly Twitter) could similarly integrate Tesla's robotaxi service as a tab — eliminating the need for users to download a separate app and giving Tesla a powerful distribution channel.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **End of Model S and Model X production:** Elon Musk posted that custom orders for the Model S and Model X have ended, with only remaining inventory available — Model X reportedly already sold out of inventory. He announced an official ceremony to mark the end of an era, expressing personal attachment to both models. Larry Goldberg notes the Model S was named Motor Trend Car of the Year in 2013 and was subsequently called the most important car of the last 70 years by the same publication.

2. **Tesla's cumulative profitability vs. EV competitors:** The video displays a chart showing cumulative cash flow since founding for pure EV manufacturers. All competitors remain deeply in negative territory, while Tesla is the only pure EV manufacturer to have turned the corner into cumulative profitability. Rivian is specifically called out as having lost tens of billions of dollars with no clear path to recovery.

## Market & Competition

1. **Ford CEO's skunk works team and competitive timeline:** The Ford CEO disclosed in a recent interview that Ford established a skunk works team in California four years ago, staffed primarily with Formula 1 and Tesla engineers — with the CEO's own badge not working in the building. The resulting all-electric vehicle, designed to compete with Model Y and Model 3, will be shown later in 2026 and launched in 2027. Larry Goldberg argues Ford wasted approximately $13 billion it could not afford on this effort, and that had Ford collaborated with Tesla years ago, it could have been competitive much earlier. Both hosts agree that by the time Ford's vehicle launches, FSD capability will have made hardware-level EV competition secondary.

2. **Waymo's architectural dependency as systemic flaw:** Larry Goldberg frames Waymo's reliance on cloud connectivity not as a minor technical issue but as a fundamental systems architecture mistake he has seen repeatedly in corporate IT — applying available technology rather than solving from first principles for the actual problem. He contrasts this with Tesla's approach of designing the car to be entirely self-contained from the start.
