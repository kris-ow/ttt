This panel discussion from the "brighterwithherbert" channel brings together four Tesla bull investors — host Herbert, Alexander Mertz, Jeff Lutz, and Cernin Basher — to analyze three major developments they believe are significant for Tesla shareholders: regulatory clarity on Cybercab production limits, European hiring activity, and a major AI chip order expansion. The overarching thesis is that Tesla's autonomous vehicle and robotics ambitions are accelerating faster than the market recognizes, with several perceived obstacles turning out to be non-issues.

Key Points:

1. The 2,500 Cybercab production cap is a myth. There has long been a widespread belief — shared by Tesla bears and bulls alike — that a NHTSA regulation capped autonomous vehicles without steering wheels or pedals at 2,500 units. The panel clarifies that this limit only applies to manufacturers seeking an exemption from federal motor vehicle safety standards, typically for experimental or prototype vehicles. A 2022 regulatory update clarified that if a manufacturer can self-certify that a vehicle without a steering column meets all applicable federal motor vehicle safety standards, there is no production cap at all. Tesla has not applied for such an exemption, which the panel interprets as a strong signal of confidence that the Cybercab can fully self-certify. Drone footage from independent observer Joe Tegmire shows Tesla is already producing Cybercabs and subjecting them to crash testing, which is consistent with the self-certification pathway.

2. Zoox (Amazon-owned) serves as an instructive comparison case. Zoox designed their robotaxi with passengers facing each other and no front crumple zone, which is not compliant with federal safety rules. They self-certified anyway, ran into trouble with NHTSA nine months later, and spent roughly three years in dialogue before finally receiving a formal exemption. Tesla's Cybercab, by contrast, is designed more like a conventional vehicle — passengers facing forward, traditional crumple zones — making self-certification far more achievable. The panel argues Tesla deliberately chose this design approach over a more futuristic pod-style vehicle specifically to avoid the regulatory complications Zoox encountered.

3. Cybercab production has started but the ramp is deliberate, not stalled. Approximately 430 Cybercabs have been identified by a robo-taxi tracker using license plate recognition, but both Elon Musk and Tesla engineering VP Ashok Elluswamy stated on an earnings call that 500 vehicles were already deployed. Analyst Brian Wang estimates the tracker undercounts actual vehicles by roughly 30%, consistent with Waymo's tracker showing 130 vehicles when Waymo itself reported 200 in Austin. Five minor incidents (four under 4 mph, one at 17 mph into a fixed pole) were reported to NHTSA in January and generated outsized negative headlines, including claims Tesla's robotaxi was four times more dangerous than human drivers. The panel believes Tesla intentionally slowed the unsupervised rollout after that media reaction while refining edge case handling — such as a school bus stop sign being misread when the bus is perpendicular. Only eight vehicles have been confirmed operating unsupervised. The panel expects convergence of hardware production and software readiness within 30 to 60 days.

4. Tesla is hiring vehicle operators and autonomous vehicle engineers across Europe. New job postings show Tesla hiring vehicle operators in Croatia, Greece, Portugal, Slovenia, and the Netherlands, plus an electrical design engineer for robotaxi/autonomous vehicles based in Berlin. These countries are new additions beyond previously known markets like France, Germany, and Italy, suggesting Tesla is expanding its supervised FSD ride-along program to new markets as a precursor to full deployment. A NHTSA announcement on autonomous vehicles was noted as imminent at the time of recording (March 10, 2026).

5. The IG Metall union lost ground at Giga Berlin, opening expansion possibilities. The union's support fell from 40% to 31% in the 2026 vote. Elon Musk had previously stated he would make Giga Berlin the largest factory in Europe and manufacture Optimus, Cybercab, and Semi there if employees and the German government were supportive. The panel sees this result as clearing a path for that expansion, though they note geopolitical considerations may make Tesla more cautious about non-US investment for now.

6. Tesla nearly doubled its Samsung AI6 chip wafer order. Tesla placed an additional order for 24,000 wafers per month from Samsung's Texas fab, bringing the total to 40,000 wafers per month. The panel notes these are capacity reservations, not orders for a finished chip design (AI6 hasn't been formally released), and that Elon has secured this capacity because the same chip architecture is intended for Dojo, FSD, robotaxi systems, and Optimus robots. Having three distinct major product lines consuming the same chip gives Tesla an unusually high certainty of demand, making it an attractive customer for Samsung. A Korean news outlet reported the Samsung Taylor, Texas fab is delayed by six months, which the panel speculates may actually be partly caused by the complexity of accommodating Tesla's expanded order.

7. Elon Musk stated Tesla will be among the first companies to achieve AGI, and the first in humanoid/atom-shaping form. The panel interprets "atom-shaping form" as referring to robots capable of physical manipulation at a molecular or atomic level — potentially including manufacturing equipment like lithography machines for chip production, or more speculatively, matter assembly. Jeff Lutz argues Tesla's AI is fundamentally different in quality from large language models: FSD operates at an infinitesimally low error rate under real-world conditions, whereas current LLMs demonstrate 5 to 10 percent error rates on complex reasoning tasks. The panel contrasts vision-to-action neural nets (which understand physical context) with word-prediction models (which can fail simple contextual logic tests, such as being told to recommend walking to a car wash).

8. Speculation about a SpaceX-Tesla merger accelerated. Peter Diamandis posted that SpaceX plans to IPO at a $1.75 trillion valuation, with a March 2026 confidentiality filing. Elon reportedly replied positively without correcting the premise. Alexander Mertz reiterates his thesis that SpaceX will IPO (he predicts June 9, 2026), then use its elevated publicly traded valuation to acquire Tesla three to five months later, offering Tesla shareholders a premium to secure the necessary votes. The panel notes that Gwen Shotwell, long-time SpaceX president and arguably Elon's top executive, is now representing XAI in public forums, including announcing a 1.2 gigawatt power commitment for XAI's data center at the Tennessee-Mississippi border. The panel sees her expanded role as consistent with a future merger structure.

9. Tesla China risk is real but declining in long-term materiality. Currently about 50% of Tesla's production capacity is in China, generating approximately 22% of revenues due to lower local pricing. About 95% of inputs are locally sourced, with roughly one-third of output exported to Europe and Canada. The panel acknowledges heightened geopolitical risk in the near term but argues that Tesla's future growth — robotaxi, Optimus, AI infrastructure — is predominantly US-centric and far less dependent on China than the current car business. Cernin Basher states that in a worst-case scenario where Tesla China shuts entirely, the impact on a 10-year horizon would be "negligible."
