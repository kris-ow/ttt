This video is a casual conversational interview between Brian (Futurazza) and Will (Tesla Jigsaw), a UK-based EV advocate, discussing how rising fuel prices in the UK are making electric vehicles increasingly attractive to mainstream consumers. The conversation covers the cost comparison between petrol and electric driving, available EV incentives in the UK, the state of the used EV market, and the regulatory landscape pushing the UK toward full electrification by 2030.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **UK Petrol Prices Creating Strong EV Value Proposition:** Fuel costs in the UK have reached approximately £85 to fill up the average petrol family car, with diesel exceeding £100 per fill. Will notes that driving on home overnight electricity tariffs is roughly a tenth of the cost of petrol, making the financial case for switching to an EV extremely compelling for anyone with a driveway. Brian adds context for US viewers that the UK petrol price works out to approximately $7.63 per gallon based on a price of £1.52 per liter at Asda, with the pound trading at roughly $1.32–$1.35.

2. **Used EV Market in the UK Is Expanding and Affordable:** Will highlights that a used Model 3 can be purchased for under £10,000 on the secondhand market, and basic EV runarounds (BMW i3, Nissan Leaf, Renault Zoe) are available for as little as £2,000. He notes that his own used 2019 California-built Model 3 would have served his needs indefinitely, and that he and his household recently upgraded to a Model 3 Juniper (premium rear-wheel drive). Brian echoes that affordable used EVs in the $20,000–$25,000 range challenge the persistent myth that EVs are only for wealthy buyers.

3. **Real-World Savings Case Study:** Will shares the example of a friend driving a Volvo S60 2L petrol who spends just under £400 per month on fuel. Will calculated that switching to a Model 3 with home charging would save approximately £360 per month — enough to cover a PCP (personal contract purchase) finance payment on a Tesla with money left over. Even a Model 3 owner relying solely on the Supercharger network was still saving several hundred pounds per year compared to running a petrol car.

4. **UK EV Incentives — Electric Car Grant (ECG):** The UK's Electric Car Grant, launched in 2025, provides an automatic dealer discount on new battery electric vehicles priced under £37,000. Band one (greenest manufacturing/sustainability standards) offers £3,750 off, with approximately nine qualifying models. Band two (meets minimum sustainability criteria) offers £1,500 off, with over 40 qualifying models. Tesla vehicles do not currently qualify because the base Model 3 starts at £37,950 — just above the threshold — though Tesla's manufacturing sustainability would otherwise meet the criteria.

5. **Tesla's Own Incentive Response:** In lieu of qualifying for the ECG, Tesla is offering a £3,750 trade-in bonus (on top of the vehicle's trade-in value) on all new inventory Model 3 and Model Y vehicles, excluding performance variants. This offer runs from April to June 2026 with short delivery deadlines. It stacks with 0% finance available on non-performance base versions, and an additional £500 discount is available via referral links.

6. **Home Charging Incentives Are Limited for Homeowners:** Will explains that UK homeowners receive no government grant for home charger installation. However, renters, flat residents, and landlords can claim up to £500 per socket, and a workplace charger scheme offers up to £500 per socket for a maximum of 40 sockets per business, extended through March 2027. Octopus Energy is highlighted as a major player offering EV leasing, heat pumps, and public charging solutions including the Electroverse card, which provides 8% off charging across most of the UK's rapid charging network. A scheme called "Turbocharge" (via Octopus) installs charging points on pavements outside homes or on nearby lamp posts.

7. **VAT on Public Charging Nearing Reduction:** Brian and Will discuss the long-running campaign by the late Quentin Wilson and Faircharge to reduce the VAT rate on public EV charging from 20% down to 5% — matching the reduced rate applied to home electricity. A recent UK court ruling appears to have pointed toward adopting the domestic 5% VAT rate for public charging, suggesting this long-sought change may be imminent or already in motion.

## Market & Competition

1. **UK ZEV Mandate Sets 2030 Deadline for New Petrol/Diesel Sales:** The UK's Zero Emission Vehicle (ZEV) mandate requires no new petrol or diesel car sales from 2030, a date that was previously shifted to 2035 before being moved back to 2030, creating significant supply chain uncertainty for automakers. A rising ZEV sales target requires 33% of manufacturer vehicle sales to be battery electric vehicles by 2026, with financial penalties for non-compliance. A pay-per-mile road charge for EVs is also being discussed for introduction by 2028, and EVs will begin paying Vehicle Excise Duty (road tax) going forward.

2. **Shifting Public Sentiment Toward EVs:** Will observes that EV adoption is visibly growing in his local area of Market Harborough, England, with at least 10 EVs on his street alone. He notes that geopolitical tensions involving Iran and resulting fuel price volatility are accelerating consumer interest in switching to electric. Brian notes that persistent misconceptions (range anxiety, explosion risk, battery replacement costs) remain a barrier but are slowly eroding as EVs become more common and financially appealing. The UK lacks a major domestic auto manufacturing base to protect, which has generally resulted in lower EV import barriers compared to other markets.
