The video centers on a significant regulatory discovery relevant to Tesla's Cybercab robotaxi program. Host Herbert Alm interviews Cern Basher, a Chartered Financial Analyst and founder of the investment advisory firm Brilliant Advice, to discuss research originally surfaced by an anonymous X user named "Genma JP" — claimed to work in a competitor's full self-driving division. Their central argument is that the widely cited 2,500-vehicle federal cap on autonomous vehicles without steering wheels or pedals does not apply to Tesla if the company pursues full safety compliance rather than seeking a regulatory exemption.

1. The 2,500-vehicle cap is an exemption, not a universal rule. The limit only applies when manufacturers apply for a temporary exemption under federal motor vehicle safety standards (FMVSS), typically sought by companies that cannot yet meet all standard requirements. Tesla has never publicly applied for this exemption, which itself requires a formal petition, a public comment period, and can take years to process. The speakers argue this is telling — why would Tesla be ramping Cybercab production without having applied if they believed they needed the exemption?

2. A 2022 NHTSA ruling (the "Occupant Protection Final Rule for Dual-Mode Vehicles") opened a pathway to unlimited production for fully autonomous vehicles. This 155-page ruling, finalized in March 2022, eliminated the requirement for steering wheels and pedals as long as vehicles continue to meet the same crashworthiness standards as conventional vehicles. The ruling also addressed "dual-mode" vehicles — those with stowable manual controls — allowing manufacturers to self-certify in both configurations. If Tesla engineers the Cybercab to meet all FMVSS crashworthiness requirements from day one, there is no production cap at all.

3. The three FMVSS series each carry different implications for the Cybercab. The 100-series covers crash avoidance (lighting, stability control, rear visibility, braking). The 200-series covers crashworthiness (airbags, roof crush, door integrity, occupant protection). The 300-series covers post-crash safety (fire risk, electrical shock protection, fuel containment). The 2022 rule change primarily addressed the 200-series, making it easier for vehicles without steering columns to comply because certain requirements — like limiting how far a steering column can intrude into the cabin during a crash — simply don't apply if the column doesn't exist.

4. Removing the steering wheel may actually improve, not hinder, FMVSS compliance. Several 200-series rules specifically govern steering column behavior in crashes. Without a steering column, those rules become irrelevant. The vehicle is then treated as having two forward-facing passengers, and standard occupant protection rules apply as they would in any passenger car. The speakers note the irony that critics argued the absence of controls was a compliance problem, when in some respects the absence makes compliance easier.

5. The rearview mirror requirement (Section 111) is a remaining ambiguity. The Cybercab has no rear window and is not designed to accommodate a conventional rearview mirror. However, the speakers argue Tesla could display rear camera footage on its large interior screen, which would functionally exceed what a mirror provides. Given the current favorable regulatory environment under the Trump administration, they believe NHTSA is unlikely to block production over this specific rule, which they describe as outdated and not written with autonomous vehicles in mind.

6. Case study — Zoox (Amazon): Zoox initially self-certified their autonomous vehicle as fully compliant in July 2022. NHTSA challenged this in March 2023, and after approximately three years of back-and-forth, Zoox was granted an exemption in September 2025 — but crucially, only up to the 2,500-unit cap. As a condition, Zoox was required to retract its self-certification claims. The speakers use this to illustrate both that self-certification is a viable initial strategy and that the exemption route is slow and limited.

7. Case study — GM/Honda Cruise Origin: Cruise applied for a 2,500-unit exemption in 2022 for a vehicle with lounge-style, face-to-face seating. NHTSA raised unresolved concerns around occupant positioning, airbag deployment logic, and emergency egress. Cruise withdrew the petition in 2024 without a final decision. The 2022 FMVSS rule changes explicitly exclude vehicles with non-forward-facing seating from the new compliance pathways, which the speakers suggest is precisely why Tesla designed the Cybercab to look like a conventional two-seat car rather than a lounge-style vehicle.

8. Cybercab's conventional form factor was a deliberate regulatory strategy. The speakers speculate that Elon Musk originally envisioned a Zoox-style vehicle with face-to-face seating and elevator-style doors, but the design was adapted to a forward-facing, conventional layout to ensure regulatory certification and enable unlimited production scaling. The robovan, expected 3-4 years later, is described as the product that will explore more unconventional configurations once pathways are better established.

9. Tesla's production evidence supports the self-certification thesis. Journalist Joe Techmir documented Cybercab units being moved to crash testing facilities — vehicles without steering wheels. The speakers note that you cannot self-certify a vehicle in a different configuration than the one being produced: crash testing steering-wheel-free vehicles is direct evidence Tesla intends to certify the car as built, not seek an exemption. Techmir's tour of Giga Texas also confirmed no storage of backup steering wheels or pedals, and workers found the speculation baffling.

10. Two separate regulatory layers govern deployment. The first is federal vehicle certification (FMVSS compliance), which the speakers believe Tesla is on track to clear. The second is state-level or city-level approval for autonomous commercial operations, which Waymo has already paved the way for in roughly 10+ cities. The speakers interpret Elon Musk's comments about regulations "keeping pace with production" as referring primarily to this second layer — city-by-city rollout approvals — rather than the federal vehicle cap.

Conclusion and recommendation: The speakers conclude that Tesla's strategy is full FMVSS compliance with no exemption needed, enabling theoretically unlimited Cybercab production. They describe the 2022 NHTSA rule change as "hiding in plain sight" since it was published and argue that Forbes and other critical outlets have been spreading what they call FUD (fear, uncertainty, doubt) by misrepresenting the regulatory landscape. They also briefly note that if U.S. regulatory hurdles emerged unexpectedly, other countries — citing Japan, South Korea, Europe, Australia, and New Zealand — are actively moving toward approving FSD and autonomous transport, creating potential alternative deployment markets.
