## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab Unit Economics:** Cern Basher modeled Cybercab payback periods and showed some scenarios where the vehicle could begin paying itself off within 6 months, though he notes this depends heavily on utilization, pricing, and fleet management. The host's own analysis suggests a cost of goods sold around $17,000–$18,000 before reaching Model 3-scale volumes, making a $25,000 sale price highly profitable — and noting that for every Cybercab sold at $34,000 against a $17,000 COGS, Tesla effectively gets a "BOGO" where it can keep one for itself at zero net cash outflow. Cern states that no competitor's robotaxi platform comes within 2–3x of Cybercab's cost of goods sold; even a hypothetical cheap Indian-sourced vehicle would require costly sensor/compute upfits that alone exceed Tesla's entire sensor suite cost.

2. **Cybercab Production Timing:** Tesla has already been producing Cybercab units (described as "hundreds" made), and the Cybercab production line and capital expenditure are characterized as already committed. Guest Herbert and the host agree that consumer sales of Cybercab to individuals are unlikely before the second half of 2027 at the earliest, with the near-term focus being Tesla-owned fleet deployment. However, guests note Tesla has "reiterated Cybercab production starts this year." The CFO confirmed Tesla would access financial markets (loans) to fund robotaxi fleet buildout, with a non-recourse securitized loan structure floated as a likely mechanism, collateralized by the vehicle fleet itself.

3. **Tesla vs. Uber Economics:** In an extended analysis, Cern Basher breaks down Tesla's structural cost advantages over Uber. Uber takes a platform fee exceeding $1/mile plus approximately half the driver's revenue. Tesla avoids driver costs entirely, charges retail electricity at approximately 17 cents/kWh vs. Uber partner vehicles paying 50+ cents/kWh at third-party chargers, and owns its own service center network — eliminating the need to pay retail repair rates or establish third-party service partnerships. Tesla also benefits from AI-optimized fleet positioning (heat-map based), pre-positioned vehicles at residential locations if consumer sales occur, and the ability to conduct fleet-wide overnight maintenance without service downtime. Cern concludes that "fleet owners don't need Uber" and that Tesla uniquely has no interest in the Uber partnership because sharing revenue is structurally irrational at scale.

4. **Robotaxi Delay Risk Analysis:** Cern performed a DCF sensitivity analysis showing that a one-year delay in robotaxi deployment reduces Tesla's present value by only approximately 2–3%, given the long time horizon of the thesis. He cautions against linear extrapolation of delays — the ramp can catch up quickly once launched. Current unsupervised deployment in Austin is described as ongoing but still limited in scale as of April 2026.

5. **Two-Seat Design Rationale:** Data cited by Cern shows that 80–90%+ of rideshare trips involve two or fewer passengers, validating the Cybercab's two-seat design. The broader Tesla fleet (Model Y, future Robo Van, Cybertruck) handles the remaining multi-passenger demand within the network.
