This video features host Herbert interviewing Jo Bhakdi, an economist, investor, and tech entrepreneur, about Tesla's long-term investment thesis centered on space-based AI compute, Terafab, and the convergence of SpaceX's Starship with orbital AI data centers. The core argument is that while Terafab sounds like a distant, decade-long vision, the actual economic inflection point — where space-based AI becomes cheaper than terrestrial AI infrastructure — could arrive as early as 2027, making it far more investable and near-term than the market currently prices in. Jo Bhakdi contends that the singular forcing function for this entire thesis is Starship's commercial viability and cost-per-kilogram to orbit dropping below $200.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **FSD as the hardest problem, but imminent:** Jo Bhakdi describes FSD as the most technically difficult challenge Tesla has faced — harder than Optimus and vastly harder than space-based AI. He characterizes it as the primary near-term cash flow driver over the next three to four years via robotaxi deployment. Both he and Herbert express belief that fully working FSD is "very, very soon" and "imminent," and note that mainstream media (citing an article from The Verge) has shifted to accepting autonomous vehicles as inevitable rather than debating whether they will happen at all.

2. **AI as the shared foundation:** Jo Bhakdi emphasizes that Tesla's AI development for FSD is the same AI being used to develop Optimus robots, which in turn is what leads toward AGI. He frames the three pillars — robotaxi, Optimus, and space-based AI — as deeply interconnected rather than separate bets, with FSD being the foundational technological unlock.

## Robotaxi
1. **Robotaxi as the primary near-term cash flow source:** Jo Bhakdi explicitly identifies FSD-enabled robotaxi as his expected main cash flow generator for Tesla over the next three to four years. He uses the Cybercab as an analogy for the space-based AI thesis: once you have FSD and no one else does, you can produce a perfectly scalable, cheap Cybercab and manufacture them at will while competitors cannot, making it "shooting fish in a barrel."

## Humanoid Bots
1. **Optimus ranked as moderately difficult with large TAM:** Jo Bhakdi places Optimus as the second-hardest challenge after FSD, but notes that the debate has largely shifted from "will it happen?" to "when?" He argues that even with the most advanced existing AI models available today, developing Optimus would still be "a giant headache," but the direction is clear and uncontested. He contrasts this with space-based AI, which he considers the easiest of the three major Tesla theses from a technical difficulty standpoint.

## Market & Competition
1. **Terafab and the space-based AI thesis:** Elon Musk's newly announced Terafab concept — a massive chip manufacturing facility intended to produce a terawatt of compute — is the video's central topic. Jo Bhakdi reframes Terafab not as a single giant factory but as an economic concept: Tesla building its own chips optimized for space deployment. He argues the concept goes live the moment Tesla can produce any chip that delivers economically superior space-based AI, which he believes could happen within two years given existing Samsung fab partnerships. 2. **The $200/kg to orbit forcing function:** Jo Bhakdi identifies cost-per-kilogram to orbit as the single most important variable for the entire space-based AI thesis. He argues that once Starship is commercially operational and reused 10–20 times, the cost drops below $200/kg — a 10x reduction from current Falcon costs. At that moment, space-based AI data centers become cheaper than terrestrial buildout, and the economic case becomes self-sustaining. His internal schedule puts this at early 2027, with a one-year buffer bringing it to 2028 in public statements. [NEW]

3. **Space-based AI chips do not need to compete with Nvidia:** A key insight Jo Bhakdi presents is that in space, the energy constraint that gives Nvidia its competitive advantage on Earth disappears. Solar power in orbit provides approximately five times the energy per square meter per day compared to Earth. This means a lower-tech, cheaper chip optimized for inference could outperform Nvidia's economics in a space stack, even if it is vastly inferior on terrestrial benchmarks. Tesla could manufacture large volumes of simpler chips and dominate orbital AI compute without needing cutting-edge semiconductor fabrication. [NEW]

4. **AI economic singularity already reached:** Jo Bhakdi argues that the true economic inflection point — where AI generates more value than it costs — was reached around November–December 2025. He references Andrej Karpathy's observation that December 2025 was another "ChatGPT moment" driven by agentic AI, which changed how every software developer works. He contends this has created infinite and permanent demand for compute, making the energy bottleneck the critical constraint and giving the space-based AI thesis urgency. [NEW]

5. **Starship V3 and V4 timeline:** The conversation references Starship Block 3 (V3) as targeting an April 2026 launch, with V4 scheduled approximately six months after that. Elon Musk has stated Starship B3 will expand payload capacity to 200 tons to orbit, up from 100 tons. The "mini satellite" demonstrated carries 100 kilowatts of power, with future satellites expected to reach the megawatt range. Jo Bhakdi notes that even with launch delays, the trajectory toward sub-$200/kg costs is clear and close. [NEW]

6. **Present value analysis of Terafab:** A slide prepared by Cern Basher (referenced in the conversation) calculates the present value of Terafab assuming: $2 trillion per year in revenue by 2035 (9 years out), no growth beyond that, a 50% profit margin, a 50% probability of failure, and a 15% discount rate. Under these conservative assumptions, the present value comes out to approximately $1 trillion. Elon Musk responded that "growth will continue far past a terawatt." Jo Bhakdi's view is that the real investor alpha is in recognizing the failure probability is much lower than 50%, not in debating the revenue figure. [NEW]

7. **Wall Street disconnect on space-based AI:** Jo Bhakdi observes that Wall Street has been largely unresponsive to the Terafab announcement, with the stock moving more on near-term EV sales news than on the orbital AI thesis. He argues this is the same dynamic seen with Optimus — analysts are catching up faster than before, but the space-based AI thesis is not yet on institutional radar as a near-term business opportunity. He sees this gap as the primary source of alpha for informed investors. [NEW]

8. **Samsung fab relationship:** Herbert notes that Samsung has recently announced a second fab, and that Tesla's AI5 chip is being designed with Samsung manufacturing capacity, with Elon Musk reportedly claiming ownership of essentially all output from the first fab. Jo Bhakdi frames this as confirmation that Tesla is already executing the "pragmatic chip manufacturing maximization" strategy he describes, separate from the longer-term Terafab vision. [NEW]
