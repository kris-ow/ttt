This April 13, 2026 livestream from the "futureaza" channel covers more than a dozen news stories spanning Tesla's US market share, global EV trends, FSD approval in Europe, competition from legacy OEMs, and data center delays, followed by a live Q&A session. The host's core argument is that while Tesla's absolute US sales figures are declining, the broader EV market is shrinking faster, leaving Tesla as the relative winner in a smaller race — and that legacy automakers like GM, Honda, and Toyota are making strategically poor decisions by retreating toward internal combustion.

## Electric Vehicles
1. **Tesla US Q1 Sales Lowest on Record, But Market Share Rises:** Tesla sold 117,000 units in Q1 2026 in the US, marking its lowest quarterly sales figure and its third consecutive year of Q1 declines. The last time Tesla's Q1 and full-year US sales grew was in 2023. The host notes the expiration of federal EV tax credits compounded the decline, but acknowledges that the rest of the EV market is falling even harder, leaving Tesla as the largest relative beneficiary — though the host is candid that "number one in a smaller race is not as exciting."

2. **Cybertruck Leads EV Truck Segment Despite 45% Drop:** In the EV truck segment, the Cybertruck retained the number one sales position despite falling 45% year-over-year. The F-150 Lightning held second place but has been discontinued. The Rivian R1T, Chevy Silverado EV, and GMC Sierra EV all performed poorly, with the Sierra up only 3% on a very low unit base. The host frames this as further evidence of Tesla's relative dominance even as the absolute market contracts.

3. **China EV Exports Hit Record High in March:** Chinese EV exports surged 140% to a record 349,000 vehicles in March, according to Bloomberg data, driven partly by fuel price shocks stemming from a Middle East conflict that has restricted over 10 million barrels per day of crude supply through the Strait of Hormuz and pushed oil prices above $100 per barrel. Demand for EVs and hybrids rose across Asia Pacific, Europe, and the US as fuel costs climbed.

4. **Honda Acknowledges Chinese Supply Chain Superiority:** Honda publicly acknowledged the strength of China's EV supply chain, mirroring similar comments from Toyota the prior week. The host interprets these statements as signals to domestic suppliers to cut costs, and frames both companies' admissions as a sign that legacy Japanese automakers are in serious competitive trouble. A small Honda K-car EV was also discussed — priced at approximately $27,000 with roughly 100 miles of EPA range — which the host considers too expensive for too little capability and unlikely to reach North America.

5. **GM Doubles Down on ICE, Announces Gas-Powered Blazer and V8 Investment:** GM confirmed a 2027 US production start for a gas-powered Chevy Blazer and announced an additional $150 million investment in new small-block V8 engines. The host is sharply critical, arguing GM is retreating to high short-term margins at the cost of its long-term competitive position, especially given looming EV mandates in many global markets. He contrasts this with GM's previously credible EV efforts (Blazer EV, Equinox EV, Silverado EV) and calls the ICE reinvestment "a dead end."

6. **Dealership Model Costs Consumers $3,900–$4,900 Extra Per Vehicle:** A new study found that franchise dealership inefficiencies add between $3,900 and $4,900 to the cost of a new vehicle. Broken down: inventory carrying costs add roughly $1,000; floor plan interest rates of 6–9% add approximately $1,600; and facility and staff overhead adds $1,200 to $1,900 in what the study authors call "avoidable costs." The host uses this to reinforce Tesla's direct-sales model advantage.

7. **Kia Delays Software-Focused Cars, Pledges $28 Billion Investment:** Kia acknowledged it is not yet software-focused and announced a $28 billion investment to develop better-integrated EVs where software is not an afterthought. The host views this as an admission of failure relative to Tesla but gives credit for the commitment to improvement.

8. **Volkswagen Midcycle Refresh Framed as Course Correction:** Volkswagen announced the ID.3 and ID.4 will be replaced by vehicles the company is calling "true Volkswagens," with executives saying the brand lost its core identity. The host characterizes this as a modest midcycle refresh rather than a fundamental redesign and is skeptical of the framing.

9. **Windrose Electric Semi Positioned as Tesla Competitor:** A startup called Windrose Electric has built an electric semi that the host describes as competing directly with Tesla Semi, in contrast to legacy players like the Freightliner eCascadia and Volvo, which appear to compete mainly with each other. The host teases an upcoming in-depth video with Mark from The Tesla Life covering the comparison, noting that starting from a clean-slate design has advantages and trade-offs versus adapting existing manufacturing lines.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **FSD Approved for Testing in the Netherlands, EU-Wide Vote Pending:** The Dutch Vehicle Authority (RDW) has approved Full Self-Driving for use in the Netherlands and has notified the European Commission of its plan to seek EU-wide approval. The next step is a vote by all EU member states, requiring a majority within the responsible committee. The host emphasizes that RDW frequently sets standards that influence most of Europe, and that approval could cascade to non-EU countries as well. He frames this as a major milestone for those who said European regulatory approval would never happen.

2. **New LFP Batteries Reportedly Coming to Tesla Base Models:** Based on audience questions and upcoming research the host is preparing, Tesla is reportedly set to use new LFP (lithium iron phosphate) batteries in base model vehicles. The host says he is covering the story later in the week and is still researching the supplier, but confirms the directional claim: "Tesla will buy batteries from anybody if they're good."

## Robotaxi
1. **Cybercab Production Lines Building Both Test Mules and Ride-Ready Units:** Citing Joe Tegtmeyer's reporting, the host explains that there are two separate Cybercab production lines at the factory — one building test mules and one building vehicles intended for paid rides. The ride-ready vehicles are being built now, which the host takes as a signal that commercial deployment is closer than many had assumed.
