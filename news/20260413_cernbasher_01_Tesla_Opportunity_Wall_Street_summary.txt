This video segment features Cern Basher discussing "Digital Optimus" — a concept describing Tesla's potential AI agent business that operates purely in software, performing white-collar computer-based work rather than physical labor. The core thesis is that this opportunity is completely overlooked by Wall Street, and could potentially be Tesla's largest business by addressing the portion of GDP not covered by robotaxis (transportation) or physical Optimus (manual labor). The discussion includes a rough financial model estimating present value across multiple pricing tiers and growth scenarios.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Digital Optimus defined as Tesla's AI agent for computer work:** Digital Optimus is framed as the software counterpart to physical Optimus — an AI agent that performs white-collar, computer-based tasks rather than physical labor. The host argues that since computer work (non-transportation, non-physical labor) represents the majority of global GDP today, the revenue opportunity is enormous. Tesla's product set — robotaxis for transportation (~5% of GDP), physical Optimus for physical labor (~25–30% of GDP), and Digital Optimus for the remainder — would collectively address virtually every segment of global economic output.

2. **Tesla's vision-based approach as a key differentiator:** The host contrasts Tesla's approach to AI agents with that of Anthropic and OpenAI. He argues that competitors rely on APIs — coding the AI into specific software to read and manipulate data — which is inherently limited given the tens of millions of software applications in existence. Tesla's approach, by contrast, uses vision and context-awareness: the AI looks at whatever is on the screen, just as a human would, and learns to use it without requiring pre-coded integrations. This is described as "true context-aware intelligence" and is said to leverage all of Tesla's learnings from FSD and its vision-based autonomous driving stack. Ash Kalsuami, previously in charge of vision for Optimus and robotaxi, is hinted (though not officially announced) to now be leading Digital Optimus under the project name "Macrohard."

3. **Tesla's existing hardware fleet as an unfair infrastructure advantage:** Unlike physical Optimus, which is constrained by chip manufacturing capacity (partly why Terafab is being built), Digital Optimus does not face the same scaling bottleneck. The host notes that Elon Musk has stated there are 9 million Tesla vehicles in the world, roughly half of which may be capable of providing digital inference. Additionally, Tesla's Supercharger network and home charging infrastructure could supply the power needed. This means the inference chips and power infrastructure already exist, allowing Digital Optimus to scale much faster than physical Optimus.

4. **Five-tier revenue model with present value estimates:** The host presents a rough five-tier pricing model for Digital Optimus, all assuming 10 million agents deployed by 2031. At $100/month, the model yields $12 billion in annual revenue, $8 billion in profit (assuming 70% software margins), and a present value of approximately $32 billion assuming no growth beyond that point. At $500/month, the model yields $60 billion in revenue, $42 billion in profit, and a present value of $160 billion. At $5,000/month — described as replacing a full employee — the present value reaches $1.6 trillion at 10 million agents. The host recommends being conservative and pegging the present value at $30–$50 billion based on the $100–$500/month tiers.

5. **Growth scenarios dramatically expand the opportunity:** Adding long-term growth rates to the $500/month tier model, the host shows that present value rises from $160 billion (0% growth) to approximately $500 billion at a 10% annual growth rate. He acknowledges that pricing will likely fall significantly over time, but argues this will be more than offset by volume increases — potentially trillions of digital agents at lower price points. He also notes that if Digital Optimus is shared 50/50 with SpaceX/xAI, Tesla's attributable present value would be reduced proportionally.

## Market & Competition

1. **Anthropic and OpenAI as revenue benchmarks:** To ground the pricing assumptions, the host cites current market data: OpenAI is generating approximately $20 billion per year in revenue, and Anthropic is at approximately $14 billion and growing rapidly toward $20 billion. The host personally states he pays Anthropic approximately $50 per day, which exceeds $500/month — supporting the assertion that the pricing tiers used in the model are not unreasonable. Anthropic is noted to be planning a public offering sometime in 2026, with a speculated valuation around $400 billion, which the host uses to argue that high revenue multiples for AI software businesses are already being accepted by markets.

2. **Compute scarcity as a real constraint for competitors:** A participant in the discussion highlights that existing LLM providers are already capacity-constrained — OpenAI queries take long processing times, and Anthropic has reportedly implemented surge pricing (5x higher fees during peak hours from 5:00 a.m. to noon Pacific time). This is used to reinforce the argument that Tesla's distributed inference advantage via its vehicle fleet gives it a meaningfully stronger starting position in this market.
