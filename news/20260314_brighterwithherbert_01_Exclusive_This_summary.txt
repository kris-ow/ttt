Figure's humanoid robot division made waves by releasing a 2.5-minute uncut video of their Figure 03 robot autonomously cleaning a living room, performing multiple complex tasks in sequence without any human teleoperation. Host Herbert and robotics expert Scott Walter — a two-time robotics company founder with decades of simulation experience — analyze the video in detail, discuss what it signals about the state of humanoid robotics, and contextualize it alongside news from Tesla, NVIDIA/ABB, and startup Roa AI.

1. The Figure 03 video is technically significant for several reasons beyond surface impressions. The robot demonstrated whole-body coordination — not just arm or hand movements, but full postural engagement, leaning into tasks. It handled flexible objects (a cloth/towel), picked up a cell phone from a flat surface (historically very difficult), and operated a TV remote by picking it up, stabilizing it with a thumb, pressing the power button, and then repositioning it neatly. Crucially, it maintained short-to-medium horizon task memory — for instance, temporarily setting aside objects and returning to collect them later.

2. The towel-over-the-shoulder moment was called out as one of the most humanlike behaviors in the video. The robot was carrying a spray bottle plus other objects, encountered a third item it couldn't hold conventionally, and improvised by slinging it over its shoulder. Scott Walter noted this may have emerged from training data where a human teleoperator did the same thing habitually — raising questions about whether this is genuine emergent behavior or an artifact of a specific person's mannerisms being absorbed into the model.

3. Elon Musk's public question — asking whether the video was autonomous or teleoperated — was treated as meaningful validation. Figure CEO Brett Adcock confirmed it was fully autonomous. Scott Walter argued the smoothness of motion itself is evidence of autonomy, since teleoperation typically produces jerky, imprecise movements. He referenced robo-boxing footage as a comparison showing how poor human-operated robot control tends to look.

4. Key open questions remain about how the task was structured and prompted. It is unclear whether the robot was given a simple prompt like "tidy the living room" or a detailed itemized list of subtasks. Walter speculates the training involved pre-learning discrete skills — pillow placement, object sorting, surface wiping — and that the robot may work from an implicit "preferred state" baseline, comparing the current room configuration to an expected ideal. The starting state in the video was also notably tidy relative to a real-world messy living room, which limits direct applicability claims.

5. The remote control sequence specifically impressed as a dexterity milestone. Picking up a flat remote from a table, stabilizing it against thumb pressure, identifying and pressing the correct button, then self-correcting the placement — all in a timeframe comparable to a human — represents meaningful manipulation capability. Walter questioned whether the robot has been trained only on this specific remote model, noting that button layouts and sizes vary enormously across brands, which would affect generalizability.

6. Bipedal form factor is being validated by demos like this. The narrow gap between the coffee table and sofa in the video would be impassable for a wheeled robot base, underscoring why humanoid bipedalism is practically advantageous in home environments — not just aesthetically appealing.

7. Tesla's Cortex 2 supercomputer is described as the most important near-term development to watch, more than any robot hardware reveal. Half of the Cortex 2 installation is expected to come online within approximately one month of the video's recording, while construction continues. The plan being reported (attributed to journalist Joe Techmar) is to complete the mega cooling system and half the computing cabinetry, then finish the second half while expanding data center capabilities through the summer. Tesla is also reportedly stopping Model S and Model X production at Fremont to convert that line for humanoid robot manufacturing, targeting 1 million robots per year at that facility, with a separate Giga Texas site targeting 10 million robots per year.

8. Tesla's Optimus Gen 3 reveal is expected within a month (Q1 of the year of recording). The hosts frame this less as a robot hardware milestone and more as a precursor to the real story: massive-scale simulation-plus-real-world training loops using 20,000–30,000 physical Optimus units in a real gymnasium environment, feeding data back into supercomputer-driven simulation.

9. The NVIDIA-ABB Robotics partnership claims 99% simulation accuracy, but Scott Walter urges caution about interpreting that figure. The 99% claim refers specifically to how faithfully ABB robot motion in NVIDIA Omniverse matches the real robot's movements — accomplished by pulling ABB's actual controller software and plugging it directly into the simulation rather than approximating it with a physics engine. This is a genuine breakthrough for robot motion fidelity, and something Walter spent 40 years advocating for. However, it does not mean the full simulated environment (conveyors, end-of-arm tooling, process effects like painting or drilling) achieves the same accuracy. He notes that photorealism and simulation fidelity are entirely separate properties.

10. Roa AI, newly out of stealth and based in Palo Alto, claims to have solved the robot training data scarcity problem through video generation. Their Direct Video Action (DVA) model pre-trains on tens of millions of hours of human video, then fine-tunes on approximately 10 hours of task-specific data. The system imagines short video sequences of future states as a planning mechanism, then uses an inverse dynamics model (IDM) to translate observed human hand movements into robot control signals. Walter flagged the published edge-case recovery videos on their website as particularly credible — showing robots recovering from failures like straps coming loose during a decanting task — and called Roa one of the most exciting companies to watch in 2026.

11. Both hosts agreed that despite all home-environment marketing, humanoid robots will deploy commercially in factories first. The economics are decisive: factories will pay $100,000–$200,000 per year per robot, whereas consumer home users might pay $20,000–$30,000 once. Factories also lack vulnerable occupants (children, elderly, pets), reducing safety certification complexity. The home demonstrations serve dual purposes: capturing public and investor excitement, and generating diverse training data that makes factory-deployed robots more generalizable and capable.
