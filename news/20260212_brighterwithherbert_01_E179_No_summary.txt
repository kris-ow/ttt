This roundtable episode brings together four Tesla bulls — host Herbert, Jeff Lutz (manufacturing expert), James from Invest Answers, and analyst CERN Basher — to debate Tesla's near-term stock trajectory amid a period of significant news flow. The central tension is whether the stock remains flat as investors wait for clarity on major catalysts, or whether a flood of new buyers rushes in anticipating AGI, autonomy, and robotics. The panel largely agrees the stock is undervalued on a fair-value basis but disagrees on what specific trigger will move the market.

TECHNICAL SETUP AND MACRO CONTEXT

1. Tesla's chart remains in an uptrend. James notes the stock has been above its 200-day moving average and the optimized trend indicator has been blue (bullish) since mid-2024. He views this as a positive technical backdrop despite broader market weakness.

2. PMI correlation with Tesla's stock price is a meaningful signal. Historical data shows Tesla stock price tracks the Purchasing Managers Index with roughly a 2-3 month lag. Recent PMI data showed a breakout, which the panel reads as a potential leading indicator of Tesla stock appreciation in the coming months.

SPACEX-TESLA MERGER DEBATE

3. The panel broadly agrees a 2026 merger is unlikely, with end-of-2027 being the earliest realistic scenario and most leaning toward 2028 or later. James assigns it a 20% probability of ever happening before 2028, citing extreme dilution risk, mismatched life cycles (Tesla is at a commercialization inflection while SpaceX is pre-revenue scale), regulatory complexity across the US, Europe, and China, and conflicting shareholder bases including early SpaceX backers like Peter Thiel and Google.

4. The valuation asymmetry problem cuts both ways. If the merger happens now, SpaceX's private valuation potentially exceeds Tesla's, which is unfavorable for Tesla shareholders. If Tesla's robo taxi ramp succeeds first, SpaceX shareholders would regret not merging in 2026. Jeff Lutz adds that Elon's preference for vertical integration across engineering teams — eliminating duplicate HR, finance, and management — is a real motivation, but timing is the key obstacle.

5. A Department of Defense wildcard could accelerate the timeline. James references Peter Hegseth visiting Starbase and the DoD budget growing from $1 trillion to $1.5 trillion. If the DoD handed SpaceX a $50 billion contract to provide space-based defense capabilities, that could force Elon to consolidate all resources — solar, batteries, Optimus, Starlink — immediately. The panel views this as a low-probability but high-impact scenario.

6. Larry Goldberg's timeline prediction: SpaceX IPO around June-September 2026, followed by a standard 3-month investor lockup, then a possible Tesla-SpaceX merger discussion toward late 2026 at the earliest. His key insight is that Starlink revenue is about to "skyrocket," analogous to how the Model 3 transformed Tesla's financials. He believes Starlink could reach $100 billion in revenue, with margins significantly higher than Tesla's automotive business.

ROBO TAXI RAMP AND THE WALL STREET INFLECTION POINT

7. The panel debates what car count triggers institutional recognition of the robo taxi business. James argues 2,500 vehicles is a reference point because Waymo operates roughly that fleet and is valued at $116 billion. Jeff counters that simply surpassing Waymo in unit count is a media-friendly narrative that markets can understand. CERN pushes back, arguing the number needs to be 5,000-10,000 before it registers as a meaningful percentage of Tesla's earnings — 10,000 cars would generate roughly $400 million in net income, about 10% of Tesla's 2025 net income of $4 billion.

8. The key insight is that markets price on earnings expectations, not earnings themselves. The panel agrees Wall Street won't wait for robo taxi revenue to show up on a balance sheet — it will price in the trajectory once the ramp becomes visible. Jeff notes the "race to the front" dynamic: if one investor is waiting for 20,000 cars, the next rational actor buys at 19,000, and so on down.

9. Current robo taxi safety streak stands at nearly 600,000 miles without a severe incident. By comparison, the average human driver has a serious accident every 300,000 miles. James characterizes Tesla's unsupervised FSD as currently twice as safe as a human. FSD data more broadly shows 6-7x safer performance.

10. Cyber Cab production ramp context from Jeff Lutz (manufacturing expert): the unboxed manufacturing process itself is not the hard part — it's the reaction injection molding for body panels (eliminating the paint booth, saving over $1 billion in capex) and the associated reliability, crash, UV, and salt testing. He does not expect tens of thousands of Cyber Cabs by April or May 2026 but believes the ramp is well underway, with pre-production validation units already driving off the factory floor. He emphasizes that Tesla is attempting something more technically ambitious than typical low-cost car makers, who simply remove features rather than redesigning the manufacturing process.

11. Tesla is hiring 53 Cyber Cab manufacturing roles, AI safety operators near Dallas, and spotting Cyber Cabs doing autonomous parking lot loops from the production line. Tesla China has activated an AI data center for FSD training. The EU is near regulatory approval. The SELF DRIVE Act passed Congress. Lemonade insurance now offers 50% FSD discounts in Oregon, not just Arizona.

OPTIMUS AND THE ECONOMICS OF HUMANOID ROBOTICS

12. James presents the macroeconomic scale of Optimus. A single Fremont factory line producing 1 million Optimus units sold/leased at $75,000 each generates $75 billion in annual revenue — roughly 0.25% of US GDP from one factory. A 10-million-unit line at Giga Texas would generate $750 billion per year, approximately 2.5% of US GDP, before accounting for the multiplier effect of those robots working in the economy.

13. Elon's "Optimus Academy" concept involves 10,000-30,000 robots in self-play simulation to generate real-world data sufficient to validate sim-to-real training. James views this as a signal that Elon will do everything possible to reach that fleet size quickly, possibly through internal Tesla deployment before external sales.

14. Jeff Lutz flags the US industrial robot deficit. China is installing industrial robots at a 4:1 rate versus the US, with far lower production costs for those robots. Optimus is potentially the only viable path to closing this gap because it is not a conventional industrial robot — it is a general-purpose humanoid that can be deployed across task types without facility redesign.

FSD PURCHASE DECISION (48-HOUR WINDOW DISCUSSED)

15. CERN's framework for the buy/don't-buy FSD decision: buy if you plan to keep the vehicle long-term, if you intend commercial use, and if you believe subscription prices will rise significantly. Don't buy if you're keeping the car 2-3 years or if the $8,000 (approximately 20 shares at ~$400) would generate higher returns as Tesla stock. James's counter-argument: even without purchasing FSD, Tesla can activate the software remotely and offer owners a revenue split through the robo taxi network, so the purchase is not strictly necessary to capture value.

$100 TRILLION VALUATION SCENARIO

16. CERN's back-of-envelope math: at a 25x PE, reaching $100 trillion requires $4 trillion in annual profit. With 10 billion robots generating $400 per unit per year in profit, or 1 billion robots generating $4,000 each, the math is achievable without including robo taxi revenue. He caveats that the timeline — specifically whether this is achievable by 2035 — is the real question, not the number itself.

YEAR-END PRICE TARGETS AND FAIR VALUE

17. James pegs Tesla's current fair value at $3,500 per share, rising to approximately $4,000 by end of 2026. He notes no one has successfully challenged the long-term model assumptions, and even cutting numbers in half yields a $2,000 fair value. Herbert targets $600-$800 by year-end depending on robo taxi ramp speed. The actual stock was trading near $400 at time of recording.

Conclusion and calls to action: The panel recommends patient accumulation, views robo taxi fleet size as the single most important near-term catalyst, and advises against over-indexing on demos or announcements. The merger overhang is real but ultimately resolvable. The panel unanimously agrees the biggest risk is not to the bull thesis, but to timing — everything is coming, the question is when markets wake up to it.
