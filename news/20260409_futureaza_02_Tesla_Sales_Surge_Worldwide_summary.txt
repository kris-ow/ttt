This episode of Futurza features host Brian and co-host Herbert from Brighter discussing three major Tesla developments: Intel's partnership with Tesla and SpaceX on the Terafab chip manufacturing complex, the release of FSD version 14.3 and its implications for unsupervised robotaxi deployment, and a notable surge in Tesla vehicle sales across several international markets. The core thesis is that Tesla is systematically assembling the pieces — compute infrastructure, software maturity, and demand drivers — needed to transition from an EV company into a subscription-based autonomous mobility platform.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD 14.3 Released with 20% Faster Reaction Time:** Tesla released FSD version 14.3, which Tesla itself emphasized delivers a 20% faster reaction time. This improvement is framed as critical for safety and as the key enabler for scaling unsupervised robotaxi operations. The hosts note that achieving this required a full top-to-bottom rewrite of the system. Visualization and camera processing speed were also improved, allowing the vehicle to detect obstacles more rapidly.

2. **Reasoning Capabilities Added but Limited in Scope:** FSD 14.3 includes reasoning functionality, but it is currently focused narrowly on destination understanding — interpreting what actions to take upon arriving at a destination. Tesla indicated that broader reasoning capabilities will be released incrementally in future point releases. The hosts and their guest David Moss agreed this is not yet the reasoning breakthrough that would fully change the game for autonomous driving.

3. **David Moss Testing and Safety Validation:** Tester David Moss — known for performing coast-to-coast drives with FSD 14.2 and being featured in Tesla's own coverage — logged over 10 hours on FSD 14.3 before and after the live stream, with plans to do an additional 5 hours. Moss conducted back-alley pedestrian pop-out tests at night to evaluate reaction time, and the system demonstrated fast and reliable obstacle detection. Moss believes FSD 14.2 was already good enough for unsupervised robotaxi deployment, and that 14.3's safety improvements further reinforce that case.

4. **FSD Wide Release Imminent:** As of the time of recording, over 50 testers had evaluated FSD 14.3, and the consensus among them was that it is ready for wide release. Herbert views wide release as a significant demand driver for Tesla vehicles, citing a personal anecdote about a brand-loyal Mercedes/BMW driver who expressed interest in buying a Tesla after experiencing FSD firsthand.

## Robotaxi

1. **Unsupervised Deployment: A Choice, Not a Capability Gap:** The hosts argue that the gap between Tesla and Waymo on unsupervised robotaxi is not one of technical ability — it is a strategic choice by Tesla to delay. They note that Waymo still relies on more archaic, rules-based encoded systems rather than neural networks, and that Tesla's FSD is already operating at a level comparable or superior to what Waymo uses without a safety driver. The 20% reaction time improvement in 14.3 is cited as closing the last meaningful safety gap.

2. **Robotaxi Revenue as the True Stock Catalyst:** Herbert argues, drawing on Tesla's full stock history since IPO, that the only true catalyst for sustained stock price increases is earnings — specifically, repeated earnings beats over multiple quarters. A robotaxi city announcement or product demo will not move the stock meaningfully. The real inflection point will come when robotaxi revenue appears as a distinct line item on Tesla's balance sheet, analogous to how Apple's transition to a subscription model was only credited by Wall Street once the numbers were undeniable.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Germany Sales Hit Best-Ever March:** Tesla new car registrations in Germany quadrupled year-over-year in March 2026, representing the best March Tesla has ever recorded in Germany. The hosts frame this not merely as a recovery but as a record-breaking performance.

2. **Sales Rebounding Across Multiple Markets:** Beyond Germany, the hosts note that Tesla sales are up in South Korea and Japan, and are rebounding in China following the retooling disruptions of the prior year. Q1 2026 was acknowledged as soft overall, attributed to Q1 seasonal patterns and lingering effects from the end of IRA incentives.

3. **Model Y L as a Demand Contributor:** Herbert estimates the Model Y L (an apparently larger Model Y variant) could add approximately 10,000 to 20,000 incremental global sales, though he does not expect it to dramatically move overall delivery numbers. Brian speculates it may outperform Herbert's estimate, while acknowledging this is speculation without supporting data.

4. **FSD as a Demand Driver for Vehicle Sales:** Herbert identifies FSD wide release as a more significant demand driver than the Model Y L. He draws an analogy to Apple's transition from hardware to subscription revenue, suggesting Tesla is at a similar inflection point where FSD subscription uptake will eventually reshape how Wall Street values the company — but that this will only be reflected in stock price once it shows up in earnings results over multiple quarters.

## Market & Competition

1. **Intel Partnership on Terafab Is a Strategic Breakthrough:** Intel announced it will join Elon Musk's Terafab chip manufacturing project alongside SpaceX and Tesla. The hosts describe this as solving a key missing piece — access to advanced semiconductor manufacturing expertise. Intel is one of only a handful of companies in the world with knowledge of leading-edge chip fabrication processes (sub-5nm, approaching 2nm and below). Intel shares rose modestly on the announcement.

2. **Terafab Cost Estimate of $20 Billion:** Reports cited by the hosts suggest the Terafab project could cost approximately $20 billion with Intel's involvement — a figure the hosts describe as attractively low given the scale of the ambition, noting that China has already spent approximately half a trillion dollars just to reach 5nm fabrication capability.

3. **Samsung Already Committed; Intel Adds American Dimension:** Samsung had already agreed to build chip fab facilities in Taylor, Texas for Tesla, with plans for a second factory — both of which Tesla would absorb entirely. Intel's addition to the partnership brings the further advantage of being a domestic American company, which the hosts view as strategically important from a supply chain sovereignty standpoint.

4. **Intel Acquisition Floated as a Possibility:** The hosts reference a view from someone named "Joe" (not further identified) that Tesla or Elon Musk may ultimately acquire Intel outright. Brian notes that Elon himself had suggested buying Intel was an option, and that Intel likely joined the partnership partly to avoid that outcome.

5. **Waymo Falling Behind on Neural Network Transition:** The hosts assert that Waymo began with rules-based encoded systems rather than neural networks, and is only now beginning to transition. They credit Tesla as the clear leader in autonomous driving compute today, with the world's largest supercomputers dedicated primarily to FSD training, and a second system (Cortex 2) being developed for Optimus training.

6. **JP Morgan Bearish Calls Called Out:** The hosts mock JP Morgan's repeated sell ratings on Tesla, citing a calculation that if an investor had reduced their Tesla position by 1% every time JP Morgan issued a sell recommendation, their original $1 million investment would have gone to zero — while simply holding would have grown it to approximately $20 million. The hosts note that JP Morgan is itself one of Tesla's largest outside shareholders, which they find contradictory.
