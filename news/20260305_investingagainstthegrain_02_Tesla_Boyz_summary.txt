This is a Wednesday co-stream between hosts Herbert (Investing Against the Grain channel) and Nick Gibbs, joined by guest Hans Nelson (Hans C Nelson YouTube channel) for his final appearance on the show. The conversation centers on a post Elon Musk made earlier that day claiming "Tesla will be one of the companies to make AGI, and probably the first to make it in humanoid, atom-shaping form." The hosts unpack what this means for Tesla's competitive position, the XAI/SpaceX merger implications, and X Money's potential launch, with Hans departing the show to focus on building a product for content creator back-catalog management.

1. Elon's AGI post — Tesla vs. XAI distinction. Musk's statement that Tesla (not XAI) will make AGI surprised some, since most assumed XAI's Grok would power autonomous vehicles and humanoids. Herbert notes Elon has previously called FSD "baby AGI," so Tesla's AI team already has a credible claim, but the post raises the question of whether the two companies are becoming so intertwined — sharing chips, compute resources, and personnel — that the distinction is increasingly meaningless.

2. The competitive AGI landscape at the frontier. Hans argues Google (Gemini) is likely to be first to crack "digital AGI," with Anthropic being a surprising contender, citing Claude Opus and their coding model. He frames this multi-player race as healthy for humanity because competitive tension keeps any single actor from a runaway monopoly, with each player's strengths and weaknesses balancing out.

3. What "atom-shaping form" means beyond humanoids. Hans explains that factories, lithium refineries, battery manufacturing equipment, and Starlink's advanced satellite manufacturing are all atom-shaping machines. As AI intelligence gets embedded in these systems, fewer human operators will be needed. The key insight is that Tesla's deep vertical integration allows it to run the "innermost flywheel" faster than competitors — AI improves the factory, the factory produces better robots, those robots improve the factory further.

4. Tesla as the only "Mag 7" player doing physical AGI. Herbert argues that among the major tech competitors racing toward AGI, Tesla is uniquely positioned because it actually owns and operates manufacturing. Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, and Apple have no equivalent physical manufacturing loop. Nick partially pushes back, naming Amazon as a close second in terms of vertical integration, which Herbert concedes. The Chinese are identified as the only other serious competitor in physical AI manufacturing.

5. Nico's framing: AGI as a frontal lobe analogy. Nick describes what Tesla and XAI are building together as essentially a synthetic frontal lobe — the prefrontal cortex (reasoning, represented by Grok-side LLMs getting better at memory and reasoning) combined with the motor cortex (dexterity, locomotion in varied environments, being developed by the Optimus humanoid team). Hans pushes back that this is incomplete — the limbic system, fight-or-flight responses, and deeper memory structures are underappreciated components that the broader AI research community largely ignores but are essential for true AGI.

6. Hans's project announcement and departure. Hans is leaving the co-stream format to build a product that helps content creators make their back-catalog libraries more interactive and searchable for audiences. He frames a content creator's archive as essentially a memory bank, connecting it to his view that memory is one of the core unsolved problems in AI. He started with a highly structured content library (not his own, which he describes as unstructured) as an easier first target. He expressed thanks to collaborators including Herbert, Farzod, the Tesla Owners Silicon Valley group, and others.

7. Tesla hiring robo-taxi engineers in Berlin. Tesla's Giga Berlin careers page is now listing autonomous vehicle engineers. Elon recently said he envisions Giga Berlin producing Semi trucks, Cybercabs, and Optimus robots. The hosts interpret this hiring as a signal that European robo-taxi rollout is being actively prepared, likely 12 to 18 months from deployment. Nick predicts that once unsupervised autonomy launches in the Netherlands, Tesla fans and investors will travel across Europe to experience it, creating political pressure on other countries to adopt it.

8. NHTSA data deadlines and robo-taxi incident context. Two key dates are flagged for the following week: March 9 (Tesla's deadline to submit incident data to NHTSA) and March 10 (a full-day NHTSA event at the Department of Transportation headquarters). Herbert pushes back strongly against viral headlines claiming Tesla's robo-taxi has four times worse accident rates than humans, noting that all five January incidents were fender benders, four of them under four miles per hour, and one involved hitting a stationary post at 17 mph in a parking lot. He connects these incidents to a specific software release that caused low-speed proximity issues and expects the February data to show improvement.

9. SpaceX IPO and potential Tesla merger. David Faber on CNBC raised merger rumors on the morning of the stream. Peter Diamandis posted that SpaceX is planning to file for IPO in March 2026 at a $1.75 trillion valuation, with a future Tesla merger mentioned — and Elon replied confirming the substance of the post. The hosts speculate a merger of equals would require Tesla's market cap to roughly match SpaceX's, implying a Tesla share price around $46,642 according to a Google AI calculation cited on stream. XAI paying off $12.5 billion in debt ahead of the IPO filing was also noted as potentially preparatory.

10. X Money and the financial super-app vision. Elon posted that "X Money is going to be very big." The platform is in limited beta, reportedly offering 6% APY on deposits, cashback rewards, and stable coin integration, with a full launch expected in one to two months. Hans draws a direct line back to Elon's original X.com vision from the late 1990s — he wanted to replace banks entirely, was ousted by Peter Thiel and David Sacks for having too large a vision, and is now returning to that same problem with 25 years of additional knowledge. Hans argues the fraud problem that killed that vision then is now solvable via crypto rails, stable coins, and AI-assisted fraud detection. The hosts debate whether X Money should aim to be a brokerage house (highest value, where most personal wealth sits) rather than just a checking/savings account product.
