This video features a conversation between Brian (Futurazza) and Herbert (Brighter) covering Tesla's recent sales recovery in Europe, the cancellation of the Sony-Honda Mobility EV project, a mysterious crated vehicle spotted at Giga Texas (possibly a new Model Y variant), and the future demand impact of FSD approval in Europe and China. The core thesis is that Tesla is quietly winning while legacy automakers and new entrants are retreating from EVs, and that FSD approval in new markets will eventually be a significant demand catalyst.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Tesla breaks 13-month European sales losing streak:** Tesla ended a roughly 13-month sales decline in Europe in February 2026. However, both hosts caution against over-interpreting the year-over-year improvement, noting that the prior year's low baseline was partly due to a factory retooling window for the new Model Y. In January and February 2026 combined, Tesla recorded approximately 20,000–21,000 sales in Europe, compared to roughly 32,000 in the US over the same period. The broader European car market grew only 1.7%, though plug-in hybrids and EVs did grow within that market.

2. **BYD expanding aggressively in Europe:** BYD is outpacing Tesla in European sales, having expanded its dealer presence across the continent and aggressively pricing its vehicles to attract new buyers. The hosts acknowledge BYD as a credible competitive force in the region.

3. **Legacy automakers retreating from EVs:** A wave of legacy automakers are scaling back or canceling EV plans. Sony-Honda Mobility is suspending development and sale of its jointly developed EV (the "Afeela"), and will refund reservation holders. Jaguar reportedly pushed back its full-EV transition target from 2030. Additional companies mentioned as pulling back include GM, Stellantis, Ford, and several European manufacturers, with significant write-downs being taken across the industry. The hosts note this trend is occurring "every other day."

4. **Mystery crated vehicle arrives at Giga Texas — possible Model Y XL:** A crated vehicle was spotted and photographed at Giga Texas by Joe Tegtmeyer, sparking speculation. Herbert believes the vehicle is likely a Model Y L shipped from Giga Shanghai, but possibly a slightly longer variant he dubs the "Model Y XL" — featuring a marginally extended rear third row designed for the US market. The reasoning offered is that Tesla has discontinued the Model X to free up Fremont factory space for Optimus production, and a Model Y XL could fill that product gap. Brian leans toward it being a standard Model Y, noting that the plastic wrapping makes it difficult to confirm. Both agree that Elon Musk has previously hinted that something "way cooler than a minivan" is coming, which at least one host argues rules out a simple Y or Y L variant. The hosts note Tesla has previously shipped body-in-whites to factories worldwide to validate manufacturing prior to local production.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD approval in China and Europe as a future demand driver:** FSD is currently in testing in China and is expected to roll out more broadly. Both hosts agree that once FSD is approved in Europe and China, it will become a meaningful demand driver — but caution that timing is uncertain and the current version may not be compelling enough to move the needle on its own. Herbert references a potential "ChatGPT moment" for a more advanced future FSD version (he mentions "14.3" speculatively as an example of a version that could reach that threshold). Brian suggests that by the end of 2026, buyers in those markets may start purchasing Teslas specifically for the autonomy feature, even in supervised form. The analogy drawn is to the iPhone's App Store moment, where a technology shifts from novelty to non-negotiable over a period of one to two years.

## Market & Competition

1. **Emotional backlash against Tesla in Europe may be temporary:** The hosts discuss political sentiment in Europe around Elon Musk's actions as a factor suppressing Tesla sales. Brian draws a comparison to Volkswagen's Dieselgate scandal, noting that consumer anger faded quickly and did not have a lasting impact on VW sales, implying Tesla's situation may similarly normalize over time.
