Tesla's global position is examined across four key storylines: China sales figures, FSD regulatory progress and competition, Model Y expansion into new markets, and Audi's strategic pivot back toward internal combustion engines. The video features Herbert (the host) in conversation with Brian White, a Tesla-focused journalist from the YouTube channel Futurza who has traveled to China three times in the past year. The central thesis is that despite skeptics declaring Tesla's decline, the company is holding its ground competitively while autonomous driving capability is emerging as the decisive differentiator going forward.

Key Points

1. Tesla China February sales: context matters more than the headline number. Tesla's Giga Shanghai posted 58,599 wholesale units in February 2026, a 91% year-over-year increase. However, this is largely a statistical artifact — the year-ago period was suppressed because the factory was shut down for Model Y retooling. Month-over-month, sales were actually down 15%, and Brian White cautions against either a victory lap or alarm, noting that Q1 is seasonally weak and Chinese New Year further depresses January-February output. The more meaningful comparison is against the broader Chinese auto market, where BYD reported a 30%-plus decline in its own market — making Tesla's stability look considerably more impressive.

2. Tesla's domestic China sales reveal a best-ever February. Breaking down the 58,599 wholesale figure, approximately 38,000 vehicles were sold within China specifically, with Model Y comprising 66% and Model 3 comprising 34% of that mix. According to data from analyst Roland Percher, this was Tesla's best February ever in China. Year-to-date, Tesla is down about 6% versus the same period last year, representing 9% of last year's total — consistent with a slow-start quarter rather than structural decline.

3. The Model Y beat the Xiaomi YU7 in February, undercutting the "Model Y killer" narrative. Xiaomi's YU7 launched with strong reservations and significant hype as a potential Tesla competitor. Brian White argues the Chinese market is simply too large for a single "killer" to emerge, and Xiaomi's advantages — domestic brand, lower pricing, deep tech engineering heritage, and a manufacturing partnership that avoided early production hell — make it a strong but regionally limited competitor. Xiaomi recently purchased its manufacturing facility outright, eliminating profit-sharing. White rates Xiaomi as a serious long-term competitor but notes it currently only competes in China, where Tesla is already capacity-constrained at one factory.

4. Segment comparison is critical: the top-selling car in China by volume is priced around $10,000 USD. The Geely Xingyuan (Xingguang) outsold both Tesla and Xiaomi in China during the month at approximately 70,000 RMB, or roughly $10,000. White points out this is an entirely different segment, and notes that Tesla's gross margin on a Model Y could exceed the entire retail cost of that vehicle — making unit-count comparisons largely meaningless without segment context.

5. FSD approval in China is imminent, and Xpeng's CEO is publicly challenging Tesla's autonomy leadership. Xpeng's CEO stated in a March 2026 interview during China's "Two Sessions" political conference that Tesla's unsupervised autonomous driving "has not yet been realized" and declared China the global leader in the field. He predicted L4 autonomous driving — where a vehicle handles all tasks without human intervention within designated areas — will be fully rolled out in China within one to three years. Brian White agrees this timeline is plausible, noting Huawei functions as the "Intel Inside" of Chinese FSD, supplying technology to roughly a dozen manufacturers. Xpeng has already partnered with Volkswagen and is running an internal robo-taxi pilot program in Guangzhou, with plans for a public beta by end of 2026.

6. Tesla's FSD in China currently underperforms its US version. White explains that Tesla trained its Chinese FSD largely on YouTube videos of Chinese driving rather than on extensive local data, making it noticeably inferior to the US product. Engineers at Xpeng who regularly travel to Silicon Valley to test Tesla FSD confirmed this assessment to White directly.

7. Brian White tested Xpeng's VA 2.0 (their FSD equivalent) twice and saw rapid improvement in six weeks. On his first test two months ago, the system was uncomfortably aggressive, triggering proximity warnings when pulling up to stoplights. On his second visit six weeks later, the car was assertive but smooth — comfortable starts, stops, and merges. Notably, it handled a scenario involving 30 scooters coming the wrong way down a one-way street, a situation White believes would cause US-trained Tesla FSD to simply stop and wait. He rates Xpeng's VA 2.0 as comparable to "a very early version 13" of Tesla FSD.

8. Brian White believes a larger Tesla vehicle, possibly the Model Y L (six-seater), is being produced at the Texas Gigafactory. While Elon Musk previously suggested the Model Y L would not come to the US until late 2026 at the earliest, White states he has received firsthand insights from people in Texas suggesting production is already underway. He acknowledges he could be wrong and it may be a different large vehicle, but is confident "a bigger car" is being made there now.

9. The Model Y L has been spotted uncamouflaged in Australia and camouflaged in South Korea, signaling imminent availability. Both markets are served by Giga Shanghai, which is already producing the vehicle, so distribution should proceed quickly. White also believes Giga Berlin will begin producing the Model Y L shortly.

10. Audi's CEO says gasoline engines have a future well into the 2030s, a strategy both hosts view as a strategic miscalculation. Audi CEO Gernot Döllner told Car and Driver that faltering EV sales and strong ICE demand are prompting a more flexible powertrain strategy. He suggested drivetrain concepts are "swinging back to ICE" and that the company may develop a next-generation combustion engine platform if US demand warrants it. White interprets "people don't want EVs" as really meaning "people don't want our EVs at our prices," and criticizes Audi for failing to build demand the way Tesla did with the Model 3. He notes that by 2030, autonomous capability will be widely expected in vehicles, and an ICE-focused Audi will be playing entirely the wrong game.

Conclusions and Predictions

Brian White predicts that by 2030 autonomous driving capability will be a standard consumer expectation, making ICE-focused legacy automakers increasingly irrelevant. He suggests Audi should stop developing new engines, extract maximum value from existing platforms, and aggressively invest in compelling EV products — ideally a small, sporty EV in the spirit of the Audi TT. He also calls out the short CEO tenure cycle (typically three-year contracts) as structurally incentivizing short-term thinking at legacy automakers. On the China autonomy race, he considers a 1–4 year timeline to L4 for leading Chinese manufacturers to be reasonable but notes the difficulty of achieving the final "handful of nines" in reliability, especially given China's uniquely chaotic traffic environment where physical contact between vehicles is relatively normalized.
