This video, published April 1, 2026, features host Herbert and guest Brian White (of the YouTube channel Futuraza) discussing Tesla's potential upcoming vehicles, the expanding Cybercab testing footprint across the U.S., the growth of Tesla's unsupervised robotaxi geofence in Austin, and the broader vision of Tesla as a full transportation ecosystem. The core argument is that Tesla is methodically building toward autonomous transportation across multiple vehicle form factors and infrastructure layers, from Cybercabs and Model Ys to the Vegas Loop.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **New vehicle teased by Elon Musk:** Elon Musk recently posted that "something way cooler than a minivan is coming," prompting speculation about what Tesla's next vehicle might be. An old Tesla lineup image previously shared by the company showed silhouettes of vehicles that appeared to be placeholders, with some interpreted as a Cybercab, a human-drivable version (potentially dubbed the NV91), and possibly an unknown third vehicle — though Brian White notes the two end silhouettes appear identical, suggesting they were just placeholders and the Roadster was likely omitted entirely from that image.

2. **Cyber SUV speculation:** Images from what appears to be a Tesla design studio in France show a render or sketch of a Cyber SUV-style vehicle in the background, fueling speculation. Brian White finds the Cyber SUV visually appealing — he says it looks better than the Cybertruck — and notes it could potentially use a plastic-impregnated steel material similar to the Cybercab rather than stainless steel, reducing weight and avoiding the need for a new paint shop. However, he is skeptical it will happen soon, citing the significant engineering overhead required (including a different rear casting to allow a third row) and Tesla's stated focus on autonomy over new human-driven vehicles.

3. **Cybertruck floor space and possible Cyber SUV incentive:** Herbert references a Giga Texas factory floor map discussed in a prior interview with Joe Tegtmeyer, which showed that the floor space dedicated to Cybertruck production is massive relative to current demand, while Cybercab has a smaller footprint. Brian White finds this the most compelling argument for a Cyber SUV — that Tesla may need to better utilize existing Cybertruck production equipment rather than let it sit underused.

4. **Model Y L as the most likely near-term new product:** Brian White expresses high confidence that the Model Y L (a longer version of the Model Y) is the most likely upcoming new vehicle for the U.S. market. He cites the observation of what appeared to be a Model Y body-in-white arriving in an inbound crate at Giga Texas — noting that outbound vehicles would be sealed inside before shipment, making this likely an inbound validation unit from either Giga Berlin or Giga Shanghai. He draws a parallel to how Tesla previously shipped Model 3 and Model Y bodies-in-white from Fremont to Shanghai during production validation. He also notes that scheduled downtime at Giga Texas is coming up in the next week or two, which he speculates could be related to line changes. A Model Y XL (three-row stretched version) is also discussed but considered far less confirmed.

5. **Robovan not on near-term roadmap:** Brian White states that when he asked Tesla engineers about the Robovan, they said they were "not even thinking about it," and that either Franz von Holzhausen or Elon Musk confirmed it is not yet being actively pursued — describing it as something they are excited about in concept but "not time" for yet. Elon's comment that the upcoming vehicle is "something much cooler than a minivan" is interpreted as ruling out the Robovan as the teased product.

## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab testing footprint — 36 units spotted across 7 regions:** According to the Cybercab tracker, 36 Cybercabs have been spotted across the country, across seven testing regions: Austin, Bay Area, Chicago, Alaska, Boston, Buffalo, and Washington D.C. Twenty-seven of those are in Austin, likely because they are being built at Giga Texas and Austin is the primary robotaxi testing market. Additional sightings in locations like Oklahoma and Los Angeles suggest the true count is higher than tracked. Joe Tegtmeyer has also observed Cybercabs disappearing from the outbound lot at Giga Texas within a day or two of being spotted, indicating they are actively being deployed.

2. **Cybercab crash testing underway:** Joe Tegtmeyer captured footage showing 12 Cybercabs at a crash testing facility, with several showing signs of completed or in-progress tests. Eight additional Cybercabs were spotted at the Alpine lot on the same day. The video also briefly highlights a unique aerial-style view of a Cybercab's front hood area and door from above.

3. **Model Y validation rigs spotted across multiple cities:** In addition to Cybercabs, Model Ys with validation-mounted sensor rigs are being spotted in multiple cities simultaneously, including Garland, Texas (near Dallas-Fort Worth). Brian White clarifies that these vehicles are not doing HD mapping from scratch like competitors — Tesla already has the maps it needs and is using LiDAR-equipped validation vehicles to confirm the accuracy of existing map data. He argues that Tesla will likely be able to cross-reference data sets efficiently enough that not every street will need a dedicated validation drive.

4. **Unsupervised geofence in Austin expanded:** The unsupervised Tesla robotaxi geofence in Austin has been expanded, with a user reporting that the unsupervised Model Y robotaxi now crosses the river and reaches downtown areas it previously did not serve. Tesla Robotaxi's official account responded to the user's post with "Map found," confirming the expansion. The broader area around Austin still uses safety operators (referred to as "safety hitchhikers"), while only the smaller confirmed geofenced zone is fully unsupervised.

5. **Waymo data on robotaxi trip occupancy:** Herbert references data from Waymo showing that 90% of robotaxi trips have two or fewer passengers — with 71% being single-passenger trips and 5% being zero-passenger (likely repositioning) trips. This is used to defend the Cybercab's two-seat design, with the argument that Model Ys in the fleet can handle the roughly 10% of trips requiring three or more passengers.

6. **Fleet ownership advantage for quality control:** Brian White argues that because Tesla owns the Cybercab fleet (rather than selling directly to consumers), any quality issue or recall is far easier to manage — Tesla can schedule service on its own timeline, run overnight repair sessions, and cycle vehicles through quickly without inconveniencing individual owners.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **Vegas Loop as a stepping stone to full autonomy:** Elon Musk posted that Teslas are currently operating in tunnels under Las Vegas transporting millions of people via the Boring Company's Vegas Loop. The newest station opened at the Fontainebleau Resort. Brian White explains that the Loop is not yet autonomous because Tesla did not want to create a software fork earlier than necessary — maintaining two separate code bases would have been costly. He believes the Loop will transition to autonomous operation within a few months of the wider surface-level robotaxi rollout, at which point a unified software layer can be applied.

2. **FSD reliability in personal use:** Brian White states that it has been approximately one year since he has needed to make any critical intervention while using FSD, across two countries and three states. He describes a recent trip home from a doctor's appointment where FSD completed the entire drive including pulling into the driveway without any input. He contrasts this with his recent experience in China with human drivers, expressing strong preference for autonomous driving.

3. **Robotaxi vs. unsupervised FSD complexity:** Brian White argues that robotaxi is actually easier to solve than full unsupervised FSD for personal vehicles, because the robotaxi fleet has dedicated remote operators at HQ on standby — whereas personal FSD currently relies on the driver as the safety backstop. He notes that when Waymo makes a mistake, the public understands it is the system; when a personal FSD vehicle makes a mistake, it is unclear whether it was the driver or the software.

4. **Cybercab FSD form factor advantage:** Brian White argues that the Cybercab's smaller form factor may actually make it easier to deploy as a self-driving vehicle than larger models like the Cybertruck, which had early FSD examples clipping curbs. A smaller vehicle would have more margin within lane boundaries. He contrasts this with early Cybertruck FSD, where the large footprint created curb-clipping issues.
