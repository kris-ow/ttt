This video is a conversation between host Brian (Futurazza) and Jordan Gizigi from "The Limiting Factor," a battery technology analyst, recorded on April 1st. The discussion centers on Donut Labs, a startup that had promised delivery-ready solid-state batteries in customers' hands by Q1 2026, and why that promise — like so many "miracle battery" announcements before it — failed to materialize. The core thesis is that the gap between laboratory battery performance and real-world commercial viability is enormous, and that Donut Labs has been engaging in selective, misleading marketing rather than transparent scientific disclosure.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Donut Labs' unfulfilled Q1 promise:** Donut Labs had publicly announced that delivery-ready solid-state batteries would be in customer hands by Q1 2026, promising 400 watt hours per kilogram energy density and five-minute charging, targeted at electric motorcycles. The host says he expressed skepticism from the start, and as of April 1st, no delivery has occurred. The Wall Street Journal had run a headline asking "Will this miracle battery finally change your mind about EVs?" amplifying the hype around the claims.

2. **Battery specs claimed at CES were likely an "OR" list, not an "AND" list:** Jordan explains that Donut Labs presented a "laundry list of the highest performing specs we've ever seen in any battery" at CES. His assessment is that under certain laboratory conditions, the company can probably meet each spec individually with heavy caveats, but cannot hit all specifications simultaneously. A recent test cycled a cell brought up to 100 degrees Celsius, and after approximately 10 to 50 cycles, the battery was only providing 50% capacity — far short of the advertised performance.

3. **Lab-to-real-world manufacturing gap:** Jordan uses the analogy of making shoes by hand versus manufacturing them in a factory at high volumes and low cost. He explains that batteries placed into actual products must meet durability requirements across all temperature and weather conditions, function within a pack shared by hundreds of other cells, and sustain performance for over a decade. This is fundamentally different from what can be demonstrated in a controlled lab setting.

4. **Donut Labs' April 1st video walkback:** On the morning of the video recording, Donut Labs released a new video in which the founder acknowledged that the battery that had been tested is not actually the final battery, and that a version two with "much better performance" is coming. He also stated that cycle life is complicated and that skeptics wouldn't believe the data anyway — citing over 100% Coulombic efficiency on the first few cycles as a misunderstood proof point. Jordan explains this is a well-understood, mechanically obvious phenomenon related to lithium ion behavior in anode overhangs during battery formation and is not evidence of superior performance.

5. **Donut Labs may be a middleman, not a manufacturer:** Jordan speculates that Donut Labs may not actually be producing the batteries themselves, but rather purchasing lab samples from another company that is the actual manufacturer. In this interpretation, the Donut Labs founder may have genuinely been sold on the technology and is acting as a middleman between a battery manufacturer and the end customer (Verge, an electric motorcycle company), rather than knowingly perpetrating a fraud.

6. **Semi-solid state batteries already in market — MG4:** In contrast to Donut Labs' undelivered promises, the video notes that the MG4 is described as the world's first mass-market EV with a semi-solid state battery and is entering the European market. Jordan explains that semi-solid state batteries use a small amount of gel or "jelly" between layers to address the interface contact problem in fully solid-state batteries, where two "brick-like" solid layers cannot achieve perfect contact regardless of pressure.

7. **QuantumCape as a more credible comparison:** Jordan references QuantumCape as a better-funded, more credible solid-state battery developer. QuantumCape originally targeted around 400 watt hours per kilogram energy density, and what they have now is in the range of just above 300 watt hours per kilogram. Despite being better-resourced and more transparent, it will be approximately seven years after their original announcement before a commercial product is seen.

8. **Solid-state batteries are a 10–15 year horizon, not a step change:** Jordan's assessment is that solid-state batteries may become a genuine improvement on liquid electrolyte batteries in approximately 10 to 15 years, but the transition will be gradual rather than a sudden step change. He notes that current solid-state batteries do offer range improvements but come with significant additional costs, and that every battery chemistry involves trade-offs. Progress in the field comes from tens of thousands of scientists and engineers making incremental molecular-level improvements over time.

9. **"Miracle battery" announcements are endemic:** Jordan notes that miracle battery announcements appear roughly two to three times per week across the industry. Every battery startup must claim superiority over existing technology to attract investment, leading to a constant stream of overclaimed performance specs. Technologies like LFP (lithium iron phosphate) and sodium-ion, once dismissed by skeptics, have proven viable — though sodium-ion research slowed when lithium prices fell. Sodium-ion batteries are now appearing in passenger vehicles in China with growing adoption.

## Market & Competition

1. **Selective marketing versus scientific fraud:** The hosts debate whether Donut Labs' founder is lying or engaged in selective truth-telling for marketing purposes. Jordan's interpretation, based on the founder's behavior in online interactions and his April 1st video, is that the founder views this as a "marketing game" — giving selective truths to generate excitement without technically lying outright, while consistently omitting critical caveats and withholding data that would qualify his claims. Brian draws a parallel to Thomas Edison's investor demonstrations with incandescent bulbs (swapping bulbs between investor presentations) and Steve Jobs' iPhone demonstration (using different phones between demo steps to avoid failure).

2. **No identified victims, no clear legal liability:** The hosts agree that as long as Donut Labs has not accepted new donations or investments since the CES show, there may be no legal victims and therefore no crime — just a "social experiment." The question of who would have legal standing to bring a case is raised but left unresolved.
