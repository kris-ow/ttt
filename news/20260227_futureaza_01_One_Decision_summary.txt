This video is a conversation between host Brian of the Futureaza channel and Norton, operator of Cybertruck.eu, recorded in early 2026. The discussion covers Tesla's position in Europe across several fronts: the Cybertruck's unofficial presence on the continent, the pending approval of FSD supervised mode, the prospects for robotaxi deployment, and the ongoing sales decline Tesla is experiencing in European markets. Norton speaks from firsthand experience as someone who legally registered a Cybertruck in the Czech Republic and maintains contacts within Dutch Tesla regulatory circles.

Key Points:

1. The Cybertruck is technically impossible to buy officially in the EU, but Norton successfully imported and registered one in Prague. To comply with EU regulations, the vehicle required changes including converting rear lights from red to amber, repositioning lights to meet placement rules, adding a fog light, and covering or rounding sharp edges to meet pedestrian safety standards. Norton notes more Cybertrucks are slowly making their way to Europe through similar case-by-case processes.

2. Tesla is unlikely to ever officially sell the Cybertruck in Europe through standard manufacturer channels. Norton and Brian both agree that the extent of modifications required, combined with the European market's general disinterest in large trucks, makes it economically and logistically impractical. Elon Musk himself reportedly doesn't want to invest the effort given that even US Cybertruck sales face challenges.

3. FSD supervised mode is on the verge of regulatory approval in the Netherlands, likely by April 2026. The Dutch vehicle certification authority, the RDW, was originally expected to approve FSD supervised in February 2026. That deadline passed, and March approval is also looking unlikely. Norton's contacts within the Tesla Owners Netherlands community suggest April is the realistic window for an actual software update enabling supervised FSD for existing Dutch owners who have already purchased the feature.

4. FSD in Europe is specifically classified as Level 2 autonomy, meaning the driver remains responsible. Norton clarifies the important regulatory distinction: Level 3 autonomy (eyes-off in designated areas) requires the manufacturer to assume legal responsibility, and BMW is already pursuing Level 3 certification in Europe. Ford's BlueCruise is also reportedly undergoing the process. Tesla's FSD supervised does not claim Level 3 status, keeping liability with the driver, which is a key reason why its regulatory path differs from competitors.

5. Czech Republic and Germany have already passed laws allowing certified Level 3 systems on highways. This creates a regulatory framework that is gradually opening up, but certification is mandatory and manufacturer liability is the central sticking point that determines which systems qualify.

6. FSD ride-alongs are currently booming across Europe, with demo sessions in countries including the Czech Republic booking up completely and being extended. Norton is personally attending a ride-along the following day. Feedback from Dutch participants is that FSD drives reliably and safely, handling construction zones, pedestrians, and cyclists correctly, though it is described as "boring" because it simply does what it is supposed to do without drama. The system is still being tuned for European road conditions.

7. Robotaxi approval in Europe is tied to new UNECE (UN Economic Commission for Europe) legislation being developed to create a global framework for autonomous driving. Several European countries are already piloting autonomous vehicles, but these are limited to controlled environments and pre-set routes rather than open-road, general-purpose operation. A broader robotaxi rollout is likely years away pending this legislation.

8. Tesla is in its 13th consecutive month of year-over-year sales decline in Europe. Brian notes that overall European automotive sales were up approximately 1 to 1.3 percent last year, and EV market share is growing due to upcoming mandates. Tesla's decline is happening against a rising tide, meaning it is actively losing share. Germany is cited as particularly difficult, where Volkswagen, Audi, Porsche, Mercedes, and BMW all have home-field advantage with competitive EV lineups.

9. BYD is the most disruptive competitive threat in Europe. BYD sold approximately 18,000 vehicles in Europe in a recent quarter and is building a factory in Hungary that will allow it to produce very cheap EVs within the EU, potentially bypassing import tariffs. Norton draws a comparison to BYD's strategy in Nepal, where it patiently built a dealer network and now accounts for roughly 60 percent of all new car sales in that country. The same patient market-entry strategy is playing out in Europe.

10. Chinese brands are gaining ground partly through legacy brand acquisitions. Volvo, owned by Geely, continues to perform well in Europe because consumers still associate it with Swedish safety and premium quality. MG, a defunct British sports car brand bought by a Chinese company, sells compact SUVs successfully in the UK largely on brand nostalgia, even though buyers understand the cars are Chinese-made. The MG example is presented as evidence that brand memory confers a measurable sales advantage.

11. Legacy European automakers have squandered their transition window. Both speakers argue that companies like Volkswagen wasted time and capital on hydrogen development and by delaying full EV commitment in favor of hybrids. The Nokia comparison is raised as a cautionary analogy for automakers that fail to adapt quickly enough to platform shifts.
