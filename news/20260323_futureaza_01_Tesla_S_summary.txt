## Electric Vehicles

1. **Tesla dominates Norway year-to-date sales.** Tesla outsold every other car brand in Norway combined for the month, with a chart from Roland Percher showing Tesla's share dwarfing all competitors. The host frames Norway as the "ultimate testing ground" for EV adoption and notes this kind of outperformance recurs every quarter in pockets of Europe.

2. **UK sales show delivery-wave pattern, not structural decline.** UK Tesla sales appeared weak in early data but surged to number one once shipments arrived. The host criticizes legacy media analysts for misreading delivery wave timing as a demand signal, calling it a persistent and predictable pattern every quarter.

3. **Tesla to buy $4.3 billion of LG Energy battery cells from a disbanded GM plant.** This was major news the prior Tuesday, though the host notes he had covered it two weeks earlier. The deal reflects Tesla's ongoing effort to secure battery supply at scale.

4. **Tesla real-world range accuracy within 3% of EPA ratings.** A comparison of EPA-rated versus real-world EV range found Tesla missed by only 3%, while Ford underperformed by 16% and Lucid underperformed by 13%. BMW beat its EPA rating by 19%, Mercedes and Mini by 12%. The host considers Tesla's result strong, noting internal combustion vehicles would likely fare worse under similar scrutiny.

5. **Stellantis gains access to Tesla Supercharger network.** Stellantis is now able to use select Superchargers, making it the latest legacy automaker to join Tesla's charging ecosystem. The host welcomes the development while noting it applies to select locations only.

6. **Bentley abandons plans for four new EVs scheduled by 2035.** The host explains that luxury ICE brands struggle with electrification because EV performance requires genuine engineering depth — not just premium materials and powerful motors — and Bentley lacks the volume and engineering capability to do it independently. He suggests partnering as a coachbuilder on third-party EV platforms would be a more viable path.

7. **Tesla Roadster timeline remains uncertain.** Elon Musk indicated maybe by end of April, but the host notes events announced that far in advance tend to slip. No concrete Roadster news exists as of the broadcast.

8. **Tesla approved for building in South Haven, Mississippi.** A brief mention that Tesla received local approval for construction in South Haven, Mississippi, described as a positive for the state.

## Energy

1. **Tesla in talks with Chinese firms to buy $2.9 billion worth of solar equipment.** Sources report Tesla is negotiating to purchase solar manufacturing equipment from Chinese companies despite import tariffs. The host notes this would help Tesla bring advanced solar panel production in-house — specifically referencing a different panel composition (described as "spacera solar panels") — and could support both terrestrial deployment and potentially power needs for SpaceX's planned space-based compute infrastructure. Tesla's vertical integration ethos is cited as the driver.

2. **BYD achieves 80% charge in 5 minutes via megawatt charging using vertical integration.** BYD can charge its new vehicle to 80% in approximately 5 minutes and to full in about 9 minutes. The key enabler is total vertical integration: BYD mines materials, manufactures batteries, owns chargers, and controls the grid-side storage (Megapack equivalents). The battery chemistry is LMFP (lithium manganese iron phosphate) rather than standard LFP, offering higher energy density at some cost to cycle life — though still rated for approximately 600,000 miles versus the prior 1 million miles. The host notes on-site Megapack deployments already make this grid-compatible and predicts other manufacturers will simply buy BYD batteries.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **US regulators deepen FSD investigation while closing a separate defect petition.** Regulators closed a defect petition covering over 2 million vehicles related to a specific FSD software issue, but opened renewed scrutiny under a separate probe. The host dismisses media framing that Tesla is "one step from recall," arguing the regulatory process has many intermediate steps and the coverage is analytically lazy.

2. **Nvidia's Drive platform adopted by BYD, Isuzu, Nissan, and others for Level 4 vehicles.** Multiple automakers have adopted Nvidia's autonomous driving system. The host characterizes Nvidia as a "picks and shovels" provider in the autonomous gold rush — beneficial to Nvidia but unlikely to meaningfully accelerate competitors, since even a turnkey solution working today would require three to five years of new vehicle development before deployment.

## Market & Competition

1. **Rivian and Uber announce partnership for up to 50,000 autonomous robotaxis — but Rivian has no autonomous system.** Rivian's current driver assistance system cannot handle roundabouts or sharp turns, and the host says the company has been "very careful" not to show it publicly. He interprets Uber's strategy as broadly partnering with every potential autonomous provider hoping something sticks. Rivian also announced it no longer expects to achieve adjusted core profit in 2027 as it ramps R&D on its autonomous program. The host notes Rivian is approximately $24 billion underwater, a level no company has historically survived.

2. **Uber also partnered with Lucid and others in broad autonomous fleet strategy.** The host frames Uber's approach as scattershot rather than strategic — essentially offering purchase commitments to anyone who can deliver a working autonomous vehicle.

3. **Terafab: Tesla/SpaceX/xAI semiconductor fabrication facility announced by Elon Musk.** At a Saturday night event in downtown Austin, Elon Musk unveiled plans for "Terafab," a joint venture between Tesla, SpaceX, and xAI to build a semiconductor fabrication facility targeting a terawatt of annual AI compute at a 2-nanometer process node. The pilot line is planned near or on the east campus of Giga Texas, with an eventual offsite facility at full scale. At full capacity, it would match roughly 70% of TSMC's global output. Barclays estimates a bull-case cost of $50 billion but notes realistic costs could be 10 times that — $500 billion. China has spent hundreds of billions and achieved only 7nm precision, partly due to ASML lithography embargoes that Tesla/SpaceX/xAI may not face. The host is skeptical about execution timelines, drawing parallels to Dojo arriving too late, and notes that semiconductor fabs produce chips with a shelf life — late chips may already be too energy-inefficient to be competitive.
