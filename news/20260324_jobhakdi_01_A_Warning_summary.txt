This video from Jo Bhakdi (channel: jobhakdi) addresses a specific financial risk for Tesla LEAP options holders stemming from a potential Tesla-SpaceX merger. The core argument is that even a low double-digit probability (~10–15%) of a merger occurring in 2026 creates a non-trivial tail risk for holders of far out-of-the-money Tesla call options, and Jo Bhakdi adjusts his personal strategy accordingly. The video also includes a broader bullish commentary on SpaceX's long-term potential relative to Tesla.

## Market & Competition
1. Jo Bhakdi cites commentary from "Alexander Mertz" — described as someone sophisticated in understanding Tesla's legal and shareholder dynamics — who outlined the most likely Tesla-SpaceX merger timeline. Her assessment is that a merger *could* theoretically happen in 2026, requiring Tesla shareholder approval, but is unlikely because regulators reviewing a merger of this scale typically take 12 to 24 months. Jo Bhakdi agrees with her assessment and characterizes the probability of a merger happening in 2026 as a "low double-digit percent" — roughly 10–15% — which he considers non-trivial.

2. Jo Bhakdi discusses two distinct merger scenarios with very different implications. In the first scenario, SpaceX acquires Tesla: Tesla stock would be delisted, and LEAP option holders with strike prices above the acquisition price would lose their entire investment, as options would accelerate to expiration at the merger price. In the second scenario, Tesla acquires SpaceX: Tesla would issue a large number of new shares to SpaceX shareholders, causing significant share dilution, but existing Tesla call options would likely be unaffected. The share dilution would not necessarily cause a share price drop — it depends on market sentiment.

3. Jo Bhakdi expresses concern that if Tesla were to acquire SpaceX, it could delay Tesla's financial inflection point. His reasoning is that Tesla, currently valued at approximately $1.5 trillion, would effectively become a $3–3.5 trillion company by absorbing SpaceX, but SpaceX currently generates significantly less cash flow than Tesla. While SpaceX has enormous long-term cash flow potential, the near-term drag could weigh on Tesla's valuation trajectory.

4. Jo Bhakdi makes a strong bullish case for SpaceX, describing it as a more compelling opportunity than Tesla. He frames SpaceX's satellite/space infrastructure ambition (referencing a "Dyson sphere" concept) as having an effectively infinite total addressable market — described as "millions of times the size of the human economy" — while being cash flow positive from day one and enabled by AI. He argues this makes it easier to execute than EVs, FSD, Optimus, or Starship individually.

## Financials
1. Jo Bhakdi describes his personal LEAP options strategy in detail. He explains that when Tesla drops sharply, he converts stock holdings into LEAP call options with long expiration dates (e.g., December 2027) and high strike prices (e.g., $500–$800), accepting a high breakeven (e.g., $580 for an $80 option at a $500 strike) in exchange for large upside leverage. He states he previously rolled over 50% of his entire stock portfolio into Tesla LEAPs when Tesla traded around $220, buying $600 and $700 strike options, and that this strategy significantly outperformed holding shares outright.

2. Due to the merger tail risk, Jo Bhakdi says he is modifying his LEAP strategy. He would no longer put as large a proportion of his portfolio into far out-of-the-money LEAPs as he did previously. He frames his personal risk tolerance as being willing to lose approximately 5% of his money on the merger tail risk, but not the much larger allocation he used before. He notes that lower strike prices (e.g., $400–$450 if Tesla is at $300–$350) reduce this risk because they are more likely to be in the money even at a merger acquisition price. He still considers Tesla likely to be above $800 by end of 2027, though acknowledges this is not guaranteed. He also notes Tesla could fall below $300 in the near term due to macro factors such as a market crash, independent of Tesla-specific fundamentals.
