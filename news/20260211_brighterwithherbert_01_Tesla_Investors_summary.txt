This weekly co-stream between Herbert (brighterwithherbert), Hans Nelson, and Nick Gibbs (Investing Against the Grain) covers three major storylines for Tesla investors as of early March 2026: departures at xAI following its SpaceX acquisition, a cluster of positive robo taxi developments, and Elon Musk's emerging vision for a moon-based AI compute infrastructure. The hosts assess what these developments mean for Tesla shareholders, debating whether the SpaceX-xAI-Tesla consolidation thesis is a headwind or a significant catalyst.

1. xAI co-founder departures: noise or signal? About half of xAI's roughly 12-person founding team has now left, with most departures happening over an extended period rather than all at once. Hans argues this is broadly comparable to OpenAI, where only about two original founders remain, and attributes it to the natural lifecycle of early-stage companies — founders chase 100,000x returns, and once a liquidity event arrives (the SpaceX acquisition of xAI), many cash out or seek the next startup opportunity. Nick adds that anyone trying to spin off independently now faces an almost insurmountable infrastructure gap: Micron's high-bandwidth memory is sold out through 2026 into 2027, Nvidia has a multi-year GPU backlog, and companies without a massive existing profit engine (like Meta's ad business, Google's search, or Amazon's AWS) simply cannot compete at scale. Herbert raises the possibility that some departures may be Elon-initiated restructuring rather than voluntary exits.

2. The xAI all-hands revelation mid-stream. During the discussion, a viewer named Xander flagged that xAI had publicly posted a 45-minute all-hands meeting in which Musk announced a new organizational structure. The accompanying slide explicitly addressed departures, stating that at a certain scale, some people are better suited for earlier stages of a company. Herbert interprets this as confirmation that the departures were at least partly orchestrated top-down as part of a deliberate phase change following the SpaceX acquisition, rather than a talent crisis. Hans frames it as xAI moving from startup mode to a more mature organizational structure commensurate with its new ambitions and compute resources.

3. Seven concurrent robo taxi catalysts listed by Herbert. The unsupervised robo taxi fleet has grown from five to seven cars in a matter of days. Tesla posted 53 Cybercab manufacturing job openings and is hiring AI safety operators near Dallas. Cybercabs were spotted at the end of Giga Texas's production line with logistics testing already underway. Tesla activated a dedicated FSD training AI data center in China. Europe is reportedly close to regulatory approval for FSD. The U.S. Congress advanced the Self-Drive Act through committee, with a potential law by end of summer if proceedings continue smoothly. Lemonade Insurance rolled out its 50% FSD discount in Oregon as promised and is expanding to eight more states. The hosts note that while progress feels slower than anticipated before the June 2025 launch, robo taxi deployment follows an exponential curve that will eventually feel sudden. Nick predicts the stock will see a meaningful move upward by June 2026 as Wall Street finds autonomy's revenue model easy to model — fleet size, miles driven, per-mile charge, city count.

4. The broader AI infrastructure consolidation argument. Nick makes a forceful case that the AI race has become a game exclusively for companies with massive, unrelated profit engines funding compute buildout. He predicts OpenAI is in serious trouble and Anthropic may be as well, while Meta, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, and the Musk constellation will be the survivors. He draws an analogy to Tesla selling EV credits to legacy automakers — OpenAI and Anthropic are currently paying cloud companies (Amazon, Microsoft, Google) to run their training, thereby funding the very competitors who will eventually displace them. Amazon is spending $200 billion on AI infrastructure in 2025 alone.

5. Elon's moon/space compute vision and what it means for Tesla. Musk has been tweeting extensively about building a lunar base as a platform for AI satellite manufacturing. Hans explains the strategic logic: Earth has finite atoms and energy for thinking machines, but space offers far more of both, and SpaceX is already positioned to get there. The proposed lunar factory would manufacture AI satellites and launch them via rail gun directly from the moon's surface. Hans estimates the foundations for this will be laid around 2028-2029, with it becoming a tangible investment thesis in the 3-to-5-year window. Tesla's role would likely include chip fabrication and solar cell manufacturing, given the overlap between those two processes. Hans describes the Tesla-XAI relationship as analogous to the two halves of the frontal lobe: Tesla is building the motor cortex (Optimus, physical movement) while xAI is building the prefrontal cortex (reasoning, world modeling), and they are destined to converge in Optimus.

6. The Tesla-SpaceX merger debate. The hosts debate whether a merger or reverse merger between Tesla, SpaceX, and xAI is likely. Hans puts it near 50/50, citing regulatory complexity as the key unknown. Nick argues the synergies are greater than a simple sum of parts — reduced legal friction between entities, leaner teams, and elimination of intercompany accounting. Herbert references an analyst model suggesting Tesla shareholders would likely receive a valuation premium in any merger structure to compensate for SpaceX's currently lower cash flow relative to Tesla's roughly $100 billion in revenue and approximately $12-16 billion in annual profit. The group agrees that if SpaceX and XAI do not merge with Tesla, they will likely go public separately, which would dilute Tesla's "Musk premium" as investors gain other vehicles for Musk exposure.

7. Risks to the bull case. Nick identifies two primary risks: Tesla failing to maintain its current acceleration in robo taxi deployment, and a macro deflationary spiral triggered by the Federal Reserve being too slow to cut rates, leading to a self-reinforcing cycle of reduced spending, layoffs, and asset selling. He also briefly mentions the risk of renewed regulatory or legal hostility toward Elon Musk personally as a lower-probability but non-zero black swan.
