This video from the "futureaza" channel is part of a recurring series titled "Are They Doomed?" in which host Brian evaluates Tesla across several dimensions — engineering culture, demand, cost controls, cash on hand, and roadmap — to argue that despite growing skepticism and competitive pressure, Tesla is not doomed. The core thesis is that Tesla's pivot away from being purely a car company toward autonomous transportation-as-a-service, energy storage, Optimus, and chip manufacturing gives it a roadmap that competitors cannot easily match. Brian scores Tesla across categories and arrives at an overall score of 74 out of a possible higher total, which he describes as the best score in the series so far.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Declining Global Delivery Trend:** Brian presents Tesla's global delivery figures showing a peak of approximately 1.8 million vehicles followed by two consecutive years of declining deliveries. He acknowledges this trend is "not great for demand" and notes that Giga Berlin and Giga Texas both carry surplus capacity they are not fully utilizing. He scores Tesla's demand a six out of ten — "better than average, not great" — while noting this could improve as FSD matures and robotaxi deployment begins.

2. **Cybertruck Economics Under Scrutiny:** Brian flags the Cybertruck as a cost outlier relative to its sales volumes, stating that its costs are "out of line" for the number of units being sold. However, he frames the Cybertruck as a necessary engineering stepping stone — used to validate Tesla's 48-volt architecture, steer-by-wire system, and other technologies that feed directly into the Cybercab platform. He stops short of calling it a success but sees strategic justification for its development.

3. **FSD as a Demand Catalyst:** Brian argues that Full Self-Driving is a meaningful differentiator that drives purchase intent in markets where it is available, with some U.S. customers unwilling to buy a car without that capability. He qualifies this by noting that in many countries FSD is not available, making Tesla "just a car" in those markets — a position where competitive pressure from companies like BYD is more acute.

## Market & Competition

1. **BYD Surpasses Tesla in Global BEV Sales:** Brian briefly acknowledges that BYD has officially surpassed Tesla in global battery electric vehicle sales, referencing what appears to be a mid-2025 article. He downplays the significance of this milestone, noting that BYD has its own challenges and that surpassing Tesla in unit sales does not mean Tesla is finished, particularly given Tesla's pivot toward services and software.

2. **Competitive Moat in Robotaxi Economics:** Brian argues that neither Uber, Waymo, Lyft, nor any other competitor can match the cost structure Tesla is building for its robotaxi network, particularly through the Cybercab's design decisions. He frames this as a structural, engineering-driven advantage rather than a temporary lead.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD as a Global Expansion Opportunity:** Brian discusses FSD's current limitations — particularly its unavailability in many international markets — as a factor suppressing demand today but representing significant upside as regulatory approvals expand. He frames unsupervised FSD as the unlock for a new category of demand, including from buyers who currently would not purchase a non-autonomous vehicle.

## Robotaxi

1. **Robotaxi Is Inevitable Regardless of Belief:** Brian makes a conviction argument that the robotaxi transition is coming whether or not skeptics accept it, comparing disbelief in it to disbelief in gravity. He frames the Cybercab's engineering and cost structure as uniquely advantaged, and states that no competitor can match Tesla on price once the network is deployed at scale.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus Described as a Near-Ready but Deliberately Held Product:** Brian suggests that Optimus could be sold today in its current form and would find buyers, but Tesla is choosing to wait — letting "the soufflé rise a bit" before release. He raises the question of how many units Tesla plans to sell versus retain for internal use as robots-as-a-service, noting that a keep-heavy strategy would require significant upfront capital that would weigh on the balance sheet.

## Energy

1. **Megapack Deployments Accelerating:** Brian highlights a GitHub tracker maintained by a community member that catalogs global Megapack installations, describing the deployment pace as fast and growing. He characterizes each Megapack unit as generating hundreds of thousands of dollars in profit and notes that the energy storage segment is already revenue-positive on an ongoing basis. He calls this part of the roadmap "very exciting."

## Financials

1. **$44.1 Billion Cash on Hand — Impressive for a Car Company, Insufficient for Tesla's Ambitions:** Brian reports Tesla holds $44.1 billion in cash, which he describes as exceptional by automotive industry standards but potentially insufficient given the capital requirements of Optimus deployment, robotaxi fleet buildout, and the Terafab chip manufacturing facility. He scores cash on hand a six out of ten for this reason.

2. **Terafab Cost Estimates Range Widely — Up to $50 Billion and Beyond:** Brian discusses the Terafab chip fabrication facility (co-funded with SpaceX) and notes that while an early estimate pegged costs at $20–25 billion (described as a low estimate), a Barclays report puts the figure at $50 billion, with some other estimates going even higher. He acknowledges uncertainty about whether the project will ultimately succeed, noting that Tesla has canceled major initiatives before (including Dojo and battery swap).

3. **Cost Controls Scored Highly:** Brian scores Tesla's cost controls an eight out of ten, citing vertical integration (in-house battery production, in-house lithium refining, in-house seat manufacturing), a lean model lineup strategy, direct-to-consumer sales eliminating dealership markups, and a culture of continuous cost reduction. He draws a historical parallel to Henry Ford's Model T, which ran for nearly 20 years while its price dropped from approximately $2,500 to $5–600.

4. **Engineering Culture Scored Eight Out of Ten:** Brian scores Tesla's engineering culture an eight, drawing on personal conversations with Tesla engineers who described a culture where feedback is not just accepted but demanded. He contrasts this with traditional automakers where minor engineering changes can take months or years, and where siloed hierarchies prevent open communication.

5. **Overall Roadmap Score of Nine Out of Ten:** Brian awards Tesla's roadmap a nine out of ten — the highest category score — citing the Cybercab, Optimus, Megapack, and the Terafab as key pillars. The deduction from a perfect score reflects uncertainty around Terafab costs and unresolved complications in its development. Tesla's composite score across all categories reaches 74, which Brian describes as the highest in the series.
