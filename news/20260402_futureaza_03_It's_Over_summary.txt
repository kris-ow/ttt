## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab Production Confirmed Underway:** The hosts referenced that Cybercab production has already started at the Austin factory, and that hundreds of Cybercabs have already been built, which the hosts interpreted as Tesla having high internal confidence the technology is solved. An overhead drone shot (attributed to Joe Tegtmeyer) showed a Cybercab without a traditional hood/frunk cover, interpreted as a lightweight service hatch rather than a full hood mechanism — consistent with a purpose-built robotaxi design philosophy. The hosts confirmed the vehicle is front-wheel drive per conversations with Tesla engineers.

2. **Cybercab Unit Economics and BOGO Logic:** Cern Basher and Brian discussed the cost structure of the Cybercab in detail. Brian stated his estimate that before reaching Model 3-level volumes, Cybercab cost of goods sold could fall to $17,000–$18,000. At a $25,000 sale price, that yields a positive margin; at $34,000, Tesla effectively gets one unit to keep in its robotaxi fleet for every one it sells — a "BOGO" dynamic that could bring their fleet deployment cash burn close to zero. Cern confirmed no competitor — including Uber with any known vehicle partner — can approach this cost structure.

3. **Tesla vs. Uber: Structural Advantage:** Cern Basher laid out a systematic comparison of Tesla's robotaxi cost advantages over Uber. Key points included: Uber must purchase vehicles from third parties and pay a markup; Uber pays retail electricity rates at non-Tesla chargers (~$0.50/kWh or more) versus Tesla's own charging infrastructure (~$0.17/kWh); Uber has no service centers and must partner for maintenance; Uber's platform fee structure (over $1/mile platform fee plus taking roughly half of driver revenue) is not sustainable in an autonomous world where fleet owners have no need for an aggregator. Cern noted that whoever owns the fleet controls the supply and has no incentive to share revenue with Uber.

4. **Cybercab Two-Seat Design Validated:** The hosts discussed the two-seat design decision, citing data suggesting that 80–90%+ of ride-share trips involve two or fewer passengers. They noted Tesla's fleet would handle larger groups through Model Y vehicles, the upcoming Robo Van, and eventually Cybertruck-based vehicles. Cern also raised the strategic point that selling Cybercabs to individual owners creates a naturally distributed fleet positioned near where demand originates.

5. **Robotaxi City Expansion and FSD Subscription Thesis:** A guest (attending the Chattanooga Charge event) argued that Tesla's future vehicle lineup — Cybercab, Robo Van, autonomous Cybertruck delivery vehicles, Semi — will all require FSD subscriptions, and that this is how Tesla will hit the FSD subscriber targets embedded in Elon Musk's compensation plan. The guest argued Tesla is not "too early" on autonomy given that Cybercab production is already underway, and that a one-year delay in robotaxi rollout only reduces the present value of the stock by approximately 2–3% based on Cern's DCF modeling.
