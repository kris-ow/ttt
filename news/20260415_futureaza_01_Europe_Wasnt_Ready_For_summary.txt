This video covers the landmark approval of Tesla's FSD Supervised in the Netherlands by the Dutch Vehicle Authority (RDW), marking the first authorized use of FSD in Europe. Host Brian of Futureasa interviews Case Roland Champ, a Netherlands-based Tesla driver who claims to be the first non-Tesla employee to receive the European FSD early access update, discussing the software's real-world performance on Dutch roads. The core thesis is that this Dutch approval could serve as a gateway to broader EU-wide FSD authorization, potentially unlocking a major new market for Tesla's autonomous driving technology.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **Dutch RDW Approval Opens European FSD Era:** Tesla's FSD Supervised received landmark approval from the Dutch Vehicle Authority (RDW), enabling its use on public roads in the Netherlands for the first time. Case Roland Champ describes receiving the early access email within 24 hours of the official announcement and claims to be the first non-Tesla employee to receive the update. He had logged nearly 1,000 kilometers in just over two days of testing at the time of the interview.

2. **European FSD Build Differences from US Version:** The European FSD build is version 14.225 but contains several region-specific modifications compared to the US version. The maximum speed cap is set to 130 km/h (the Dutch national maximum), down from the previous 140 km/h cap used in Autopilot and FSD internationally. The system also features contextual speed awareness, meaning it dynamically adjusts speed based on surrounding traffic rather than defaulting below the limit — a behavior Case found frustrating in the US. Additional European-specific features include: the lead vehicle being highlighted in yellow, and a mandatory 3.5-minute instructional video with two follow-up questions that every user must complete before activating FSD.

3. **Handling European Road Complexity — Pedestrians and Cyclists:** Case notes that Amsterdam's road environment is dramatically more chaotic than anything he encountered during 30,000 kilometers of US FSD driving, with dense pedestrian and cyclist traffic, frequent jaywalking, and cyclists riding against traffic. He observes that the European FSD build is somewhat more assertive with vulnerable road users — creeping forward to signal vehicle intent — but expresses the opinion that it still needs to be slightly more assertive in dense city centers, where overly cautious behavior can cause other road users to take advantage of the vehicle's hesitation.

4. **Early User Reactions Overwhelmingly Positive:** Case shared the FSD experience with family members, including his mother and sister, both first-time users. His mother adapted within approximately three minutes and immediately expressed interest in using it for a roughly 2,000-kilometer road trip to Spain. His sister, though initially more apprehensive, also adapted quickly. Case describes the broader reaction from everyone he has shown it to as "overwhelmingly positive" and "blown away."

5. **Pathway to EU-Wide Approval:** Case explains that the RDW ruling can effectively be adopted by other EU member states, which can either accept it directly or apply additional national limitations. Tesla has formally requested EU-wide approval, which requires a majority vote from the relevant economic commission. If that vote passes, Case estimates the earliest timeline for other EU member states to begin receiving FSD updates could be four to six months from the time of the interview. He believes the approval process will accelerate rapidly once the vote clears, describing it as dominoes starting to fall.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **FSD as a European Sales Driver:** Host Brian speculates that the European FSD rollout could drive vehicle sales in the region, noting that FSD is available both as a subscription and as an outright purchase in Europe. The broader implication discussed is that a compelling autonomous driving feature — now legally authorized — could reinvigorate Tesla's demand in a market where the brand has faced headwinds.
