This video features host Herbert (brighterwithherbert) interviewing Cern Basher, a Chartered Financial Analyst and founder of investment advisory firm Brilliant Advice, on why Tesla's current valuation may be fundamentally wrong. Their core argument is that Wall Street analysts are systematically ignoring several of Tesla's emerging business units — particularly Terafab, Digital Optimus, robotaxi, and physical Optimus — treating them as zero-value when each, if structured as a standalone private company, would command valuations in the hundreds of billions to trillions of dollars. The two discuss detailed valuation models for each segment and argue the biggest stock catalyst will come not when revenues materialize, but when the market begins to price in the probability of success.

## Market & Competition

1. **Wall Street's Systematic Undervaluation of Emerging Businesses:** Cern argues that traditional public market analysts focus only on Tesla's auto, energy, and services segments, and at best are beginning to incorporate robotaxi — but even then with extremely conservative projections. The core thesis is that if Tesla's robotaxi, Optimus, Digital Optimus, and Terafab businesses were standalone private companies, they would each command valuations in the billions to hundreds of billions. Because they are embedded within a public company, analysts wave them off as having no revenue, effectively assigning them zero value in their models.

2. **Morgan Stanley's Robotaxi Projections Critiqued as Far Too Conservative:** Morgan Stanley's published estimates show approximately 1,500 robotaxi vehicles in 2026, growing to 3,000 by end of 2027, 7,500 by 2028, 12,500 in 2029, 30,000 in 2030, and not reaching 5 million vehicles until 2040. Herbert and Cern argue these numbers are irreconcilable with Tesla's stated Cybercab production plans — with some estimates suggesting 30,000 Cybercabs produced in 2026 alone, and potentially 100,000 the following year — making Morgan Stanley's robotaxi fleet figures appear to assume almost all produced vehicles go unused or unsold.

3. **Analyst Consensus Delivery Estimates Are Declining, Not Rising:** Tesla recently published a company-compiled analyst consensus showing Q1 2026 delivery expectations of 365,000 vehicles and full-year 2026 deliveries of approximately 1.689 million — a slight increase over 2025's 1.636 million. However, a comparison to Q4 2025 estimates reveals that analyst projections for 2029 "other models" (covering Cybertruck, Roadster, Semi, and Cybercab) have declined by approximately 268,000 vehicles over the past three months, a trend Cern describes as "really puzzling" given visible Cybercab production activity.

4. **AI Agent Market Expected to Consolidate to a Small Number of Winners:** Both speakers argue that the digital AI agent market — currently fragmented — will consolidate to perhaps around 100 major players globally, with large tech companies (Amazon, Google), Chinese government-backed entities, and potentially OpenAI and Anthropic among them. They argue Tesla/SpaceX holds a uniquely advantaged position because no other entity has both a Terafab-equivalent initiative and a rocket company capable of deploying AI compute infrastructure in space.

## Robotaxi

1. **Robotaxi Ramp Timeline and City Rollout for 2026:** Cern's catalyst table for 2026 outlines that Tesla has guided for robotaxi unsupervised service launching in the first half of 2026 across Dallas, Houston, Phoenix, Miami, Orlando, Tampa, and Las Vegas. Evidence of vehicle stockpiling has been observed in Dallas and Houston. The expectation is for a serious unsupervised ramp by Q3 2026, with potential expansion to additional U.S. markets in Q4 2026, though Cern acknowledges this timeline could slip by six months to a year.

2. **Cybercab Production Already Underway:** As of the video's filming (late March 2026), over 100 Cybercab units had already been built ahead of the April production start date. Many of these early units are engineering test vehicles equipped with steering wheels and pedals for regulatory testing in various markets. Cern notes that analysts who physically toured the factory have seen a highly automated Cybercab production line, making the Morgan Stanley fleet estimates even harder to reconcile.

3. **Cybercab Purchase and Network Enrollment Expected as a Major Catalyst:** The milestone table includes an anticipated event — not yet announced at time of filming — allowing consumers to pre-order and purchase Cybercabs for either personal use or enrollment in Tesla's robotaxi network. Cern describes this as a "pretty major event" with significant financial modeling implications.

4. **Waymo Loses Money Per Vehicle; Tesla's Model Is Different:** The speakers briefly contrast Tesla's robotaxi economics with Waymo's, noting that Waymo loses money on every robotaxi it operates, whereas Tesla's model is designed to be profitable — a key structural difference that helps explain why Tesla's ramp could be far more aggressive than Waymo's.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Physical Optimus Factories Under Construction:** Two Optimus manufacturing facilities are in active development: a 1 million unit per year Optimus production line being built in Fremont (enabled by the sunsetting of Model S and Model X production), and a separate 10 million unit per year facility in Austin where land is currently being cleared. Cern frames these construction activities as the real forward-looking indicators of progress, even if no finished Optimus Gen 3 units are yet publicly visible.

2. **Optimus Gen 3 Reveal and Pilot Production Expected in 2026:** The milestone table anticipates an Optimus Gen 3 product reveal in approximately Q2 2026, with pilot production expected in Q3–Q4 2026. Cern attributes the delay from prior timelines to the genuine difficulty of designing the required components at manufacturing scale — Tesla had to create novel parts itself rather than sourcing them externally.

3. **Digital Optimus: A Potentially Larger Business Than Physical Optimus:** Cern introduces "Digital Optimus" as a software-based AI agent system that performs computer work (white-collar tasks) rather than physical labor. He argues this could be Tesla's single largest business opportunity. His rationale: robotaxi addresses roughly 5% of GDP (transportation); physical Optimus addresses roughly 25–30% of GDP (physical labor); Digital Optimus goes after the remainder — the majority of global GDP represented by knowledge work and computer-based tasks. A Digital Optimus product launch is expected by end of 2026.

4. **Digital Optimus Valuation Model — Conservative to Aggressive Cases:** Cern presents a tiered valuation model for Digital Optimus based on monthly subscription pricing and agent deployment. At a $100/month basic tier with 10 million agents by 2031 and 70% profit margins, the present value is approximately $32 billion (described as a floor). At $500/month with the same agent count, the present value rises to approximately $160 billion, growing to roughly $500 billion if a 10% long-term growth rate is assumed. At $5,000/month (full employee-replacement tier), 10 million agents would imply a present value of $1.6 trillion. Cern recommends anchoring conservatively at $30–50 billion present value as a starting floor, with significant upside depending on pricing and scale.

5. **Digital Optimus Differentiated by Vision-Based Approach:** The speakers argue Tesla's Digital Optimus is architecturally distinct from OpenAI and Anthropic's approaches. Rather than using software APIs to interface with specific applications, Tesla's system uses a vision-based method — observing and interpreting whatever is on screen, just as a human would — enabling it to operate across tens of millions of different software environments without requiring bespoke integrations. This approach leverages Tesla's accumulated expertise from FSD's camera-vision architecture.

## Energy

1. **Terafab: A Terawatt-Scale AI Compute and Manufacturing Platform:** Elon Musk has announced "Terafab" (also referred to in the video as "Terra"), a project aimed at increasing global annual AI compute capacity approximately 50x — from the current industry-wide approximately 20 gigawatts to 1 terawatt. The plan involves deploying approximately 80% of these chips in space, requiring a massive solar array to power them. The initiative is being pursued within SpaceX and Tesla rather than as a separate company.

2. **Terafab Valuation: Napkin Math Suggests ~$1 Trillion Present Value:** Cern's back-of-envelope model for Terafab uses TSMC/Samsung economics of approximately $2 billion revenue per gigawatt as a conservative baseline. Scaled to 1 terawatt over approximately 9 years, this implies $2 trillion in annual revenue and, at a 50% profit margin, $1 trillion in annual profit. Applying a 50% probability of success and discounting at a 15% required rate of return with no assumed growth, the present value is approximately $950 billion — roughly $1 trillion. Elon Musk publicly responded to Cern's post stating that growth will continue well past 1 terawatt, implying Cern's model is conservative.

3. **Alternative Terafab Valuations — Moonshots Analysis and Private Market Comparison:** Peter Diamandis and his Moonshots team produced their own Terafab analysis with three cases: a conservative enterprise value of $200–400 billion, a base case of $500 billion to $1 trillion, and a bull case of $2–5 trillion (based on cost savings to Tesla and SpaceX rather than external revenue). In a hypothetical private market context, Cern estimates Terafab would be worth approximately $40 billion at a 45% VC discount rate, or approximately $270 billion at a 25% growth startup discount rate. He notes that Tesla's stock has not reflected any of this value since the announcement.

4. **Terafab Capex and TSMC Comparison:** Building out the Terafab is estimated to require approximately $300 billion in capital expenditure. TSMC currently has a market cap of approximately $1.7 trillion and produces more than half of the world's current approximately 20 gigawatt annual AI compute. Cern argues that if Terafab reaches TSMC-equivalent scale, it could add value equivalent to Tesla's entire current market cap — and the full 1 terawatt objective represents a 100x expansion of TSMC's current output. He suggests TSMC-equivalent scale could be reached within 3–5 years, with the full 1 terawatt target in approximately 9 years.

5. **Cortex 2 Expansion Milestones:** The 2026 catalyst table includes Cortex 2 (Tesla's AI training cluster) reaching 50% capacity in Q2 2026 and full capacity by Q3 2026.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Wall Street EV Delivery Forecasts and Cybercab Absence:** Analyst consensus projects total Tesla deliveries of approximately 1.689 million in 2026, rising to approximately 1.9 million in 2027, 2.1 million in 2028, 2.6 million in 2029, and 3 million in 2030. The "other models" category (Cybertruck, Roadster, Semi, and Cybercab) shows declining growth in outer years — a trend the speakers find inexplicable given visible Cybercab factory activity. Neither Cybercab nor robotaxi fleet volumes appear to be meaningfully incorporated in these numbers.

2. **Model S and Model X Sunset to Make Way for Optimus Production:** Tesla is sunsetting the Model S and Model X product lines to repurpose the Fremont factory floor for a 1 million unit per year Optimus production line. This is framed as a deliberate and strategic reallocation of manufacturing capacity rather than a sign of weakness in those vehicle lines.

3. **Tesla Semi Production Ramp in 2026:** The milestone table includes Tesla Semi moving into production in Q2–Q3 2026. Cern notes that semi-autonomous trucking ("robo trucking") represents a significant long-term opportunity as an extension of the robotaxi thesis to freight.

## Financials

1. **Combined Valuation Model Using Wall Street's Own Numbers:** Cern presents a model that uses analyst consensus delivery figures through 2030 (growing 10% annually thereafter), Morgan Stanley's robotaxi fleet projections, and his own estimates for Semi and energy storage. Ignoring Optimus, Digital Optimus, Terafab, Autobidder, robo trucking, and solar entirely, the model yields an implied Tesla market value of approximately $1 trillion in 2026, growing to approximately $3.5 trillion by 2035, with an implied share price of approximately $775 in 2035.

2. **Present Value Sensitivity Analysis:** Using a Gordon Growth Model-style present value approach, Cern shows that at a 10% discount rate, a 14% long-term earnings growth assumption produces a stock price broadly in line with Tesla's trading price at the time of filming (approximately $360). He notes this is extremely sensitive to growth rate assumptions — dropping to 13.5% growth produces a price around $350, while lower growth assumptions imply stock prices in the $200s or lower. The implication is that current market pricing embeds no value for robotaxi, Optimus, Digital Optimus, or Terafab.

3. **Q1 2026 Production and Delivery Report Due April 2nd:** The video notes that Tesla's Q1 2026 production and delivery report is due April 2nd, with earnings expected in the third week of April 2026.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD Supervised Approval Expanding Internationally:** Supervised FSD approval is imminent in Europe, with China also appearing close. As of approximately March 28, 2026, there are indications that Israel approval is also near, with South Korea expected not far behind, and Australia and New Zealand also being anticipated. Cern notes European customers have been waiting for years with no fundamental technical reason for the delay.

2. **FSD Cumulative Miles Approaching 9–10 Billion:** Tesla is on the verge of crossing 9 billion cumulative FSD miles at the time of filming, with 10 billion expected sometime in Q2 2026. A newer FSD version (referenced as "FSD 14.3") is anticipated in approximately April 2026.

3. **Robotaxi Flywheel Acknowledged But Not Modeled by Wall Street:** Cern references a Morgan Stanley diagram acknowledging the robotaxi data flywheel — unsupervised driving data accelerates FSD improvement, which drives personal FSD adoption, which feeds more robotaxi data — yet notes this qualitative understanding is not reflected in Morgan Stanley's quantitative projections, which remain minimal through the end of the decade.
