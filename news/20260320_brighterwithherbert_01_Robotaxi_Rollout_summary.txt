This panel discussion from "brighterwithherbert" features four Tesla bull analysts — Jeff Lutz, Cern Basher, and Larry Goldberg — examining multiple converging signals that suggest a wide-scale robotaxi rollout is imminent. The core thesis is that FSD v14.3 (with reasoning capability), Cybercab production ramp, fleet staging in new cities, and favorable regulatory momentum are all converging in an April–May 2026 timeframe. The panel also addresses competitor announcements from Nvidia/Uber and Rivian, Tesla's safety PR challenges, and the Terrafab semiconductor project.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD v14.3 with Reasoning Is in Internal Testing:** Elon Musk confirmed via X that FSD v14.3 is currently in internal testing with a wide release expected "in a few weeks." The panel considers this the most significant near-term catalyst, as Elon previously described v14.3 as "the last big piece of the puzzle." The key capability being added is AI reasoning, which would allow the car to read and interpret road signs in real time — something the panel identifies as a critical gap in current FSD performance. Larry Goldberg cautioned that like all major FSD releases, v14.3 will likely iterate through multiple sub-releases (14.3.1, 14.3.2, etc.) over a 3–4 month period before achieving wide capability.

2. **Sign-Reading as a Critical Safety Gap:** Multiple panelists shared real-world examples of FSD failing at unusual or context-dependent signs — including pedestrian crossing caution signs on country roads and conditional turn-restriction signs in Washington D.C. Larry noted that as recently as current versions (v14.x), the car still misses certain non-standard signs. The panel agreed that reasoning-based sign comprehension is foundational to safe autonomous operation and is precisely why Elon calls it the "missing piece." This capability is also essential for international expansion, as signs vary across languages and jurisdictions globally.

3. **Grok as the Car's Operating System:** Larry Goldberg described his expectation that Grok will become the primary operating system interface for Tesla vehicles — enabling voice/text control of windows, doors, navigation, and driving style preferences. He suggested FSD revenue sharing between Tesla and xAI for Grok's role in the car will likely be in the 15–25% royalty range of FSD subscription revenue, which he characterized as fair but not material until robotaxi fleet scale is achieved.

4. **NHTSA and Regulatory Momentum:** The panel discussed Secretary Sean Duffy's AV Safety Forum and his formal announcement that vehicles no longer require steering wheels or pedals. A Tesla robotaxi program manager publicly thanked Secretary Duffy and stated that "innovative American-made AVs like the Cybercab will vastly improve roadway safety and we look forward to working with you on launching them at scale this year." Jeff Lutz noted that this represents a "pull" dynamic — the U.S. government proactively accelerating AV adoption out of competitive concern with China — rather than a "push" from industry lobbying.

5. **Safety Data and Media Narrative Challenges:** Tesla's NHTSA-reported robotaxi safety data for January 15–February 15 showed only 1 incident — a low-speed (under 1 mph) rear-end contact when a vehicle ahead stopped suddenly. The panel contrasted this with Waymo's much larger incident count in the same period, which the media largely ignores. Tesla also released FSD supervised safety data showing the system is approximately 9x safer than unassisted driving. However, a viral Fox News story about a Cybertruck crash claimed FSD was engaged; Elon Musk responded that logs show the driver disengaged Autopilot 4 seconds before the crash. The panel expressed frustration with Tesla's reactive PR approach and noted that the section of the screen showing whether FSD was active was deliberately omitted from the Fox News footage.

## Robotaxi

1. **Fleet Staging in New Cities Confirmed:** Tesla Model Y robotaxi vehicles — identifiable by rear camera washers and Texas manufacturer license plates — have been spotted in fleets in Orlando, Florida; Henderson (Las Vegas), Nevada; and Dallas, Texas. The vehicles were observed simulating pickup/dropoff maneuvers. Additionally, two validation Teslas with testing equipment were spotted in Houston. The panel interpreted this as strong evidence that Tesla is preparing for imminent commercial launches in these cities, consistent with the earnings call roadmap targeting Dallas, Houston, Phoenix, Miami, Orlando, Tampa, and Las Vegas by end of H1 2026.

2. **Cybercab Up Close: Cern Basher's Austin South by Southwest Visit:** Cern Basher attended a Tesla pop-up event in Austin during South by Southwest where a Cybercab, Optimus, and FSD demo vehicles were on display. Key observations: (1) The Cybercab's door jamb contained a self-certification sticker confirming it meets all federal safety standards without a steering wheel or pedals — meaning Tesla chose the conventional certification route rather than seeking an NHTSA exemption for the 2,500-vehicle cap, implying no production limit on how many can be deployed. (2) Seats are designed at wheelchair height for accessibility. (3) The vehicle has a matte finish (one made in February), though Joe Tegtmeyer has indicated production units may be glossier. (4) USB-C ports are located directly under the central screen for easier cleaning. (5) The illuminated "robotaxi" branded sticker on the doors and rear is powered by the vehicle and was described as striking at night. (6) The neutral position of the butterfly doors is likely "up," making them easy to open manually in a power failure. A trailer with a Cybercab and an Optimus seated inside was being towed through the streets of Austin.

3. **Self-Certification Regulatory Implications:** Cern Basher explained that Tesla's decision to self-certify the Cybercab under existing FMVSS standards (rather than apply for an NHTSA exemption) removes any federal cap on how many units can be produced and deployed. The only remaining constraints are state-level autonomous vehicle operating rules. The panel speculated that NHTSA, under the current administration, has likely had informal conversations with Tesla acknowledging that certain legacy requirements (e.g., rearview mirrors) will not be enforced for fully autonomous vehicles. Federal legislation (the Self-Drive Act) would ultimately create a uniform national standard.

4. **Cybercab Production Ramp Projections:** Jeff Lutz projected a very gradual initial ramp: approximately 100 units in April (start-of-production month), scaling to hundreds per month through May, reaching ~1,000 units/month by June — for a total of roughly a few thousand units in Q2 2026. He expects production to accelerate sharply in Q3. Morgan Stanley, after visiting Giga Texas, confirmed start-of-production is on track for April. However, the panel criticized Morgan Stanley's longer-term forecast of only ~30,000 robotaxi fleet vehicles by 2030, with Larry Goldberg suggesting the real figure could be 300,000–500,000 by that timeframe. Morgan Stanley set a price target of $415 for Tesla stock.

5. **Robotaxi Expansion Strategy and Economics:** The panel discussed how Cybercabs will be distributed across new cities versus concentrated in Austin and the Bay Area. Jeff Lutz noted that the profitability of a robotaxi network is maximized during peak demand periods (morning/evening commute), and that the optimal strategy is to spread vehicles across many cities to capture peaks — not flood one city. Larry Goldberg explained that Waymo is constrained by cost structure: adding more vehicles in a single city beyond a certain threshold produces diminishing returns. Tesla, with far lower vehicle costs, can put more Cybercabs in a market than Waymo and still be profitable. The panel also highlighted that Tesla vehicles generate revenue even when parked, via the AI4 chip performing "Digital Optimus" compute work — a unique competitive advantage no other robotaxi operator has.

6. **TAM Expansion Through Pricing:** Jeff Lutz cited a 2022 Uber internal presentation showing that a $1 decrease in per-mile pricing results in a 10x expansion of the total addressable market. The panel agreed that once Cybercab pricing reaches $1–$1.50/mile, the robotaxi market expands beyond urban cores and begins displacing car ownership decisions for a broad consumer base. Tesla controls this TAM expansion lever directly through its pricing strategy. Elon Musk has stated an ambition to have robotaxi presence in 50% of U.S. cities.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **New Model Variants and Production Changes:** Morgan Stanley's note, discussed by Larry Goldberg, confirmed that Tesla is not done launching new car variants. The panel noted that the Model Y Long (YL) 6-seat variant is expected to come to the U.S. market, and that additional Cybertruck variants are being discussed. Larry also raised the possibility of selling Cybercabs directly to private consumers — noting a potentially large market for a two-person urban vehicle — with the implicit expectation that private owners would also contribute their cars to the robotaxi fleet as "shepherds" during peak hours.

## Financials

1. **Terrafab Semiconductor Project Launch:** Elon Musk announced on March 14, 2026 that the Terrafab semiconductor fabrication project would "launch in 7 days," implying a March 21 announcement. The panel was cautious about expectations, with Jeff Lutz noting that complex multi-billion-dollar manufacturing projects rarely have grand opening ceremonies at groundbreaking. He emphasized, however, that the announcement itself immediately gives Tesla leverage in supply chain negotiations — including capacity reservations at TSMC fabs in Taiwan, Texas, and Arizona — even before a single wafer is produced. The panel agreed the strategic signaling value is immediate even if production is years away.

## Market & Competition

1. **Nvidia-Uber Robotaxi Partnership:** Nvidia and Uber announced a partnership to deploy robotaxis in Los Angeles and San Francisco in H1 2027, expanding to 28 cities across four continents by 2028. The vehicles will use Nvidia's Drive Hyperion hardware and AV foundation models. Jeff Lutz noted that Nvidia has been making similar AV partnership announcements at GTC for over 11 years, and cautioned that deployment timelines given 2+ years out typically lack specificity. He does not believe Rivian is using Nvidia hardware — Rivian reportedly uses custom silicon.

2. **Uber-Rivian R2 Robotaxi Partnership:** Uber announced a partnership with Rivian to deploy up to 50,000 R2 robotaxis across 25 cities in the U.S., Canada, and Europe by end of 2031, with Uber investing up to $1.25 billion in Rivian. The panel was dismissive of the competitive threat, citing: (1) Rivian will be busy ramping R2 consumer production for 12–18 months and likely cannot simultaneously build out a robotaxi network; (2) Rivian has not demonstrated AV software anywhere near Tesla's capability; (3) the R2's estimated price (~$48,000–$58,000) is roughly double the Cybercab's target cost, making unit economics fundamentally uncompetitive; (4) neither Rivian nor Uber has the charging, service, or fleet management infrastructure Tesla already owns globally. Larry Goldberg compared the Nvidia/Uber/Rivian announcements to legacy automakers' (GM, Toyota, BMW) EV announcements from the early 2020s that failed to materialize at scale.

3. **Waymo's Structural Disadvantage:** The panel revisited Waymo's cost-structure problem at length. New Street Research analysis cited by the panel shows Waymo maximizes ROI by deploying fewer cars across more cities — not more cars in fewer cities — because their cost per vehicle is so high that incremental revenue per additional vehicle in a saturated city turns negative quickly. Tesla's lower cost structure inverts this dynamic, allowing it to profitably operate larger fleets per market. The panel credited Waymo with having "opened the door" in multiple cities regulatorily, which now benefits Tesla's expansion.
