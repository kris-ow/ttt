The video pushes back against recurring media narratives that Tesla sales are collapsing globally, arguing that cherry-picked regional data and a failure to understand Tesla's shipping cycle patterns consistently mislead observers. Host Brian and guest Randy Kirk analyze January–February 2026 year-over-year sales data compiled by analyst Roland Percher, showing a net positive picture across European markets. The discussion extends to FSD pricing strategy, insurance bundling, and the long-term trajectory of autonomous driving adoption.

Key Points:

1. Year-over-year European sales data for January–February 2026 shows a net gain of approximately 7,500 units across the countries Roland Percher tracks. The hosts note that the green (positive) entries on the chart visually outweigh the red, and that this is a year-over-year comparison against a period in early 2025 when the Berlin factory was being retooled, which suppressed baseline numbers and makes the gain even more meaningful.

2. Several major European markets posted strong percentage gains: Germany up 32%, Spain up 73%, France up 24%, Portugal up 60%, Finland up 211%, Italy up 20%, and Romania up 134% (though to only 243 total units). These gains are notable because political headwinds related to Elon Musk's public profile were expected to suppress European demand, yet France and Germany — two markets most exposed to those sentiments — still showed growth.

3. Some markets declined: Denmark was down 16%, Ireland down 44%, and the UK down 41%. The hosts suggest Denmark's dip is likely a shipping timing artifact that will correct in March when a vessel arrives. The UK decline is flagged as unusual and potentially related to tariff or policy changes, with a note to watch whether it persists through the rest of Q1.

4. Norway's March data (available only about one week into the month at the time of recording) showed Tesla roughly doubling its year-to-date sales total in just those early March days, once a ship arrived. This is presented as a textbook example of Tesla's lumpy, ship-arrival-driven sales pattern, which causes apparent "collapses" and "surges" that are artifacts of logistics, not demand signals.

5. Asia-Pacific markets are performing strongly and expanding. South Korea is cited as selling 6,000–7,000 units in a strong period, Japan is described as coming on strong with FSD approval potentially imminent, and Australia continues to perform well. Newer markets including India (recently opened), Chile, and various other export destinations are adding incremental volume that, while small individually, accumulates meaningfully.

6. FSD regulatory approvals are advancing internationally. Norway is working on FSD approval, Tokyo road testing is underway, and the UAE is conducting FSD testing. The hosts argue that dismissing FSD's impact on future sales figures is analytically indefensible, since the feature is not yet available in most of these markets and its introduction will be a new demand catalyst.

7. FSD pricing is a significant barrier to adoption and needs restructuring. The US subscription is $99/month (approximately $108 with state taxes). FSD take rates reach roughly 50% when offered free but drop to 10–20% when priced, implying 30–40% of owners would use FSD if cost were not an obstacle. Both hosts agree a tiered pricing model is needed: a low-cost supervised tier for casual personal users, a mid-tier for unsupervised personal use, and a commercial/robotaxi tier at premium pricing.

8. Insurance bundling could unlock mass FSD adoption. Lemonade Insurance has announced a 50% discount on premiums when FSD is in use. The hosts speculate Tesla could bundle FSD with Lemonade or its own insurance product, potentially making the combined FSD-plus-insurance monthly cost lower than what drivers currently pay for insurance alone — removing the subscription pricing objection entirely. The caveat is that California bans telematics-based insurance pricing, which would exclude that market.

9. Chinese competitors are offering autonomous driving features for free, which creates a competitive threat in markets outside the US. Chinese automakers have adopted Tesla's over-the-air update model and are closing the technology gap. If Tesla prices FSD as a premium subscription in emerging and international markets, buyers will choose a free alternative that is slightly inferior rather than pay, and Tesla risks ceding those markets.

10. Randy makes a longer-term prediction that human driving will eventually be prohibited on public roads, similar to how seatbelt mandates and indoor smoking bans faced fierce initial resistance but became standard. Brian agrees, drawing the analogy to racetracks and off-road venues where recreational activities that are too dangerous for public spaces get contained.

Conclusions and recommendations: The hosts urge viewers not to read single-market snapshots as global trend indicators and to understand Tesla's quarterly delivery patterns as shipping-wave-driven rather than demand-driven. They recommend following Roland Percher on X for country-by-country data. On FSD, they advocate for a supervised/free or very low-cost entry tier to drive adoption and road safety, with commercial usage priced separately.
