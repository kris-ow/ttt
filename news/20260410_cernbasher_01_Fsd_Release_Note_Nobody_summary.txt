This episode of the cernbasher channel features a deep-dive conversation between the host and Phil on the significance of the FSD 14.3 release notes, particularly two technical changes that the broader community has largely overlooked: a major shift toward reinforcement learning and a ground-up rewrite of the AI compiler and runtime using MLIR. The discussion expands into broader themes including the convergence of Tesla and SpaceX into a distributed compute-and-intelligence fabric, the trajectory of Optimus development, and a bold prediction that unsupervised robotaxi operations in Austin are imminent — possibly by end of April 2026.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD 14.3's two underappreciated breakthroughs:** Phil identifies two major technical changes in FSD 14.3 that he considers more significant than the widely discussed 10x parameter count claim. First, Tesla has dramatically deepened its use of reinforcement learning (RL) to address edge cases surfaced during supervised robotaxi testing in Austin and the Bay Area. Rather than fixing individual intersection problems, RL allows Tesla to categorically cure classes of suboptimal driving behavior by defining ideal trajectories, scoring alternatives from poor to near-ideal, and reinforcing the desired behavior during training on AI4 hardware in the Cortex data center in Texas. Second, Tesla rewrote its AI compiler and runtime from the ground up using MLIR (Multi-Level Intermediate Representation), an open-standard framework that abstracts model operations from hardware while enabling aggressive optimization of both compute and memory efficiency. The release notes explicitly state this resulted in a 20% faster reaction time and improved model iteration speed.

2. **Why the 10x parameter claim may be overstated or misunderstood:** Phil argues that the widely anticipated 10x increase in parameter count from the previous version may not have materialized as described, or may be irrelevant to what 14.3 actually delivers. He explains that a 10x parameter model run on AI4 hardware would likely drop inference throughput from the required 36 cycles per second to something closer to 12, making it non-viable for real-time vehicle control. He speculates that the team may have prototyped such a model, confirmed it couldn't run efficiently on HW4, and pivoted to RL as the more effective mechanism for eliminating the long tail of edge cases. He notes Elon's public statements often reflect aspirational roadmap items that shift during engineering review cycles and should not be taken as hard commitments to any specific version.

3. **Hardware tier implications for unsupervised FSD:** Phil draws a clear line between hardware generations and their unsupervised FSD potential. He states with high confidence that Hardware 3 vehicles will likely achieve a reasonable level of supervised FSD but will never support unsupervised operation. Hardware 4 is expected to support unsupervised robotaxi operation with high confidence, and unsupervised customer-owned vehicle operation with slightly less certainty. AI5 hardware is seen as providing substantial headroom — estimated between 2x and 10x — to run significantly larger models and is expected to enable robust unsupervised FSD across all use cases.

4. **FSD unsupervised and robotaxi are essentially the same neural network problem:** Phil clarifies that at the neural network layer, unsupervised customer FSD and unsupervised robotaxi are approximately 90% the same problem. The distinction is mostly product-level rather than model-level. He estimates that roughly 90% of the current engineering focus is on robotaxi deployment, with the remaining 10% on consumer unsupervised FSD, even though advancing one effectively advances the other.

## Robotaxi

1. **Unsupervised robotaxi in Austin predicted as imminent:** The host made a public prediction that unsupervised robotaxi operations would launch in Austin by end of April 2026. While acknowledging the prediction is intentionally provocative and not made with 100% confidence, he cites multiple converging signals: FSD 14.3 appears to be the version that has been running in Austin in build-candidate form for approximately two to three months; the release reflects months of real-world RL refinement from supervised robotaxi observer feedback; and Cybercabs are now being spotted and tested across multiple cities including San Antonio, Dallas, Phoenix, and Miami, which he interprets as calibration and pre-deployment preparation ahead of dropping the safety observer in Austin.

2. **Cybercab sightings and fleet calibration underway:** Joe Tegtmeyer spotted and counted approximately 60 Cybercabs in various locations, some appearing in groupings. Phil explains these are engineering test vehicles that retain steering wheels and pedals. Some have been observed with solid-state LiDAR units mounted on top, which Phil believes are being used for calibration purposes — specifically to map the slightly different camera placements on the Cybercab body relative to Model Y, Model 3, and other vehicles. Tesla's neural network includes an abstraction layer for camera geometry, but physical calibration of production-intent vehicles across multiple cities is a prerequisite to scaling the fleet.

3. **Supervised vs. unsupervised observer positioning by city:** Phil notes a regulatory distinction between Austin and the Bay Area. In Austin, safety observers sit in the passenger seat, while Bay Area regulations require them to be in the driver's seat. The spread of Cybercab testing to multiple new cities is interpreted as a signal that Tesla is close to pulling the safety observer from the Austin fleet entirely and committing to fully unsupervised operation there.

4. **Waymo safety and experience comparison:** Phil briefly acknowledges that Waymo has a reasonably good safety record but argues its rider experience has been poor — citing incidents of vehicles blocking roads and passengers being temporarily unable to exit. He argues Tesla is deliberately moving slowly on removing safety oversight because it takes both safety and ride experience seriously, and that this caution is appropriate even if frustrating to observers who want faster deployment.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus V3 teased via shrouded design image:** The host references what appears to be a preview or conceptual image of Optimus V3, described as a shrouded or fabric-covered humanoid form. Phil interprets Elon's approach as wanting to present the closest achievable humanoid form factor for V3, with the fabric covering serving a functional purpose of protecting exposed actuator joints from debris and mechanical interference. He draws a parallel to Apple's product reveal strategy — noting that Apple did not preview the iPhone before launch — and suggests Elon's early reveals are strategic for talent recruitment, investor awareness, and competitive signaling rather than purely technical readiness.

2. **Manufacturing scale as Optimus's defining constraint:** Phil argues the hardest part of the Optimus program is not the AI stack or actuator capability but manufacturability at scale. He references Tesla's stated goal of building a factory capable of producing 10 million Optimus units annually as a starting point, describing this as an extraordinarily capital-intensive and logistically complex undertaking. He contends Elon is willing to let competitors make mistakes and learn from them, because his confidence lies in Tesla's vertical integration advantage and manufacturing know-how — the same thesis he applies to Cybercab.

3. **Power and compute constraints are Optimus's central engineering challenge:** Phil estimates Optimus carries approximately 2 to 2.5 kilowatt-hours of battery capacity, while its AI chip (currently AI5, eventually AI6) can consume up to 250 watts. Combined with actuator power demands for physical movement, the power budget creates a severe constraint. He predicts the final months of the Optimus engineering program will be dominated by engineers optimizing the compute-to-battery trade-off, potentially offloading intelligence tasks to external compute nodes via network rather than running them on-device.

4. **Optimus deployment path — factory first, consumer later:** Both hosts agree that the most economically rational deployment path begins in warehouse and manufacturing environments, where even a robot that can only move boxes provides high economic value across multiple shifts, before transitioning to service industry roles and eventually consumer home use. However, Phil notes that if AGI is the objective, training across diverse home and public environments will be necessary, not just structured factory settings. The AI stack for Optimus is described as a superset of the FSD vision stack, with the addition of a language/reasoning layer and physical skill decomposition — the ability to break complex tasks like making an omelette into constituent sub-skills.

5. **Optimus on the moon and Mars:** Phil discusses Elon's post suggesting off-Earth deployment of Optimus will proceed faster and at greater scale than on Earth. He frames manufacturing on the moon or Mars using Optimus as a potential path to vastly cheaper products through lower-cost robotic labor in resource-rich environments. He acknowledges significant engineering challenges remain — including temperature variance, lunar dust, and gravity differences requiring design adjustments — but argues Optimus-class robots will be the dominant life form in those environments once present.

## Market & Competition

1. **XAI Colossus 2 scaling and frontier model competition:** Phil discusses Elon's post that XAI's Colossus 2 facility now has seven models in simultaneous training, including Grok 4.2 at half a trillion parameters and two variants of a one-trillion parameter model, with a six-trillion parameter model also referenced. Phil explains this reflects Elon's strategy of parallelizing model development to accelerate learning — running multiple architectural variants simultaneously, identifying the strongest performer, and discarding the rest. He frames this as Elon attempting to leapfrog Anthropic and OpenAI on frontier model capability, while acknowledging Google remains a more difficult target in the West.

2. **Vision AI and the Optimus connection to XAI's imaging work:** Phil connects Elon's continued investment in image generation (referred to as "Imagine") to Optimus development, arguing that vision input and output capabilities are prerequisites for AGI and directly feed the Optimus visual stack. He dismisses the interpretation that image generation is frivolous consumer entertainment, framing it instead as foundational research for embodied AI.

3. **Distributed intelligence routing as an emerging infrastructure challenge:** The host proposes a concept he calls an "intelligence routing layer" — analogous to how the internet routes data or power grids route electricity — that would optimize in real time which compute nodes handle which AI tasks, based on cost, availability, latency, and capability specialization. Phil discusses this as an open problem, noting that today's agentic AI frameworks like Grok 4.2 route sub-tasks internally within a single vendor's infrastructure but do not yet route across competing providers. He draws an analogy to TCP/IP as a potential standard that could emerge, and speculatively connects Tesla's Autobidder (used for Megapack energy arbitrage) as a possible architectural precursor to such a routing system.
