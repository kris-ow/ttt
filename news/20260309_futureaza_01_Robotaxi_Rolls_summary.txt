This weekly Monday morning roundup from the Futurazza channel covers roughly a dozen stories spanning space technology, EV sales, battery developments, autonomous driving, and Tesla news. Host Brian maintains an optimistic but grounded tone throughout, pushing back against what he sees as dishonest competitive tactics in the space industry and premature doom narratives around Tesla's market position. The overarching thesis is that rapid technological progress makes today's limitations largely irrelevant when projecting near-future capabilities.

Key Points:

1. SpaceX unveils cellular Starlink targeting 150 Mbps per user via 5G from space. Brian contextualizes why this matters despite T-Mobile offering 309 Mbps: T-Mobile's soft data cap of 50 gigabytes would be exhausted in just 21 minutes and 34 seconds at full 5G speed, making raw speed less important than coverage and cap structure. A follow-up episode with Quincy, a former SpaceX engineer now at Electric Era charging company in Seattle, is planned to explain the technology in depth.

2. Amazon urges the FCC to deny SpaceX's plan to launch one million satellites, claiming the project would take over 220 years to deploy. Brian calls this "breathtaking dishonesty," pointing out the math is based on the current global pace of 4,500 satellite launches per year — a figure he argues is completely obsolete once Starship is operational. His counter-calculation: Starship can carry 50 to 400 satellites per mission; at a conservative 100 satellites per launch, two launch sites firing once daily produces 700 launches per year, or 70,000 satellites annually. At 300 satellites per launch, the entire one-million satellite constellation could be deployed in roughly 5 years.

3. Tesla stock is at $389.81 at time of recording, down 1% on the week and 6% on the month, but up 75% over the full year. Brian explicitly frames this as not a reason to panic or sell, noting that elevated oil prices, while economically negative broadly, tend to push consumers toward electric vehicles.

4. Tesla's European registrations rose 10% in February, and Tesla surpassed the entire Volkswagen Group (which includes Volkswagen, Skoda, Audi, Seat, Cupra, and Porsche) in quarterly sales in Norway. Brian explains the recurring "delivery wave" pattern: Tesla batches cars onto ships at quarter's end, so early-quarter data always looks weak before a large surge arrives. He stresses this is a predictable, structural phenomenon, not a sign of weakness.

5. Global sales data for January and February shows Tesla up 7,500 units year-over-year. Standout growth markets include South Korea up 342%, Japan up 200%, Australia up 62%, Germany up 32%, Spain up 73%, and France up 24%. Declining markets such as UK (down 41%) and Taiwan (down 99%) are attributed to ships not yet having arrived with inventory rather than demand collapse.

6. Xpeng has introduced its second-generation VALA 2.0 autonomous driving system and plans to launch fully driverless robotaxis in Guangzhou later in 2026. Brian, who personally drove a car running VALA 2.0, rates it roughly equivalent to Tesla FSD version 13, noting Xpeng's training data benefits from extremely chaotic Chinese road conditions — including driving the wrong way against 30 scooters — which essentially force edge-case learning at scale.

7. FSD supervised road trials have expanded globally, with active testing now in Abu Dhabi, Tokyo (ahead of a 2026 launch), and Norway, which has approved public road testing. Brian uses this multi-country expansion to argue that autonomous driving is an unstoppable trend regardless of opinions about Tesla or Elon Musk specifically.

8. CATL leads global EV battery market share at well over one-third, with BYD in a distant second at 13.8%. CATL is now deploying sodium-ion batteries in a mass-produced passenger vehicle for the first time, with 248 miles of range. Brian clarifies a common misconception: because manufacturing costs dominate battery pricing — not raw materials — sodium-ion batteries will diversify the supply chain but not dramatically cut costs. He also notes a volumetric energy density drawback: a sodium-ion battery may weigh the same as a lithium pack but occupy nearly twice the physical space.

9. BYD's Blade 2.0 battery can charge from 10% to 80% in approximately 5 minutes and is rated to last 600,000 miles. It survived being pierced with a spike during high-rate charging without fire or smoke. Brian notes these cells could theoretically accept charge from a Tesla Semi charger, underscoring the scale of power involved.

10. Tesla is preparing its largest Supercharger station yet in Yermo, California, with 400 V4 stalls across multiple phases. Brian connects this to grid-scale battery storage, noting that China deployed more stationary energy storage in December 2025 alone than the United States deployed for the entire year, making megawatt-scale charging more feasible there currently.

11. A skeptical take on Doughnut Labs and solid-state battery claims. Brian says their behavior — vague promises, red flags, a "available today" website claim since January now pushed to late April — is consistent with a product that does not yet exist. He acknowledges the odds are "not zero" but stands by his skepticism.

12. Tesla is quietly building the most vertically integrated EV battery supply chain in the Western world, handling lithium refinement, anode and sometimes cathode production, and running three separate cell manufacturing facilities: Austin, Kato Road in Fremont, and a new LFP plant in Nevada. Giga Nevada cells are made by Panasonic and are counted separately.

13. The new BYD Seal (refreshed model) is priced at approximately $24,600 in China versus the Model 3 at $34,100, and BYD includes its autonomous driving tech at no extra cost — a significant competitive advantage in markets where Tesla charges for FSD.
