Tesla and LG Energy Solution have officially confirmed a partnership to produce LFP (lithium iron phosphate) batteries at a $4.3 billion facility in Lansing, Michigan — originally built as a GM-LG Ultium joint venture that GM subsequently abandoned. The batteries will power Tesla's Megapack 3 (also called Mega Block) stationary energy storage systems. Hosts Brian and Herbert from the Brighter channel break down why this is a win for Tesla, LG, the US government, and the state of Michigan, and then pivot to discussing BYD's fast-charging battery announcement and its implications.

Key Points

1. The Lansing plant was originally a GM-LG partnership to produce high-nickel pouch Ultium cells, with GM and LG investing $2.6 billion toward it as a third Ultium manufacturing facility. GM backed out after the $7,500 EV tax credit situation shifted, tariffs on manufacturing in Mexico and Canada took effect, and their broader EV business model fell apart. The facility was largely built but without a customer.

2. Tesla stepped in as a mystery buyer, and the partnership was kept secret for approximately eight months. The identity was ultimately revealed by the US Department of Interior, likely because Tesla would be applying for federal battery manufacturing tax credits — which require government disclosure.

3. The financial incentive structure is substantial. The federal manufacturing credit is $35 per kilowatt hour on cells, plus an additional $10 per kilowatt hour if the company integrates cells into packs — which Tesla would do. At 50 gigawatt hours of projected annual capacity, that works out to roughly $1.5–1.7 billion per year in cell credits and an additional ~$500 million per year for pack assembly. Even if LG captures the cell credits and passes savings along, Tesla would still benefit from the pack integration credit on the energy storage side.

4. Production is expected to begin in 2027, with the facility projected to operate at 50 gigawatt hours of annual capacity. This is comparable to or larger than Tesla's Giga Nevada, which the hosts estimate is closer to 50 gigawatt hours in real output despite the commonly cited 35 gigawatt hour figure. Tesla also has in-house LFP production using CATL equipment in Sparks, Nevada, running at roughly 10 gigawatt hours, but that is a much smaller facility.

5. The deal serves clear US national security and energy policy interests. Batteries are considered a critical infrastructure resource — powering EVs, stationary grid storage, and AI data centers. Reducing dependence on Chinese battery suppliers is a stated government priority, and the tariff environment has already curtailed Chinese battery cell imports. Keeping LG's US facility operational directly supports that goal.

6. Michigan specifically benefits from the jobs, production capacity, and revenue, as much of the state's traditional auto manufacturing segment is struggling or contracting.

7. The hosts briefly discuss BYD's megawatt-speed fast-charging battery announcement. BYD has deployed a vehicle with this technology currently selling at approximately $23,300, and plans to expand its fast-charging station network to 400–500 locations by end of 2026. The hosts acknowledge this technology is real and deployed, unlike most "miracle battery" announcements that never leave the lab.

8. Tesla's current strategic position is that 300 miles of range is sufficient for the majority of consumers, especially those with home charging. The argument is that ultra-fast charging adds cost and weight to the vehicle, and the charger infrastructure to support megawatt charging at scale doesn't yet exist broadly.

9. The longer-term framing the hosts offer is that autonomous driving and robotaxi economics fundamentally change the charging speed calculus. If a car drives itself and can charge while the owner sleeps — or if the car earns money as a robotaxi — then charging speed matters far less than FSD capability. The hosts suggest that if fast charging genuinely moves consumer demand and can be delivered at the same price point, Tesla will adopt it; the question is whether it actually drives purchase decisions.

Conclusions and Recommendations

The hosts characterize this deal as a clear win across all parties involved — Tesla secures domestic LFP supply it badly needs for Megapack 3, LG saves a stranded facility investment, Michigan retains manufacturing jobs, and the US government advances its domestic battery supply chain goals. The hosts are not alarmed by BYD's fast-charging announcement in the near term, given the charging infrastructure gap and Tesla's autonomous driving advantage, but acknowledge it is worth monitoring to see if it materially affects sales.
