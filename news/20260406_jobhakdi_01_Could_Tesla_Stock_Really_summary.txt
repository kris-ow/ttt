In this video, Jo Bhakdi responds to a JP Morgan analyst's bearish Tesla price target and a notable single-day stock drop, arguing that the analyst's concerns are largely misplaced but that macro geopolitical risk — specifically the Iran conflict — is a legitimate reason to pause on adding to Tesla positions. He walks through the bull case for Tesla's robotaxi and Optimus businesses while explaining why he is tactically not doubling down despite being long-term bullish.

## Market & Competition

1. **JP Morgan Analyst Ryan Brinkman's Bearish Call:** JP Morgan analyst Ryan Brinkman issued a price target of $145 on Tesla, which Jo Bhakdi attributes as the proximate cause of Tesla's single-day decline of 2.7% even as the NASDAQ was up 0.5% and the S&P up 0.37%. Bhakdi dismisses Brinkman's credibility, noting he ranks number 10,493 out of 12,000 Wall Street analysts, has an average return of negative 1.60%, and a success rate of 50%. Bhakdi highlights Brinkman's track record of badly missed calls: a $90 price target in December 2020 when the stock was at $640 (it subsequently went to $1,200 pre-split), and a $130 price target in October 2024 when the stock was at $240 (it subsequently rallied to $460 within two months and $480 by 2025).

2. **Brinkman's Core Bear Arguments — and Bhakdi's Rebuttals:** Brinkman's thesis rests on Tesla being primarily an auto business trading at a 335x earnings multiple, with 50,000 units of unsold inventory sitting at the end of last quarter (produced but not delivered), and deteriorating free cash flow. He also argues the bull case for Cybercab and Optimus is "free upside" embedded in the price, that Cybercab needs regulatory approval in every market, and that if either robotaxi or Optimus slips 12 months, the entire valuation justification evaporates. Bhakdi counters that regulatory approval is not a meaningful near-term barrier — pointing to Waymo scaling without running into regulatory issues — and that the real question is simply whether FSD works well enough to deploy. He also dismisses the 12-month slip argument as irrelevant to long-term investors.

3. **Macro Risk: Iran Conflict as the Real Market Threat:** Bhakdi argues the real reason Tesla and the broader market are under pressure is the ongoing conflict involving Iran, not JP Morgan. He notes the NASDAQ is down 5.44% year-to-date, and warns there could be an additional 10–20% NASDAQ downside if the situation escalates — meaning Tesla could drop a further 10–20%. He argues the market has essentially priced in a 100% probability that the war is over, which he believes is a mispricing given that Iran has not agreed to a ceasefire and Trump faces difficult choices. He specifically calls out that 12–15% of global trade passes through the Red Sea/Gulf of Aden, that Houthi activity threatens that corridor, and that the Strait of Hormuz controls global oil flows. He also notes India is now bypassing US sanctions and buying oil directly from Iran — a sign that US leverage is weakening, not strengthening.

4. **Why Bhakdi Is Not Doubling Down — But Remains Long-Term Bullish:** Bhakdi explicitly states he is not adding to his Tesla position until the Iran situation is resolved, not because of anything fundamental to Tesla itself. He calls the current V-shaped NASDAQ recovery "potentially the fakest V-shaped recovery I've ever seen" and says the downside risk is not priced in. However, he stresses he is maintaining his core long-term Tesla position and would "absolutely double down" once the macro risk is off the table.

## Robotaxi

1. **Robotaxi Scale Needed to Match Nvidia's Profitability:** Bhakdi shares a calculation posted in his Pioneer Lands community: to match Nvidia's quarterly profit, Tesla would need approximately 1.7 million robotaxis deployed. He extends this to say that at 6 million robotaxis, Tesla would surpass Nvidia's entire free cash flow. He argues that at equivalent EBITDA to Nvidia, Tesla's valuation would be roughly triple Nvidia's — around $15 trillion — because Tesla's robotaxi TAM is more universally understood as multi-trillion dollar and its growth rate would be higher.

2. **Robotaxi Launch Timeline and Scale Expectations:** Bhakdi reaffirms his view that commercial robotaxi operations will launch this year and begin scaling in earnest in 2026. He distinguishes between "multiple thousands" (modest, less impactful on the stock) and "multiple tens of thousands" (which he believes would drive the stock above $1,000). He frames the deployment of multiple thousands as the point at which the scaling game becomes obvious to markets.

3. **Regulatory Risk Is Not the Bottleneck:** Bhakdi pushes back firmly on Brinkman's regulatory risk argument, stating that in many US states there is no meaningful regulatory barrier to rolling out tens of thousands of autonomous vehicles. He points to Waymo's ongoing scaling as evidence that regulatory approval is not an obstacle. The only real bottleneck, in his view, is whether FSD technology actually works.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus Gen 3 Delay — Bhakdi Is Relaxed:** Bhakdi acknowledges that Optimus Gen 3 has no public reveal date and is delayed, consistent with Brinkman's point. However, he says Optimus revenue streams are expected to kick in next year (2027), not this year, and he is unconcerned about the delay. He argues Tesla is right not to reveal Optimus prematurely given intense competition in the humanoid space, particularly from China.

2. **Optimus Deployment Trajectory:** Bhakdi outlines an expected trajectory where Tesla deploys a few thousand to perhaps 10,000 Optimus units in early stages for testing, then scales to hundreds of thousands, and reaches millions in the 2030s. He describes Optimus as "the second S-curve after robotaxi." Importantly, he states Tesla does not need Optimus to get the stock to $2,000 or $3,000 — robotaxi alone could drive that valuation.

## Autonomous Driving

1. **FSD as the Core Technology Differentiator:** Bhakdi describes Tesla as "a technology leader completely uncontested right now in Full Self-Driving technology," framing it as what drives both car sales and the robotaxi deployment opportunity. The central investment question he identifies is whether FSD is good enough to enable commercial robotaxi scaling this year.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Auto Business Headwinds Are Real But Secondary:** Bhakdi acknowledges the global auto industry is struggling with overcapacity and Chinese competition, and that Tesla produced 50,000 more cars than it delivered last quarter. He notes Tesla remains the most profitable automaker in the world by margin and is reclaiming the lead from BYD, but concedes the core auto business is not the investment thesis.

## Financials

1. **335x Earnings Multiple Reflects Future Optionality:** Bhakdi acknowledges Tesla trades at a 335x earnings multiple, which looks extreme when viewed purely as an auto company. He frames this multiple as justified only by the expected future revenue streams from robotaxi and Optimus, and argues the correct investment framework is to value those future streams — not the current auto earnings.
