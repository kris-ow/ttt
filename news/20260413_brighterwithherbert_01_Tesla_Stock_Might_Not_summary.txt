This video features host Herbert interviewing Larry Goldberg, a serial entrepreneur and venture capitalist, in a wide-ranging discussion about whether Tesla stock is undervalued, overvalued, or fairly priced at its current mid-$300s level. The central thesis is that Tesla's valuation depends entirely on which version of the company you're evaluating — the struggling car business suggests overvaluation, while the broader portfolio of robotaxi, Optimus, AI, and energy suggests significant undervaluation relative to future potential. Both the bull and bear cases are presented as simultaneously valid depending on the investor's time horizon.

## Electric Vehicles
1. **Auto Business Decline Driving Bear Case:** Tesla's car business is the primary basis for the bear argument. Earnings dropped 47% in the prior year, deliveries are flat, and the auto segment revenue is cited as having moved from negative $9 billion to $18 billion over a multi-year period. Bears argue that judged purely as an automaker, Tesla stock at mid-$300s is significantly overvalued, with some suggesting a fair value closer to $50–$100 for a pure auto company. Elon Musk's earlier projection of 20 million vehicle sales per year was cited as another missed target.

2. **FSD Adoption Problem Among Existing Owners:** Larry Goldberg highlights what he calls a genuine failure — Tesla's inability to get its existing customer base to actually use Full Self-Driving. He notes that while he personally uses FSD approximately 98% of the time and his wife uses it 94–95% of the time, he observes fellow Tesla owners in the Research Triangle Park area gripping the steering wheel and driving manually, even when they own FSD. He sees this as a meaningful missed opportunity, though both he and the host believe adoption will accelerate as the technology becomes more demonstrably reliable.

3. **Technology Inflection Point for Consumer Adoption:** The host shares a personal anecdote about his sister — previously a Mercedes and BMW loyalist who had no interest in Tesla — deciding to purchase a Tesla after being driven around Vancouver, Canada in Full Self-Driving mode for hours without incident. The host frames this as evidence that FSD has crossed a threshold from beta testing to genuinely useful technology, which he believes will drive broader consumer adoption. He notes a newer FSD version is expected to push capabilities further.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **FSD Going Global and Version Expectations:** The host references FSD going global as one of the key developments being tracked. A newer FSD version (referred to as FSD 14.3) is described as highly anticipated, with some interpreting delays in its arrival as meaning a promised 10x parameter improvement did not materialize on schedule. The host and guest remain confident the technology trajectory is intact, even if specific release timing is uncertain.

2. **Unsupervised FSD as a Solved Problem in Principle:** The host states his personal view that robotaxi and FSD are "solved" from a technology standpoint, framing remaining challenges as timing and scale rather than fundamental feasibility. He acknowledges unsupervised deployment will take more time to fully roll out across cities, but argues the technology is no longer in question, pointing to ongoing hiring activity as evidence of continued expansion plans.

## Robotaxi
1. **Austin Robotaxi Launch Referenced:** The host mentions robotaxi deployment in Austin as an active near-term development, citing it alongside FSD going global and Cybercab production starting as key catalysts. No specific launch date or fleet size is provided beyond the general reference to Austin being the initial deployment city.

2. **Cybercab Production Starting:** The host states that Cybercab production is starting "this month" (relative to the video's April 2026 publish date), framing it as one of the key bullish near-term catalysts alongside the Austin robotaxi launch. No specific production volume or ramp rate is mentioned in this context.

3. **Missed Robotaxi Timeline History:** The host catalogs a series of missed robotaxi timelines from Elon Musk: a claim in 2020 of 1 million robotaxis "next year," a statement that 50% of the US would have robotaxi service by a certain date that passed without fulfillment, and a more recent projection of robotaxi expanding to many cities by year-end 2025 that was pushed to mid-2026. These are presented not as disqualifying but as consistent with Elon's pattern of aspirational target-setting.

4. **Ark Invest $2,900 Price Target for 2029–2030:** The host references Ark Invest's published price target of $2,900 per share by 2029–2030. A separate analyst referred to as "Cern" is cited as having conducted a sum-of-the-parts valuation, discounting future segment values back to present value with a 50% probability haircut applied conservatively, and arriving at a present value still above $3,000 per share — suggesting even the discounted bull case implies the current ~$350 price has not priced in most of the upside.

## Humanoid Bots
1. **Optimus Production Line Announced at Fremont:** The host states that Tesla has announced a 1 million units per year Optimus production line being built at Fremont, and that this can now be physically observed happening in an actual factory. He frames this as a meaningful shift from speculation to observable construction progress. A separate 10 million units per year production line is referenced as being built at Giga Texas.

2. **Optimus Timeline Slippage:** The host notes that Elon's earlier projection of 5,000–10,000 Optimus units by end of 2025 was not achieved, with production start now pushed to 2026. This is grouped with other missed timelines but not treated as a sign the project is at risk — the host explicitly states Optimus has not been shelved and that progress is visible.

## Energy
1. **Energy Segment Financial Performance:** The energy business is cited as generating $12.7 billion in revenue with approximately 30% margins, presented as one of the strongest performing segments and a key part of the bull case. The host and guest note that energy is still in relatively early stages, currently focused on conventional battery storage and solar systems, but frame it as having enormous future potential — particularly in the context of a potential Tesla-SpaceX merger and AI-driven energy demand.

2. **Energy as a Long-Term Strategic Asset:** Larry Goldberg argues that energy will become "the currency of the future" and that the opportunities in energy are gigantic, especially when considered alongside a potential SpaceX partnership or merger. He suggests the market has not yet begun to appreciate the full scale of Tesla's energy opportunity, framing current deployments as foundational infrastructure for something much larger.

## Financials
1. **Tesla Financial Metrics Q1 2021 vs. Q4 2025 Comparison:** The host presents a comparison showing Tesla's growth over roughly five years: GAAP income grew from $438 million to approximately $840 million (roughly doubled), auto revenue grew from negative $9 billion to $18 billion, and energy revenue reached close to $4 billion. These figures are used to argue that Tesla the business has substantially improved even as the stock price has remained relatively flat over the same period, making the current valuation appear more reasonable than the 2021 peak.

2. **Capital Expenditure Trajectory:** Larry Goldberg references a prior projection he made about Tesla's capital consumption rate, noting he was approximately one to one and a half years ahead of where the company actually landed, and projecting Tesla will reach approximately $40 billion per year in capital spend. He notes that xAI and SpaceX have absorbed some capital deployment that might otherwise have gone through Tesla, and argues a potential merger of Tesla and SpaceX would create a combined entity with over $110 billion in capital resources.

3. **Valuation Framework — PE Ratio Criticism:** The video references analyst Alex Voit's argument that using the P/E ratio to evaluate Tesla is a mistake appropriate only for pure-play automakers, not for growth companies with multiple emerging business lines. Larry Goldberg echoes this by drawing a parallel to Nvidia in 2022, arguing that anyone using conventional valuation metrics to dismiss Tesla's stock price-to-sales ratio is making the same error made by those who dismissed Nvidia before its breakout.

## Market & Competition
1. **SpaceX Merger Speculation as Long-Term Catalyst:** Larry Goldberg repeatedly references the prospect of a Tesla-SpaceX merger as a potential transformational event, arguing it would combine Tesla's ~$40 billion capital spend with SpaceX's ~$75 billion to create a company with over $110 billion in combined capital, deployed with what he describes as Elon's uniquely capital-efficient execution style. He compares xAI's rapid rise to frontier AI status at a fraction of OpenAI's or Anthropic's spend as evidence of this capital efficiency.

2. **Bear vs. Bull Framing as a Time Horizon Question:** Both the host and Larry Goldberg converge on the view that Tesla bears and bulls can simultaneously be correct — bears who expect further near-term stock weakness are not wrong given the lack of near-term earnings catalysts, while long-term bulls remain correct if any one of the multiple major business opportunities (robotaxi, Optimus, AI, energy) materializes. The host explicitly frames his investment thesis as a bet on product and business progress rather than near-term price prediction.
