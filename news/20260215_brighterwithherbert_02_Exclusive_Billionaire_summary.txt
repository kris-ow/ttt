Nancy Tangler, CEO and CIO of Laffer Tangler Investments — a firm managing billions — makes the case that Tesla represents a once-in-a-generation investment opportunity, comparable to Amazon in its early years but with even more transformative potential. Speaking with host Herbert on the brighterwithherbert channel, Tangler outlines why she has Tesla as the centerpiece of her "Six for 2026" best-ideas portfolio, dismisses valuation concerns as short-sighted, and discusses the broader Elon Musk ecosystem including the potential SpaceX-Tesla merger, xAI, Optimus, and the race for AGI. The interview was recorded in early March 2026.

1. Tesla is a "once-in-a-generational" holding — valuation objections miss the point. Tangler argues that focusing on current valuation metrics fails to capture the enormous future value embedded in Tesla's pipeline — FSD subscriptions, robo-taxi, Optimus, and energy storage. She draws a direct comparison to Amazon's IPO in May 1997, noting the stock is up 250,000% since then, and asks rhetorically: when was it ever "too late" to get in? Her view is that Tesla has more transformative levers to pull than Amazon did at a comparable stage.

2. The "Six for 2026" portfolio: Tesla is the anchor. Each year Laffer Tangler publishes a concentrated best-ideas list. Last year's "Five for 25" outperformed the S&P handily, returning 28% even while holding one name that was down 27%. For 2026, the six names include Tesla, AMD, CrowdStrike, Walmart, and a power/services company. Tesla is the largest single holding across Laffer Tangler's large-cap equity portfolios, with Alphabet likely second.

3. Tangler's firm accumulated Tesla at $240/share during the DeepSeek-to-Liberation Day selloff period, when sentiment was deeply negative. She frames this as the correct method for owning the stock — buying into fear-driven selloffs caused by algorithm-driven headline reading and hedge fund pile-ons. Her investment horizon is 3 to 5 years minimum, and potentially 20 years, which she contrasts explicitly with Wall Street's focus on the "next 20 minutes to two days."

4. Wall Street's failure to model Tesla's future businesses is a structural blind spot. Analysts cannot plug Optimus revenues, FSD subscription income, or robo-taxi economics into a standard earnings model — so their valuations are inherently incomplete. This same problem applies to capex spending at Amazon and Microsoft, where Wall Street punishes management for investing in future capacity. Tangler notes that in October of the most recent quarter, a single month of AWS revenue exceeded the entire prior quarter, yet the stock sold off because of capex concerns.

5. The potential SpaceX-Tesla merger: Tangler sees the XAI-SpaceX deal as the precursor to pulling all three companies — Tesla, SpaceX, and xAI — together. She acknowledges the legitimate counter-argument that two focused companies are worth more than a conglomerate, and says she generally agrees with that principle. However, she believes vertical integration will be critical for competing in an AI-dominated future, and that SpaceX's valuation (she cites the SpaceX IPO as likely worth $1.3 trillion) may ultimately match or exceed what Tesla could capture on its own. She predicts SpaceX will go public first, then Musk will consider a merger.

6. Dual-class share structure at SpaceX is a key strategic motive. Tangler agrees with the host's theory that Musk's desire for voting control — which he lacks at Tesla after the Delaware pay-package ruling — could be the primary driver for acquiring Tesla via a post-IPO SpaceX with dual-class shares. She says she learned from the Tesla pay-package saga ("he still hasn't collected that money") and considers dual-class structures defensible when the CEO has repeatedly proven their vision.

7. Tesla's EV business is the iPhone, not the product — the ecosystem is what matters. Tangler makes the analogy directly: the Tesla car is to Tesla what the iPhone is to Apple. What matters is the ecosystem — data generated by driving that trains FSD, the robo-taxi network, CyberCab, and energy products like the Megapack. She loves the Megapack business specifically and thinks Optimus robot adoption will eventually exceed all current expectations.

8. xAI is underappreciated, and Tangler is skeptical of OpenAI's Sam Altman. The firm uses Grok almost exclusively for AI tools. Tangler says she "wouldn't want to invest in a company he's running" based on how OpenAI has commercialized its technology. She predicts Altman may not be running OpenAI within a few years. She views the xAI-SpaceX combination as giving Elon access to top scientists who can accelerate development of space-based data infrastructure.

9. AI is not a bubble — it's early innings, backed by balance sheet strength, not debt. Tangler distinguishes the current AI buildout from the late-1990s tech bubble by pointing to cash-rich balance sheets: Alphabet holds $136 billion in cash, Microsoft holds $80–89 billion. In 1999, the circular investment cycle was debt-fueled; today's hyperscalers generate free cash flow and have diversified revenue bases. She also notes that current capex as a percentage of historical norms is only slightly above the mean, versus two standard deviations above the mean in the 1990s. Hyperscalers collectively plan to spend $700 billion on AI infrastructure this year — "one man's capex is another man's revenue."

10. Musk is irreplaceable, and that is a 5–10 year risk, not an immediate one. Tangler explicitly flags succession as a long-term concern. She compares it to the Jeff Bezos-to-Andy Jassy transition at Amazon, which she says has been relatively smooth. With Musk, she sees no obvious successor — "he is the vision, the drive, and the determination" — and says the company is materially less interesting to her without him. She is willing to live with that risk for now, given his track record.

11. Catalysts Tangler is watching in 2026: removal of safety drivers in FSD entirely; expansion of FSD approvals; solar and battery production ramp (highlighted on the last earnings call); Optimus hitting dexterity milestones; the $2 billion Tesla investment in xAI (quiet since the SpaceX merger was announced); and eventual SpaceX IPO. She expects a "robust year" for Tesla stock, estimating 15–25% upside, while noting the stock could still be volatile in the short term.

12. Long-term conviction: "You cannot own enough Tesla." Tangler tells clients, her children, and friends that Tesla is her single highest-conviction idea across all companies she covers, including the entire Mag 7. She agrees with the idea, cited from a Peter Diamandis-Cathie Wood conversation, that Tesla could one day be a $100 trillion company.
