This video is a casual conversational segment between host Brian (Futuraza) and guest Randy Kirk, covering several loosely connected EV industry topics including vehicle depreciation, Chinese EV company profitability, direct sales legislation, and a minor Tesla stock purchase. The core throughline is the financial health — or lack thereof — of various players in the EV market, contrasting Tesla's position as the only profitable pure-play EV manufacturer in the West against a broader industry landscape of steep depreciation and thin margins.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **EV Depreciation Rankings Since 2020:** The hosts discuss a report ranking five electric car models by depreciation since 2020. The Audi e-tron Sportback topped the list with a roughly 75% drop, having originally cost $78,000–$84,000 and now available for $18,000–$22,000. The Chevy Bolt EV was noted as misleadingly ranked due to its high sticker price versus what buyers actually paid after credits, and the fact that most units were sold on aggressive lease deals. The Hyundai Kona, originally $38,000–$42,000, now fetches around $15,000. The Porsche Taycan, originally $152,000–$186,000 depending on trim, now has a base 4S available for around $47,000. The Tesla Model 3, with a 2020 starting base of approximately $36,000–$56,000, now trades in the $18,000–$24,000 range depending on trim, representing a roughly 54% drop over five years.

2. **Model 3 Depreciation vs. Industry Average:** The hosts note that a general benchmark suggests new vehicles retain about 45% of their original value after five years — meaning a 55% depreciation. The Model 3's approximately 54% drop means it has actually performed marginally better than the average vehicle, by about 1 percentage point. The hosts also flag that depreciation studies using 2021 COVID-era prices as a baseline are misleading, since vehicles were selling above MSRP during the supply crunch and those inflated prices were not what buyers were actually paying.

3. **BYD Mini EV Pricing Context:** The hosts briefly reference a small Chinese EV priced at $6,200, described as being capped at 100 kilometers per hour (roughly 60 mph) and featuring a 10-inch touchscreen. Brian notes he has seen these vehicles on Chinese highways being passed by other cars and advises against importing them via AliExpress, noting that tariffs and handling fees would substantially increase the cost. The segment is used to illustrate why tariffs on Chinese EVs exist.

4. **Direct EV Sales Legislation in Washington State:** Rivian and Lucid have gained the right to sell directly to consumers in Washington State, a privilege previously exclusive to Tesla, which had been grandfathered in before relevant laws were enacted. Previously, Rivian and Lucid could only operate showrooms for informational purposes and could not conduct test drives. Rivian reportedly spent nearly $5 million on a ballot push, with up to $25 million planned, which forced a legislative compromise. The hosts note that nearly 70% of consumers supported allowing direct sales, and Randy mentions he personally wrote to his representative advocating for opening direct sales to all manufacturers rather than banning them entirely.

## Market & Competition

1. **Chinese EV Profitability — Slim but Emerging:** The hosts review a report noting that Chinese EV companies are beginning to turn profits, though the margins are modest. Leap Motor disclosed its first-ever full-year profit of $78 million. NIO posted $104 million in profit in Q4, its first-ever profitable quarter, compared to a $900 million loss in the same period the prior year — though the hosts note this still leaves the company deeply underwater cumulatively. Xpeng turned a net profit of $55 million in Q4. BYD, Xiaomi, and Li Auto are noted as already being in this profitable cohort. The hosts contextualize BYD's situation: Q4 profits were down 19% year-over-year but the company remained profitable with billions in earnings even while growing and cutting costs.

2. **Tesla as the Only Profitable Pure-Play Western EV Maker:** The video references an article describing Tesla as the only profitable pure-play EV manufacturer in the West, while also noting that Tesla's profits have been declining as the company pivots toward AI and robotics. The hosts acknowledge this framing with some wry humor but do not dispute it substantively.

## Financials

1. **Minor Tesla Stock Purchase Generates Headlines:** Cypress Wealth Services LLC purchased fewer than 3,000 Tesla shares, increasing its Tesla stake by 22%, bringing its Tesla holding to approximately $6.9 million — roughly 1% of its portfolio and its 25th largest holding. The hosts mock the fact that this small transaction generated a news headline, suggesting the motivation is likely to raise the fund's public profile rather than signal anything meaningful about Tesla.
