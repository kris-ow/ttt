This episode of Futurazza features host Brian and guest Herbert from Brighter discussing a wave of positive Tesla news mid-April 2026, including a UBS stock upgrade, FSD version 14.3 developments, Cybercab production and deployment signals, and the ongoing Optimus program. The core thesis is that institutional analysts are finally acknowledging Tesla's autonomous driving lead as undeniable, and that the convergence of FSD approvals, robotaxi scaling, and Optimus manufacturing ramp represents a fundamental transformation in how Tesla should be valued. Brian also notes he is touring the Megapack factory in Loup and an EC Cascadia manufacturing line in the coming weeks.

## Autonomous Driving
1. **UBS Upgrade Driven by FSD Progress and Robotaxi Expectations:** UBS upgraded Tesla's stock, citing eventual progress on robotaxi and Optimus. Herbert notes this is part of a broader wave of institutional analyst upgrades, all pointing to the same conclusion: FSD has reached a point where it cannot be dismissed. Regulatory approval of FSD in the Netherlands (EU approval) is cited as a major unlock, along with at least one analyst physically testing FSD in Austin. The consensus shift is that FSD is now acknowledged to be safer than the average human driver.

2. **FSD Version 14.3 — 20% Faster Reaction Time:** FSD version 14.3 is described as being approximately 20% faster in reaction time, attributed to a new compiler that more directly translates input to understanding. Beta testers, including a tester referred to as "AI Driver," physically timed the system's response to traffic lights turning yellow and observed a reaction time of approximately 0.2 seconds. Herbert notes that FSD supervised is described as seven times safer than the average human driver.

3. **FSD Take-Rate Economics and Margin Contribution:** Herbert walks through the math on FSD subscription revenue at low take rates: even 30,000 vehicles out of hundreds of thousands sold per quarter subscribing at $100/month would generate $3 million per month in near-pure margin. He contrasts this with traditional automakers like Kia and Volkswagen running approximately 5% margins on a $50,000 car, yielding roughly $2,500 per vehicle. The broader argument is that analysts are still underestimating FSD because they frame it as a margin add-on rather than a fundamental transformation from a "dumb car" to a "smart car" capable of generating recurring robotaxi revenue of approximately $30,000 per year per vehicle.

4. **Spring Software Update — FSD Education App:** Tesla's spring update includes a dedicated FSD education app available on the in-car dashboard, featuring instructional videos and a purchase button. In Europe, a three-minute onboarding video is included at point of sale. Herbert argues that consumer fear and lack of awareness remain the primary barriers to FSD adoption, and suggests Tesla should consider per-use pricing or free trial periods rather than requiring a full monthly subscription commitment upfront, citing suggestions from creator Omar as well-considered approaches.

## Robotaxi
1. **Cybercab Production Line Active, Vehicles Leaving Factory:** Joe Tegtmeyer observed approximately 60 Cybercabs leaving the factory and heading to the outbound lot in mid-April 2026. Herbert interprets this as part of a coordinated multi-city testing and deployment preparation effort.

2. **34-City Data Collection Job Postings Signal Broad Cybercab Rollout:** Tesla has posted job listings for data collection supervisors across 34 cities. Herbert interprets these roles as supervisors overseeing employees who will drive Cybercabs in those cities — operating morning, afternoon, and overnight graveyard shifts — collecting real-world data with a human at the wheel while driving as autonomously as possible. Herbert argues that posting for 34 cities when only 7 were announced for launch strongly implies Tesla intends a much broader deployment, and he predicts all 34 cities could have active Cybercab operations within approximately one year.

3. **Seven-City Launch Timeline Uncertainty:** Herbert originally expected Tesla to launch robotaxi service in seven cities by the end of June 2026 but now believes that timeline may slip. He remains confident in the broader trajectory but tempers near-term expectations on the specific June target. Herbert's Bellevue, Washington neighborhood is specifically cited as one of the 34 cities he expects to see Cybercab testing soon.

## Humanoid Bots
1. **Optimus Gen 3 Delayed but Manufacturing Ramp Underway:** Herbert acknowledges that Optimus version 3 has not been revealed, and that Elon Musk's prior target of 5,000–10,000 humanoid robots by end of 2025 was not met. However, Herbert points to supplier intelligence, job postings, and observable factory development as evidence the program is healthy. Specifically, he references a 1-million-per-year production line under development in Fremont and a 10-million-per-year line under development at Giga Texas.

2. **Bull Case for Optimus Delay — The Cybertruck Parallel:** Brian draws a parallel between Optimus delays and the Cybertruck delay, arguing that scope creep driven by continuous improvement — not inability — explains the timeline slippage. The argument is that Tesla could have shipped 10,000 early Optimus units on an inefficient line producing a product they knew would be obsolete within six months, but chose instead to wait for a more capable generation and a well-optimized manufacturing process. When the factory does turn on, it is expected to be a highly efficient operation from day one.

## Electric Vehicles

The contrast is used to illustrate the structural difference between Tesla's software-defined vehicle model and legacy automakers.
