Stellantis has effectively written off its entire North American battery manufacturing investment, selling its 49% stake in a Canadian battery plant for just $100 — a near-total loss on an approximately $980 million investment. Hosts Brian (Futurazza) and guest Randy Kirk (Randy Kirk Variety Hour) discuss what this signals about legacy automakers' EV struggles, Canada's pivot toward Chinese EV manufacturing partnerships, and BYD's legal challenge to US tariffs. The conversation frames these developments as interconnected signs of a broader reshaping of the North American EV landscape.

1. Stellantis sold its 49% stake in Canada's first and only multi-billion dollar battery cell manufacturing plant to its joint venture partner LG for $100. The $100 price is not symbolic incompetence but a legal requirement — contracts require "consideration" from both parties to be valid, so a nominal sum was necessary. The plant represented roughly $980 million in investment, making this essentially a complete write-down. Randy notes that Stellantis also wrote down approximately $21.5 billion in broader EV-related losses.

2. LG, as the acquiring party, stands to benefit significantly by repurposing the plant to serve a wider customer base. LG already has manufacturing relationships with General Motors, Honda, and Hyundai, and can now offer battery cell production to those partners and others without being tied exclusively to Stellantis. This makes the deal logical from LG's perspective even at essentially zero cost.

3. The core diagnosis of Stellantis's EV failure — shared with Ford and GM — is that they were building EVs the government mandated rather than vehicles customers actually wanted to buy, at prices customers were unwilling to pay. The Dodge Charger EV is cited as a particularly glaring example of a product that missed the mark, including the detail that the vehicle was given artificial engine sounds to mimic its gas-powered predecessor. Ford wrote down roughly $6–7 billion in EV losses; GM's write-downs were in the $12–15 billion range.

4. Canada is exploring joint ventures with Chinese EV manufacturers BYD and Chery to build electric vehicles domestically for global export. Canadian Prime Minister held reportedly productive conversations with both companies, with the Honda Civic's Ontario production cited as a precedent for foreign manufacturers building affordably priced vehicles in Canada. Vehicles manufactured in Canada by BYD or Chery would qualify as domestic products, potentially exempting them from price caps and making them eligible for buyer incentive credits.

5. Canada has opened its market to 49,000 Chinese-manufactured vehicles, with the condition that half of all imports must be priced under 35,000 Canadian dollars (approximately $25,500 USD). Brian calls this an "aggressively low" price point that could theoretically be met by Tesla's standard Model 3 (built in Shanghai) but would be extremely challenging for most manufacturers. The second half of the 49,000-unit quota carries no price cap, potentially opening the door to higher-end models like the Model Y Long Range.

6. BYD has filed a lawsuit against the US government over tariffs, specifically targeting tariffs on components imported to its California factory, which performs final assembly of electric buses. The multiple BYD entities involved include BYD LLC, BYD Coach and Bus LLC, BYD Energy LLC, and BYD Motors. The lawsuit is currently on hold while courts wait for guidance from the Supreme Court's ruling on the VOS case, which is expected to be decided in May or June 2026 — the Court's typical window for major decisions.

7. Randy speculates that robotaxis could fundamentally disrupt the entire new-car market within approximately two years (by around 2028), potentially collapsing demand for personally owned vehicles. His argument is that cars built in 2028 will last until 2048, creating a glut of used vehicles no one will want because ride-hailing via autonomous vehicles will be cheaper and more convenient. He suggests this shift would also reframe the question of whether Chinese autonomous vehicles should be allowed into the US market.

8. The hosts argue that US automakers have effectively abandoned the sub-$30,000 vehicle segment, with advertised entry-level models existing largely on paper but rarely stocked by dealers. This creates a genuine market gap that Chinese manufacturers could fill. They also discuss how state-level anti-EV legislation — particularly in Washington — is driven less by opposition to electric vehicles per se and more by dealer network protection, with many state legislators being dealership owners themselves, a dynamic Brian calls "regulatory capture."
