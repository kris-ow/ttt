This panel discussion from "brighterwithherbert" features four Tesla bull analysts — Alexandra Merz ("Tesla Boomer Mama"), Jeff Lutz ("The Rational Bull"), and Matt Smith ("Nerd Alert") — debating Tesla's rapid multi-front scaling across new vehicles, robotaxi deployment, Terafab chip manufacturing, and upcoming April catalysts. The core thesis is that Tesla is simultaneously advancing on so many fronts — Cybercab production, FSD 14.3, Optimus V3, Terafab, and potential new vehicle announcements — that most investors are underestimating the speed and breadth of what is coming. The hosts also address shareholder activism around Chancellor McCormick's judicial bias and the State Street ETF proxy voting controversy.

## Electric Vehicles

1. **Elon Hints at a Vehicle "Way Cooler Than a Minivan":** Elon Musk responded to a user requesting a minivan by saying "something way cooler than a minivan is coming," and separately replied "Noted" to a post by Ryan Peterson suggesting a three-row vehicle where each row has its own pair of doors. The panel sees this as a strong signal that Tesla is working on a vehicle above the Model 3 and Model Y in its lineup, filling the gap left by the retirement of the Model S and Model X. Jeff Lutz argued it has likely been the plan for some time, noting that a portfolio consisting only of the Model 3, Model Y, Roadster, and Cybertruck lacks a natural upgrade path for customers as their finances grow.

2. **Three Candidate Vehicles Debated:** The panel debated three potential forms this new vehicle could take: (1) a Model Y XL — an elongated version of the existing Model Y with a third row, similar to the Model Y L already sold in China; (2) a Cyber SUV — a shorter-bed Cybertruck variant with a third row and possibly falcon-wing doors, drawing on Cybertruck's existing 48-volt platform and installed tooling capacity of 125K units; or (3) a Cybercab XL — a five-to-seven-seat elongated version of the Cybercab platform, which Jeff estimated could carry a bill-of-materials cost of roughly $23,000–$25,000, representing a meaningful 30% cost advantage over a Model Y XL. Matt Smith noted that for near-term realism, only the Model Y L or YXL seems achievable within roughly 12 months, while a Cyber SUV or larger transit-style vehicle is longer term.

3. **Tesla Prototype Vehicle Technician Job Openings:** The panel noted Tesla has posted job openings for prototype vehicle technicians, which they speculated could relate to the Robo Van or other future autonomous vehicle platforms. The Robo Van itself was described as still three-plus years away from launch.

4. **Tesla Roadster Reveal Pushed to Late April:** The Roadster, originally expected to be revealed on April 1st (as announced at the shareholder meeting), has been pushed to late April. The panel speculated it may now be combined with an Optimus V3 reveal event, and noted that the Roadster represents a notable collaboration between Tesla and SpaceX engineering teams.

## Robotaxi

1. **Cybercab Spotted in San Francisco, Production on Track for April:** For the first time, a Cybercab was spotted driving in San Francisco — a vehicle with no steering wheel and no side mirrors, accompanied by a validation rig. The panel interpreted the presence of a validation rig as a sign that broader rollout is imminent. Cybercab production at Giga Texas appears on track to begin in April, with Joe Tegtmeyer's drone footage showing tens of Cybercabs visible on the Giga Texas lot.

2. **AI Safety Operator Hiring Expanded to 18 Cities and 12 States:** Tesla has been rapidly expanding its hiring of AI safety operators. Initially posting for 14 cities across 9 states, Tesla added four more cities and three new states within two days, bringing the total to 18 cities across 12 states. Cities mentioned include Bellevue (Washington), Dallas, Phoenix, Orlando, Las Vegas, and Houston — many of which are outside the original seven pilot cities. The panel viewed this aggressive geographic seeding as a strong signal of imminent mass deployment rather than a fundamental technical problem.

3. **Model Y Validation Vehicles Spotted Across Multiple Cities:** Lots of Model Y vehicles equipped with rear camera washers and Texas license plates have been spotted in Dallas, Phoenix, Orlando, and Las Vegas, and validation vehicles have been seen in Houston. The panel interpreted this deliberate staging of vehicles across cities — rather than selling them to customers — as evidence that Tesla is operationally preparing for a broad simultaneous rollout and is confident in its upcoming FSD software release.

4. **Robotaxi Safety Incident Data and Ramp Trajectory:** Tesla reported five incidents to NHTSA from December 15 to January 15 — all minor fender benders, with only one at 17 mph and the others at four mph or less, including one at zero mph. From January 15 to February 15, there was one additional incident at a couple of miles per hour. The total incident count since June 22 (the robotaxi launch) stood at roughly 17. Jeff Lutz predicted that there will be a month in 2026 when Tesla produces in a single month the equivalent of Waymo's entire fleet in Cybercabs, and within six months of that point, one week's Cybercab production will equal the entire fleet of the number-two competitor.

5. **FSD Version 14.3 Expected in April — The "Reasoning" Release:** FSD 14.3, described as incorporating reasoning/more sentient capabilities, is expected to drop within April. Elon had said the release was "weeks away" back in October. The panel believes this version is the critical software enabler for scaling robotaxi deployment more aggressively, and that Tesla has likely been running a more advanced internal version on its robotaxi fleet already. The panel noted that reducing the minor incident rate with this new release would give Tesla the green light to expand far more rapidly.

6. **Contextualizing Tesla's Pace vs. Waymo:** Jeff Lutz noted that Waymo launched its service in late December 2018 and had roughly 600 cars approximately nine months later, with full removal of safety riders occurring about two years after launch in 2020. He argued that directly comparing Tesla's current early-stage deployment to Waymo's multi-year operational history is a flawed "T equals zero" analysis, and that Tesla's continued spending, city seeding, and hiring indicate no fundamental technical blocking issue.

## Humanoid Bots

1. **Optimus V3 Reveal Expected in Q1 or April:** The next-generation Optimus V3 was originally promised for Q1 2026. The panel flagged it as the single most anticipated event of the coming weeks. Elon has described Optimus V3's design change as comparable in significance to the Raptor V3 engine redesign — a major generational leap. Matt Smith and Alexandra Merz both named Optimus V3 as their top catalyst to watch, more so than FSD in Europe or the Roadster.

2. **Competitive Sensitivity Around Optimus Reveal Format:** Alexandra Merz cautioned that Tesla is unlikely to hold a large-scale "Bot Day"-style event for V3, as the global humanoid robot competition — including Chinese competitors — remains intense, and Tesla has stated multiple times it does not want to reveal too much to competitors. She expects the reveal will more likely be a controlled video release, potentially paired with the Roadster event.

3. **Optimus Production Start Expected This Summer:** Jeff Lutz emphasized that Optimus must begin production as promised this summer to generate meaningful market excitement. He noted that if Optimus V3 demonstrates meaningfully superior capabilities compared to other bots and production starts on schedule, it could be a significant market catalyst. The chips being produced via Terafab are intended to power both robotaxi AI inference and Optimus at scale.

## Energy

1. **Terafab Announced as Joint Tesla-SpaceX Venture:** Tesla announced Terafab — a domestic semiconductor chip fabrication initiative — as a joint venture with SpaceX. Elon framed it as addressing a critical capacity gap in AI compute supply, consistent with how he previously addressed battery supply chain constraints by vertically integrating. The presentation at the Terafab announcement showed accomplishments across Tesla, xAI, and SpaceX side by side to demonstrate the credibility of the team to execute on what sounds like an impossible undertaking.

2. **100 Gigawatt Annual Solar Target Announced:** Elon stated that Tesla is targeting 100 gigawatts per year of solar as part of its Terafab and energy ambitions. Matt Smith noted this largely slipped under the radar but represents very ambitious ramp plans for Tesla as a solar company. Separately, 14 gigawatts of energy deployment was referenced as an expected figure for the current quarter.

3. **Terafab Capital Requirements and Funding Structure:** The CFO confirmed on the Q1 earnings call that Tesla's $20 billion capex commitment for 2026 does not include Terafab or large-scale solar. Alexandra Merz noted that Tesla currently holds approximately $40 billion in cash, sufficient to fund robotaxi rollout and early Optimus production without a capital raise. For Terafab, SpaceX's upcoming IPO capital raise — expected to be $50–75 billion in June — is anticipated to fund an outsized share of the early Terafab build. For larger-scale Terafab ambitions, the CFO indicated Tesla is in discussions with banks about potential debt issuance or equity raises. Several analysts have estimated cumulative capex to reach terawatt scale could be in the range of roughly one trillion dollars, though Bernstein reportedly put out a figure as high as five trillion.

4. **EUV Machine Bottleneck Identified as Key Long-Lead Constraint:** Matt Smith flagged that EUV lithography machines (manufactured by ASML) represent a critical long-lead-time bottleneck for Terafab. He argued that total stated demand from major AI players — including those led by Sam Altman, Dario Amodei, and Elon Musk — already exceeds projected EUV machine capacity by 2030, making early supply agreements a significant competitive advantage. Jeff Lutz added that Elon's track record of ordering capacity, taking delivery, and immediately deploying it makes him a preferred customer for ASML and downstream chip fabs.

## Market & Competition

1. **State Street ETF Proxy Voting Controversy:** Alexandra Merz published an article and shareholder action campaign targeting State Street, the third-largest institutional Tesla shareholder, which holds more than 3% of Tesla shares. In the 2024 shareholder vote to reinstate Elon's 2018 compensation package, State Street — which sells SPY, SPLG, and DIA as "passive mirror voting" funds — voted 100% against the reinstatement instead of mirroring the 72%–28% outcome of the general shareholder vote, citing complexity as an excuse. Merz argued this constitutes a fraudulent misrepresentation of their passive mandate and a politically motivated power play. She is urging Tesla retail shareholders who hold State Street ETFs to exit those positions if no taxable event is triggered, or alternatively to demand proxy vote choice and vote with management. She plans to repeat an analysis of State Street's 2025 voting record by end of August 2026.

2. **Other OEMs Pulling Back Creates EV Market Opportunity:** Jeff Lutz noted that other automakers have pulled back from EV commitments, creating a supply gap that could actually benefit Tesla — both in terms of market share and as a justification for adding new vehicle variants. The panel expressed collective disappointment at the pace of broader EV industry adoption.

3. **SpaceX IPO — Retail Allocation and Valuation Expectations:** The panel discussed that SpaceX's IPO — anticipated around June 9, 2026 — is expected to raise at least $50 billion and possibly up to $75 billion, at a pre-IPO valuation of approximately $1.75 trillion. Alexandra Merz noted this would represent the smallest float as a percentage of market cap for a mega-cap IPO in history — roughly 3–5% of total market cap. Elon's team is reportedly pushing for 30% of IPO shares to be allocated to retail investors (versus the typical 5–10%), which would be channeled through brokers like Robinhood. Both Alexandra Merz and Matt Smith estimated SpaceX could close its IPO day with a market cap near or above $2 trillion, with Polymarket data showing 45% of bettors placing the closing market cap at $2 trillion or higher.

4. **Chancellor McCormick Judicial Bias Campaign:** Alexandra Merz detailed how Delaware Chancellor Kathleen McCormick — who ruled against Elon's $56 billion pay package and later presided over cases including attorney fee determinations in the Tesla directors settlement — was caught liking a LinkedIn post from a plaintiff's jury consultant celebrating a potential $2 billion verdict against Elon in a Twitter stock manipulation case. Merz noted that LinkedIn's reaction feature requires an intentional selection among multiple options, making an accidental like implausible. McCormick has stated she is "thinking about" recusing herself from remaining Delaware cases. Merz is organizing a notarized shareholder complaint campaign to Delaware's Court on the Judiciary to force the recusal, and provided a remote notary service option for approximately $35. She also noted that the Delaware Supreme Court previously invalidated large portions of McCormick's original pay package ruling and reduced her $176 million attorney fee award down to $71 million in the directors settlement case.
