This is a Monday news roundup from Brian at the Futuraza channel, covering a range of EV and Tesla-related headlines from the previous week. Brian's tone is conversational and editorially opinionated, frequently pushing back on what he considers poorly researched or biased journalism. The video covers Tesla Model Y L global expansion, used EV pricing trends, MKBHD's relationship with Tesla, Uber's robotaxi ambitions, Ford's factory closure, and California's EV semi voucher controversy, ending with a live Q&A session.

Key Points:

1. Tesla Model Y L (longer wheelbase, six-seat variant) is expanding globally after Australia certification. Tesla launched the YL in China in August 2025, where it was well-received. It has now been officially listed in Australia's automotive regulator database under the designation Y L5NDB, serviced by the Shanghai factory. Brian personally reviewed the car in China the prior month and noted the 2+2 configuration is more comfortable than the standard 7-seat Model Y layout. A European launch also seems likely given that the YL was spotted testing at the Nurburgring and seen at Giga Berlin. Brian believes a US launch from the Texas factory is coming by late 2026, and hints at having some basis for that confidence beyond public information.

2. Tesla used car prices are rising while other EVs depreciate sharply. Brian debunks the persistent narrative that Tesla has terrible resale value, tracing it to the pandemic-era pricing distortion when dealers were marking up vehicles like a $35,000 Toyota to $40,000-$45,000 over MSRP. When the $7,500 federal EV tax credit disappeared, it lifted the floor on used Tesla prices because new Teslas became comparatively more expensive, while other EVs that previously benefited from the credit saw steeper used-market drops. As a concrete example from the used market aggregator Plat Auto, a 2025 Equinox EV with only 1,600 miles was listed at $26,000, representing brutal depreciation. By contrast, a refreshed 2024 Model Y with 15,000 miles was listed at $35,000, which Brian called a strong deal.

3. The MKBHD-Tesla "beef" is overblown media hype. Marques Brownlee (MKBHD, 20.8 million YouTube subscribers) mentioned in his new Model Y Performance review that Tesla no longer proactively sends him vehicles, and he had to source the car himself from a local dealership. Brian explains this is simply because Tesla has no standing PR department — when they have a product to push, they push it; otherwise, there's no one making those calls. He notes Tesla had previously given Brownlee early Cybertruck access and that Brownlee called the Model S Plaid the best car of the decade, so there's no acrimony. Brownlee also sold his Cybertruck (he had an R1T already) and cancelled one of his two Roadster reservations, keeping one.

4. Uber's robotaxi strategy is more about platform than technology. The Uber CEO claimed Uber will handle more EV trips before the end of the decade than Tesla, and the company launched a new suite of services to let autonomous vehicle developers plug into its ride-hailing network. Brian is skeptical, noting Uber previously had its own AV project that it sold off. The actual pitch is essentially: "If you build a self-driving car, bring it to us." The realistic current partner is Waymo. Brian also corrects a common misconception, noting that Nvidia's Alamo (Drive) is a tool for building AV systems, not an autonomous driving system itself, contrary to speculation that legacy automakers like Ford could simply plug it in for FSD-equivalent capability.

5. Ford shut down its $5.8 billion Kentucky battery factory just months after opening, with 1,600 workers losing jobs. Brian frames this as partly a self-inflicted wound, noting Ford spent two decades telling customers EVs wouldn't work, then was surprised customers didn't buy their EVs. Ford's secretive next-generation EV pickup is rumored to feature 48V architecture, a structural battery pack, front and rear castings, and a small footprint similar to a Cybertruck — but has no release date and almost no public information. Brian is skeptical of Ford's broader EV commitment.

6. California's CARB voucher program for electric Class 8 semis is drawing competitor complaints. California earmarked $165 million in vouchers supporting Tesla Semi purchases, with the standard voucher worth $120,000 off the sticker price — enough to bring the Tesla Semi near price parity with diesel trucks on day one. Critics from competing EV manufacturers claim Tesla received preferential treatment and hasn't met certification standards. Brian dismisses this, noting the certification is about clean air compliance, which an all-electric vehicle automatically satisfies. He points out hydrogen trucks receive double the subsidy ($240,000+, up to $420,000 in pilot programs), which he notes was essentially Nikola's entire business model.

7. JD Power's 2026 EV Experience Ownership Study found 96% of current EV owners would consider another EV for their next vehicle. Brian attributes this largely to the fact that even mediocre EVs offer a fundamentally better drivetrain experience than equivalent gas vehicles, illustrating the point with his family's test drive of a Chevy Blazer EV — not a great car, but better than the gas Blazer it replaces.

8. Robo taxi accident statistics comparing AVs to human drivers are misleading. Brian has addressed this in multiple videos: the higher reported accident rate for robotaxis includes incidents that humans would never report, such as 1 mph parking lot contact with no damage, which AVs are legally required to log. It also includes not-at-fault incidents such as a bus striking a stationary vehicle.
