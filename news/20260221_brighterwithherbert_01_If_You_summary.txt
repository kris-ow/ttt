This video features Herbert (channel host) and Sern Basher, a Chartered Financial Analyst who runs the investment advisory firm Brilliant Advice, discussing the financial economics of buying a Tesla Cybercab as a personal income-generating asset. The conversation is anchored in a detailed financial model Basher built, which went viral on X with nearly a million views. Their central thesis is that at under $30,000, a Cybercab represents an entirely new asset class — a depreciating vehicle transformed into an ROI-driven income machine — and that the economics are compelling even under the most conservative assumptions.

Key Points

1. Elon Musk has publicly confirmed Cybercab will be sold to consumers for under $30,000 before end of 2026. Musk responded directly to a challenge from Dylan Loomis on X, confirming that individual customers would be able to purchase a Cybercab at that price point before 2027. This prompted Basher to model what the economics would actually look like for an individual owner.

2. Tesla has multiple strategic reasons to sell Cybercabs to individuals rather than operating the entire fleet itself. First, Uber and Lyft drivers could buy Cybercabs and quit ride-share, accelerating the transition away from human-driven ride-hail. Second, selling vehicles to consumers reduces Tesla's capital intensity — they build the car, sell it for a profit, and recycle that capital into more production. Third, and perhaps most importantly, creating thousands or millions of Cybercab owners creates a political constituency that will resist regulatory attacks on Tesla's autonomy business, mirroring the strategy Uber used with its drivers.

3. A key Tesla job posting confirms dynamic and surge pricing is planned for the robo-taxi network. Tesla is hiring a robo-taxi pricing software engineer tasked with designing algorithms incorporating base costs, taxes, tolls, market demand, and regional factors. The job description explicitly mentions "dynamic and surge pricing," signaling that a flat dollar-per-mile rate is a long-term aspiration, not a launch-day reality.

4. Current ride-share pricing context makes even a $1/mile Cybercab fare look dramatically cheap. A June 2024 study of Waymo, Uber, and Lyft pricing in San Francisco showed per-mile costs ranging from $4–$42 for short trips under a mile, and $4–$6 per mile even for trips between 2.7 and 5.8 miles. The model conservatively uses $1/mile, which Basher notes is already in use in Austin, Texas, and likely well below where pricing will actually start.

5. The core financial model uses specific assumptions to project owner income. Key inputs include: 25 mph average travel speed, 5-mile average trip length, 40% empty (repositioning) miles, 3-minute average loading/unloading time, Tesla's take rate fixed at 35%, 600,000-mile battery life (conservative versus a potential 1 million miles), $199/month FSD subscription, utilization rates ranging from 30% to 65%, $1 base fare plus $1/mile plus $0.42/minute idle charge, and home charging at $0.16/kWh.

6. At 30% vehicle utilization — the worst-case scenario — an individual owner generates roughly $32,000 in gross revenue annually, yielding approximately $14,000 in gross profit after all costs. Costs include FSD ($2,400/year), charging ($2,300/year), tires ($2,800/year), vehicle depreciation/financing, EV fees (~$1,300/year), and miscellaneous expenses. Cleaning and parking are zeroed out assuming home-based ownership but noted as variables.

7. Gross profit scales dramatically with utilization. At 35% utilization (8.5 hours/day active), gross profit rises modestly. At 65% utilization (15.6 hours/day), gross profit approaches $40,000–$41,000 per year. Variable costs like tires and vehicle depreciation increase with mileage, but fixed costs like financing remain constant, improving margins at higher utilization.

8. After a 35% tax rate, net profit at 30% utilization is approximately $8,000/year, meaning the car pays for itself in roughly 3.5 years. At 35% utilization the payback period drops to about 3 years. At 65% utilization, net profit approaches $25,000–$26,000 — nearly recovering the entire $30,000 purchase price in a single year.

9. The model also works in dense urban scenarios (15 mph, 3-mile trips, supercharger pricing at $0.35/kWh) and rural scenarios (45 mph, 25-mile trips, 100% empty return miles, $5 base fare). In urban settings, gross profit drops only slightly — from $14,000 to ~$13,000 at 30% utilization. In rural settings, gross profit at 30% utilization is approximately $21,000, rising to $57,000 at 65% utilization, demonstrating the model's robustness across geographies.

10. Tesla's optimal take rate, based on fairness modeling, appears to be 30–35%. At a 15% take rate, the owner earns 269% more than Tesla — clearly unsustainable for Tesla. At 25% (which Musk once cited), owners still earn 1.7x what Tesla earns. At 30–35%, the split approaches rough parity, with owners earning 0–25% more than Tesla depending on utilization. Basher argues this is the likely equilibrium range, with the rate potentially being dynamic rather than fixed.

11. FSD pricing is one of three levers Tesla can adjust to balance economics. Tesla could offer FSD for free and raise the take rate, charge $199/month and use a 35% take rate, or charge $999/month and lower the take rate to 15–20%. At $2/mile pricing, even a $1,000/month FSD fee with a 30–35% take rate remains fair for both parties. The key insight is that Tesla has enormous flexibility to optimize across vehicle price, FSD cost, and take rate simultaneously.

12. Tesla's fleet-scale economics are staggering even at $1/mile. With 10 million Cybercabs at 65% utilization, Tesla could generate approximately $250 billion in net income. At 50 million vehicles (estimated US need), net income approaches $1 trillion. At 100 million globally, net income could reach $1–2 trillion. Applying a conservative P/E of 30, that implies a valuation of $2.5 trillion to $70 trillion for the robo-taxi business alone — not including Optimus or other revenue streams.

13. A poll of 1,147 X users revealed extraordinary consumer demand. Only 7% said they would not buy a Cybercab. 20% would buy one, 37.5% would buy two to five, and 35% would buy more than five. Extrapolating, that sample of 1,000 respondents could have generated 3,000 to 8,500 individual orders if ordering were open today.

Conclusions and Recommendations

Basher recommends thinking of Cybercab ownership as analogous to rental real estate — a capital income asset rather than wage income — representing a historic structural shift. He suggests early buyers will benefit most, as fares will likely be higher than $1/mile at launch and will compress over time as competition and scale increase. He personally plans to convert his existing 74 Cybertruck orders into Cybercab orders. His closing advice: investors can hedge by owning both Tesla stock and a Cybercab, capturing upside regardless of whether Tesla or individual owners capture more of the economics.
